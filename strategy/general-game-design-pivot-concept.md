# General Game Design Pivot — Working Concept

> **Important:** This document is a planning concept, not a fixed roadmap. Do not follow it blindly. Release order, pacing, topic mix, titles, thumbnails, and the speed of the pivot should change when actual channel data, production limits, personal interest, game relevance, or stronger ideas point elsewhere. The purpose is to provide a sensible default path and decision framework, not to replace judgment.

## Strategic recommendation

Pivot over roughly **six months and twelve uploads**, rather than through an announcement or sudden category change.

The broad progression is:

> **OSRS design → MMO design → persistent-world design → general game design**

Do not publish five World of Warcraft videos consecutively or abruptly stop covering OSRS. That would risk teaching existing viewers that the channel has become a WoW channel. The intended lesson is different:

> **The Cube Analyst examines game design using whichever game best demonstrates the argument.**

The repository already contains enough material for most of the transition:

- Project V contains strong OSRS essays, one universal player-psychology essay, and a fully general survival-game essay.
- Project M contains ten RuneScape-to-MMO bridge essays.
- Project W contains five complete Classic WoW essays.
- The main gap is the final step from MMO analysis into a repeatable slate of general game-design essays across genres.

## Why the pivot should be gradual

The strongest current audience signal is still OSRS. The best-performing upload was a provocative, self-contained argument about a mechanic players thought they understood. The trilogy follow-ups declined sharply, which suggests that premise and packaging matter more than continuity or series structure.

Therefore:

- Every upload should stand alone.
- The pivot should never be framed as a numbered series.
- A broader topic still needs a sharp, stranger-legible claim.
- OSRS should remain a recurring pillar during and after the transition.

There is already evidence that viewers may follow the channel into adjacent games. The existing OSRS-versus-Classic-WoW video outperformed several OSRS uploads. That does not prove a broad pivot will work, but it makes Classic WoW a reasonable first expansion rather than a completely cold audience change.

The intended audience evolution is:

1. Viewers click because they recognize **OSRS**.
2. Viewers click because they recognize **MMOs and design problems**.
3. Viewers click because they recognize **the channel's way of making an argument**.

The third stage is the actual destination.

# Phase 1 — Establish the broader promise

## Upload 1 — Half the Game Isn't in the Game

This is a strong starting point because it is unmistakably OSRS while discussing a wider design issue: when external documentation becomes part of a game's functional interface.

It broadens the channel's intellectual promise without asking viewers to care about another game yet.

The audience should leave with the impression that the channel is about more than RuneScape facts. It is about invisible systems surrounding games.

## Upload 2 — 92 Is Half of 99

Follow the Wiki video with a highly legible OSRS premise.

This serves several purposes:

- Reinforces the existing audience base.
- Tests the proven contrarian mechanics format again.
- Provides a clean performance benchmark before broader experiments.
- Prevents the pivot from feeling like a retreat from OSRS.

The premise is mathematically simple, visually clear, and close to the successful “DPS is a lie” shape: a statement that sounds false but is literally true.

## Upload 3 — You Gotta Take a Break, Man

This is the first conceptual bridge.

The thesis concerns player autonomy, recovery, variety, and the way optimization can colonize rest. Those ideas apply to most grind-heavy, live-service, progression, and MMO games even when OSRS supplies the footage and examples.

The video remains safe for the current audience because OSRS is still the primary object. The broader audience can enter through the universal experience of turning recreation into obligation.

At the end of Phase 1, the three-video mix is:

- Two strongly OSRS videos.
- One OSRS-rooted universal essay.

The audience should barely experience this as a pivot.

# Phase 2 — Move from OSRS to MMO design

## Upload 4 — Best-in-Slot Is Bad Game Design

This is probably the strongest bridge script in the repository.

The title is legible to players of RuneScape, World of Warcraft, Diablo, Destiny, Monster Hunter, and most loot-based games. RuneScape can provide the strongest evidence, but the argument concerns equipment ecology across games.

Package it as a general claim rather than an OSRS-only lesson.

Preferred framing:

> **Best-in-Slot Is Bad Game Design**

Avoid reducing it to:

> Why OSRS handles best-in-slot differently

The goal is to make OSRS a compelling case study inside a wider argument.

## Upload 5 — Broken on Purpose

This should be the first fully non-OSRS release.

Its thesis is that incomplete vanilla WoW classes produced social dependence. It preserves the channel's established strengths:

- MMO systems.
- Friction as deliberate design.
- Old constraints producing unexpected value.
- A contrarian defence of inconvenience.
- Social consequences emerging from mechanics.

This is a smaller jump than moving directly to a single-player action game or unrelated genre.

The video should be presented as the same analyst examining a different object, not as a side project or special exception.

## Upload 6 — One Menu Has Carried RuneScape for 25 Years

Return to OSRS immediately after the first WoW experiment.

This prevents one weak non-OSRS result from making the audience feel abandoned. It also reinforces the actual channel identity:

> This channel analyzes invisible design machinery.

The right-click menu is pure RuneScape, but the argument has broader interface-design relevance.

At the end of Phase 2, the six-upload window contains:

- Four OSRS or OSRS-led videos.
- One universal gaming essay.
- One fully non-OSRS video.

That is a cautious expansion ratio.

# Phase 3 — Establish the wider MMO audience

## Upload 7 — Level Scaling Makes Every World Flat

This is the cleanest step from “RuneScape analyst” to “MMO design analyst.”

OSRS can provide the emotional opening: an area that once frightened the player but later became harmless. From there, the essay can compare static and scaling systems across games.

Possible structure:

1. Begin with an OSRS location that preserves the memory of former weakness.
2. Explain how static difficulty lets geography retain personal history.
3. Introduce level-scaling systems from other games.
4. Argue honestly for their advantages.
5. End with the cost: a world that can no longer remember who the player used to be.

This is an important structural change. OSRS becomes one case study rather than the complete subject.

## Upload 8 — The Four Horsemen of Steam

This is the first true general-gaming test.

It covers survival, open worlds, crafting, Early Access, scope, and studio survival rather than centring on one MMO. RuneScape: Dragonwilds can provide a bridge for the existing audience, but it remains one example inside a broader Steam argument.

Do not necessarily publish this as early as possible just because it is complete. Its performance becomes more informative after several bridge videos have already attracted non-OSRS viewers.

## Upload 9 — The People vs. the Collection Log

Return to a strong OSRS topic with clear debate potential.

The courtroom structure and the argument about measurement changing motivation have broader relevance, but the recognizable subject gives the existing audience a reliable entry point.

This creates a useful rhythm:

> broader test → trusted anchor → broader test

That is safer than progressively removing OSRS until the original audience disappears.

# Phase 4 — Test whether viewers now follow the analyst

## Upload 10 — You Were Never the Hero

This is likely the strongest second WoW choice because the argument extends beyond WoW:

> A world's indifference is what allows the player to manufacture significance.

It depends less on raid-history familiarity than some other Project W scripts. It is therefore a stronger general-design bridge than a narrowly historical Classic topic.

## Upload 11 — Cross-game bridge essay

This video should make a general claim but retain at least one strong OSRS or MMO example.

Its purpose is to test whether viewers can accept several games inside one argument without needing a single dominant franchise anchor.

Suitable forms include:

- One design problem across four games.
- One disliked mechanic defended through several genres.
- One emotional experience explained through different systems.
- One interface convention traced across game history.

## Upload 12 — Fully general game-design essay

This should be a topic that does not need OSRS, WoW, or the MMO category to justify itself.

The video should still preserve the channel's recognizable method:

- Sharp thesis.
- Concrete mechanic.
- Strong counterargument.
- Calm visual pacing.
- A conclusion that expands the implication rather than summarizing the body.

This is the first serious test of whether the channel's identity now rests on the analyst rather than the game.

# Interim video ideas for the final transition

The repository is already strong for OSRS and MMO design. The weaker area is the final move into repeatable general game-design essays. These ideas are possible bridges, not commitments.

## 1. Your Save File Is the Real Main Character

**Thesis:** In persistent and systemic games, the object players become attached to is often not the avatar but the accumulated state of the world: the base, account, colony, campaign, or save containing evidence of every prior decision.

Possible games:

- Old School RuneScape account progression.
- Minecraft worlds.
- RimWorld colonies.
- XCOM campaigns.
- Football Manager saves.
- Animal Crossing islands.

Why it fits the pivot:

It begins with the emotional logic of an OSRS account but extends naturally beyond MMOs.

Possible title:

> **Your Save File Is the Real Main Character**

## 2. Fast Travel Eats the Map

**Thesis:** Fast travel does more than save time. It removes the journeys that teach players how a world is connected, gradually converting geography into a menu.

Possible games:

- OSRS teleport networks.
- Vanilla WoW boats and flight paths.
- Skyrim.
- Elden Ring.
- Breath of the Wild or Tears of the Kingdom.
- Red Dead Redemption 2.

Why it fits the pivot:

The existing boat essay supplies a strong MMO case study, while the wider argument is understandable to nearly every open-world player.

Possible title:

> **Fast Travel Makes Open Worlds Smaller**

## 3. Durability Is Not About Realism

**Thesis:** Weapon durability exists to interrupt possession, force improvisation, support economies, and keep equipment temporary. Realism is often only the explanation printed on the tooltip.

Possible games:

- Breath of the Wild.
- Minecraft.
- Fallout.
- Survival-crafting games.
- MMOs with repair costs.

Why it fits the pivot:

It matches the channel's established strength: taking a disliked number or restriction and explaining the design problem it solves.

Possible title:

> **Weapon Durability Is Good, Actually**

## 4. The Most Important Places in Games Do Nothing

**Thesis:** Idle spaces—banks, camps, hubs, taverns, safe rooms, and ships—create social memory and emotional rhythm precisely because they do not maximize progression.

Possible games:

- OSRS bankstanding.
- WoW cities.
- Monster Hunter gathering hubs.
- Red Dead Redemption 2's camp.
- Deep Rock Galactic's space rig.
- Mass Effect's Normandy.

Why it fits the pivot:

The bankstanding essay supplies the OSRS foundation, but the broader video can examine why downtime spaces matter across social and single-player games.

Possible title:

> **The Most Important Places in Games Do Nothing**

## 5. Quest Markers Turn Adventure Into Administration

**Thesis:** Objective markers solve the problem of being lost by replacing spatial understanding with task completion. The player stops navigating the world and begins servicing an interface.

Possible games:

- OSRS and Quest Helper.
- Skyrim.
- Ubisoft open worlds.
- Elden Ring.
- Morrowind.
- Breath of the Wild.

Why it fits the pivot:

It extends the Wiki video's interface argument without duplicating it. The Wiki essay asks whether documentation became part of the game. This essay asks what happens when instructions replace spatial reasoning.

Possible title:

> **Quest Markers Changed How We See Games**

## 6. Co-op Games Need Bad Jobs

**Thesis:** Co-op becomes socially meaningful when someone must perform an unglamorous but necessary role. Complete self-sufficiency can produce parallel solo players occupying the same lobby.

Possible games:

- Vanilla WoW class dependency.
- Helldivers support roles.
- Deep Rock Galactic.
- Sea of Thieves.
- Overcooked.
- Survival-game base labour.

Why it fits the pivot:

It expands the central idea of **Broken on Purpose** into a broader theory of cooperative design.

Possible title:

> **Co-op Games Need Bad Jobs**

# Performance gates

Do not decide whether the pivot works from one non-OSRS upload.

A broader video will initially be shown to an audience trained on OSRS. Its first result may reflect audience mismatch rather than weak content. Evaluate groups of experiments rather than isolated uploads.

## After Upload 5 — first fully non-OSRS test

Continue the planned expansion when the WoW video reaches roughly:

- At least **60–70% of the channel's median OSRS seven-day views**.
- Similar first-30-second retention.
- Similar average percentage viewed.
- A meaningful proportion of new viewers.
- Comments engaging with the argument rather than only requesting OSRS.

These are rough planning thresholds, not laws. A video can still be strategically valuable below them when it has unusually strong retention, subscriber conversion, search durability, or catalogue spillover.

## After Upload 8 — first fully general test

Accelerate the pivot when:

- A general video matches or exceeds ordinary OSRS performance.
- It attracts meaningful Browse traffic rather than depending entirely on game-name Search.
- Viewers proceed from it into both OSRS and non-OSRS catalogue videos.
- Subscriber conversion per thousand views is comparable to OSRS videos.
- Returning viewers do not disproportionately skip the upload.

Slow the pivot when:

- Two consecutive non-OSRS videos receive below roughly **40–50% of normal OSRS views**.
- Their retention is materially weaker, not merely their impressions.
- Returning viewers repeatedly avoid broader topics.
- General videos consistently lack a concrete mechanical hook.
- The broader production burden is materially higher than OSRS production.
- The creator is less interested in researching or making them.

## Interpreting weak results

A broad video with strong retention but low impressions may indicate that the content works but YouTube has not yet identified the audience. That warrants another controlled experiment.

A broad video with weak click-through rate and weak retention probably indicates that the premise, packaging, or execution failed. Do not automatically conclude that all non-OSRS topics are impossible.

A broad video with weak first-week performance but durable search or catalogue growth may still be valuable over a longer horizon.

# Ongoing content ratio after the transition

Do not plan to stop making OSRS videos. OSRS should become a recurring pillar rather than a confinement.

A possible mature mix is:

- **25–35% OSRS**
- **25–35% other MMOs or persistent games**
- **30–50% general game design**

At three monthly uploads, that could mean:

- One OSRS essay.
- One MMO or persistent-world essay.
- One wider gaming essay.

At four monthly uploads, use the fourth slot for whichever category currently has the strongest idea.

This ratio should remain flexible. Audience results, personal enthusiasm, sponsor opportunities, current game releases, production feasibility, and exceptional topics should override the target mix.

# Packaging rules during the pivot

## Keep the channel identity stable

Do not change the channel name. **The Cube Analyst** already works across games.

Keep consistent:

- The cube mascot.
- Typography and thumbnail logic.
- Calm visual pacing.
- Argument-first scripts.
- The recurring analytical voice.
- The willingness to defend disliked friction and criticize accepted design wisdom.

The exhibit changes. The analyst does not.

## Titles should lead with the argument

Good:

- **Best-in-Slot Is Bad Game Design**
- **Every Vanilla WoW Class Is Broken on Purpose**
- **Level Scaling Makes Every World Flat**
- **Fast Travel Makes Open Worlds Smaller**

Weaker:

- A Discussion About Level Scaling
- My Thoughts on Classic WoW Classes
- Comparing OSRS and Other MMOs
- General Game Design Episode 1

Game names can support search in descriptions, thumbnails, tags, and secondary title language, but the browse-facing promise should remain a sharp claim.

## Do not make the pivot itself the content

Avoid titles, intros, or community messaging such as:

- The Channel Is Changing
- Why I Am Leaving OSRS
- My New Direction
- Welcome to General Game Design

The audience does not need a strategic announcement. It needs a sequence of videos worth clicking.

# Production rules during the pivot

Broader videos can become harder to edit because they require footage from several games. The transition should not accidentally destroy upload consistency.

Prefer general topics that can be produced through:

- Games already owned and understood.
- Easily captured footage.
- Official trailers and press assets where permitted.
- Store-page imagery.
- Simple diagrams and text.
- A few strong case studies rather than ten shallow ones.
- Long visual holds rather than rapid montages.

A general essay does not need to mention every relevant game. Three well-developed examples are usually stronger than twelve logo cameos.

When a topic requires extensive unfamiliar gameplay, technical setup, expensive purchases, or weeks of research, compare its likely value with a stronger and easier OSRS or MMO script.

The pivot must remain sustainable enough to publish.

---

# What not to do

Do not:

- Announce that the channel is leaving OSRS.
- Change the channel name during the experiment.
- Release all five Project W scripts consecutively.
- Label the transition as a numbered series.
- Force OSRS into a topic where it contributes nothing.
- Remove OSRS from the content mix before broader videos prove themselves.
- Judge the entire strategy from one upload.
- Judge only by raw views while ignoring retention, traffic source, subscriber conversion, and catalogue behaviour.
- Hold a strong, timely video merely because it violates the provisional order.
- Publish a weak bridge video simply because the roadmap says it is next.
- treat this document as more authoritative than actual audience evidence or personal creative judgment.
