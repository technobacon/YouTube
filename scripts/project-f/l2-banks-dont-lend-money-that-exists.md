# Banks Don't Lend Money That Exists — Full Script (Project F, launch #2)

**Thesis:** When a bank grants a loan it does not hand over other people's savings — it creates the money at that moment, by typing a matching deposit into existence, and repayment destroys the money again. This is the mainstream description: the Bank of England published it in 2014, in plain English, correcting the textbook. The video's real subject is the gap that fact exposes — a civilization in which almost nobody, including most of the people angry about banks, knows where its money comes from — and the honest, both-hands answer to the question the reveal detonates: *is that a scam?* (Mostly no. Partly yes. Precisely where is the essay.)
**Format:** Magic trick, explained — the classic three acts. **The Pledge** (the trick performed: £300,000 appears), **The Turn** (the method revealed, with the guild's own published confession), **The Prestige** (what it means that the trick is real). The magician-explainer register licenses teaching the mechanism twice: once as wonder, once as method.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,100 words)
**Voice dial:** High. Stage-patter confidence through the acts, dead sincerity at the balance sheet, register break at the reveal. One profanity, placed exactly there.
**Facts:** BoE Q1 2014 bulletin ("Money creation in the modern economy," McLeay/Radia/Thomas) verified 2026-07-08, including the loan-creates-deposit finding and the explicit correction of the intermediary/multiplier story. The ~97% deposits figure and quote verbatims flagged below.

## SCRIPT

Watch closely. I'm going to do the trick slowly, because even slowly, you won't see it.

[VISUAL: black stage. A single spotlight on a desk: a keyboard, a screen showing an account balance of £0.00]

A couple walks into a bank — this is not a joke, though it has the shape of one — and they sign for a mortgage. Three hundred thousand pounds. Papers pass, a person behind a desk does something unglamorous with a computer, and — there.

[VISUAL: the balance ticks from £0.00 to £300,000.00. One keystroke sound, absurdly small.]

Three hundred thousand pounds, in the account, spendable this afternoon. Real enough to buy a real house from a real stranger who will really retire on it. Now — the only question this video is about:

» Where was that money the day before? `[beat]` You have an answer. Almost everyone has the same answer. It's wrong, and the fun of the next fourteen minutes is that I can prove it's wrong using the magician's own confession.

---

**Act one. The Pledge — the story you already believe.**

Every magic trick begins with something ordinary, and here's ours: the model of banking you've carried since childhood, the one the piggy bank taught you. It goes like this. Savers deposit money in the bank. The bank keeps a slice for safety and lends the rest to borrowers. The bank is a *pipe* — money flows in from the patient, out to the ambitious, and the bank skims the difference in interest for arranging the introduction. Your £300,000, in this story, is other people's deposits: grandmothers' pensions, salary accounts, decades of others' thrift, gathered and re-aimed at your house.

It's a lovely model. It's coherent, it's moral — thrift funds enterprise, patience is rewarded — and it's in actual textbooks, dressed up as the "money multiplier": deposits come in, a fraction gets re-lent, that loan becomes someone's deposit, a fraction of *that* gets re-lent, and money multiplies down a chain that always starts with a saver.

Savor the model. Check it against your intuitions. Notice it's how films, journalists, and most politicians talk about banks — *It's a Wonderful Life* runs entirely on it; your money's "in Joe's house, and the Kennedy house." `[beat]` Now let's kill it.

---

**Act two. The Turn.**

When the bank approved that mortgage, here is the full and complete list of things it did. It checked the couple could plausibly repay. It registered a claim on the house. And then it typed £300,000 into their account.

`[slow]` That's it. That's the trick.

No vault opened. No grandmother's pension was fetched, moved, or even consulted. No pile of existing money anywhere got smaller so that this account could get bigger. The bank wrote a numeral into a ledger, and at the moment of writing, the money — new money, money that had never existed in any form, anywhere — began to exist. The loan is not a transfer. `[beat]` The loan is the mint. That's the whole fucking trick, and it's performed hundreds of thousands of times a day, in strip-mall branches, by people in sensible knitwear.

Now, you're having one of two reactions. Either this sounds obviously false — banks can't just *type money*, that's counterfeiting with extra steps — or it sounds like something from the conspiracy shelf, next to the videos with the red string. So here is the part I genuinely love, the reason this topic is the perfect second video for this channel: the magician's guild published the method. In 2014, the Bank of England — the second-oldest central bank on Earth, the least excitable institution in Britain — put out a bulletin titled *Money Creation in the Modern Economy*, in plain English, stating: whenever a bank makes a loan, it creates a matching deposit in the borrower's account, *thereby creating new money*. Their italics barely needed. And then, because apparently someone in Threadneedle Street was in a mood that quarter, the same document walks through the two popular stories — banks as intermediaries lending out deposits, and the textbook multiplier — and rejects them, by name, as misconceptions.

[VISUAL: the actual bulletin PDF on screen; the key sentences highlighted as read. Hold on "thereby creating new money."]

The reveal isn't the claim. `[beat]` The reveal is the *source*. This has been on a central bank's website for over a decade, downloadable, cited, taught in some graduate programs — and the piggy-bank story still runs the public conversation, the news coverage, and several textbooks. The method is published. Nobody reads the bulletin. It's the only magic trick in the world protected by being *boring*.

---

**The method, slowly. Because you deserve to see the gears.**

A bank's books have two sides, and the trick lives in their symmetry. When the mortgage is signed, the bank gains an *asset* — your promise to repay £300,000 plus interest, a genuinely valuable thing, enforceable, backed by a house. Simultaneously it writes itself a *liability* — the £300,000 deposit it now owes you on demand. Asset and liability, born together, equal and opposite, like a particle pair. The balance sheet balances at every instant; nothing was stolen from anywhere; the books are immaculate. `[quick]` And the economy's total money supply just grew by £300,000, because a bank deposit — the number in an account — *is* money. It's what you spend when you tap a card. It's what your salary arrives as.

And the symmetry runs backwards, which is the detail almost nobody's intuition survives: when you repay the loan, the money is *destroyed*. Not recycled — destroyed. Your deposit shrinks, the bank's claim shrinks, the particle pair annihilates, and the money supply contracts by the amount repaid. Money, in this system, is not a fixed pile passed hand to hand. It's a population — born in lending, dying in repayment — and its total size depends on how those two rates compare, which is why credit booms swell the money supply and credit crunches strangle it.

One more gear, then the act break. How much of the money you use was born this way? In the modern economy, essentially all of it that matters: the notes and coins — the only money most people think of as "real" — are a rounding error, a few percent of the total; the rest, on the order of ninety-seven percent in the BoE's own era-of-publication figures — flagged, verify the verbatim — is bank deposits. `[slow]` Which means the money in your life was, overwhelmingly, typed into existence by a commercial loan officer, somewhere, deciding someone was good for it.

---

**Interlude: why banks can't just print themselves rich. This section is load-rated; stand on it.**

Because I know the red string is out. If banks create money by typing, why work? Why doesn't the bank type itself a quadrillion and buy Portugal?

Constraints — real ones, and this is where the sane version separates from the crank version. First: the bank hasn't given itself money; it's given *you* money and itself your debt. Its profit is the interest margin, and only if you repay — every typed pound is a bet on a specific human's future, and enough bad bets kill the bank; ask several dead banks. Second: the moment you *spend* the £300,000, it flows to the seller's account at another bank, and your bank must settle that transfer with central-bank reserves it cannot type for itself. Third: capital requirements — regulation obliges the bank to hold a buffer of its own funds against every loan, so its lending capacity is leashed to its solvency. And fourth, over it all, the central bank sets the interest rate that governs whether borrowing is worth anyone's while — the guild licenses the trick, meters it, and prices it. `[beat]` Banks operate a mint with a governor bolted on. The scandal, such as it is, was never "infinite money." It's a subtler question, and it's the essay's last act: *who aims the mint?*

---

**Act three's shadow first: is it a scam? Both hands.**

The case for outrage, stated properly: a private company has been granted the power to create the thing everyone else must *earn* — and it charges interest on the creation. When the bets pay, the margin is private; when the trick collapses at scale, as in 2008, the public backstops the magicians, because the deposits they've typed are the money supply itself and can't be allowed to vanish. Heads the guild wins, tails the audience pays. That asymmetry is real; it has a body count of savings; no honest version of this video denies it.

The case for the trick, stated just as properly: elastic money may be the most productive invention in economic history. Chain the money supply to something fixed — gold, say — and credit can't grow when opportunity does; every boom chokes on a shortage of tokens; the nineteenth century ran that experiment repeatedly and it kept ending in deflationary seizures. Letting money be born wherever a credible promise meets a willing lender means the token supply *tracks the future* rather than the mining industry. When it works, it's why your century has antibiotics and the 1600s had turnips. The mechanism is a genuine civilizational upgrade — and, per the constraints section, it's less "out of nothing" than "out of *someone's* credible tomorrow."

Where I actually land — interpretation, labeled: the trick is fine; the *aim* is the live question. New money enters the economy wherever banks point it, and for decades they've pointed most of it at property — at bidding up houses that already exist rather than funding things that don't yet — and that rewires who can afford a life, one immaculate balance sheet at a time. Nobody voted on that either. The keyboard is legitimate; the question of what it's aimed at belongs to all of us; and you can't be in that conversation if you still think banks lend out grandmothers.

---

**Act three. The Prestige.**

The Pledge was the money you thought existed. The Turn was that it didn't. The Prestige is the part that should keep you staring at the ceiling:

it doesn't matter.

The couple got the house. The stranger retired. The £300,000 was never *anywhere* — and the house is real, the retirement is real, the twenty-five years of the couple's careful repayment will be real, and at the end of them the money will vanish back out of existence having done its work. `[slow]` Money, it turns out, was never the substance. It's the *notation* — the way a civilization writes down promises between strangers so they can be kept at scale — and a bank, under the marble and the fees, is a notary with a mint attached: it witnesses a promise about somebody's future and turns it into something the whole world will accept as payment today.

Which means the thing in your account isn't gold's ghost. `[beat]` It's someone's mortgage. Someone's small-business loan. Someone's promise, typed, circulating, briefly wearing the shape of *your* purchasing power on its way back to being kept — or broken.

» The trick works because everyone believes it. So does the audience. `[beat]` So do the magicians. And every morning, the whole theater — several billion seats — files in again, sits down, and *keeps believing*, which is either the most fragile thing you've ever heard or the most astonishing act of collective faith our species performs daily, before breakfast, without once calling it faith.

[VISUAL: the spotlight desk again; the balance ticks £300,000.00 → £0.00. The keystroke sound. House lights up — the "theater" is a bank branch, ordinary, open for business. Cut.]

Show's over. It's also just started; it never stops. Mind how you go.

## Flags for the user

- **BoE bulletin** — verified 2026-07-08: "Money Creation in the Modern Economy," McLeay, Radia & Thomas, BoE Quarterly Bulletin 2014 Q1, on bankofengland.co.uk. The loan-creates-deposit finding and the explicit rejection of the intermediary and multiplier stories are both in it. **Pull the exact sentences verbatim from the PDF before making the on-screen highlight card** — the script paraphrases closely ("whenever a bank makes a loan, it creates a matching deposit… thereby creating new money") but the card must be letter-perfect, since inviting the pause-and-verify crowd is the whole strategy.
- **~97% deposits vs. ~3% currency** — the widely cited BoE-era figure for broad money composition in the UK; the script flags it in-voice ("flagged, verify the verbatim"). Confirm the number and its date from the bulletin/companion piece, and note it's UK broad money — a US card would use different (similar-magnitude) figures.
- **Settlement in central-bank reserves / capital requirements / rate channel** — standard and safe at prose altitude; if the constraints section gets cards, source Basel capital rules and the BoE's own "limits on money creation" section (the bulletin has one — use it, it's tailor-made).
- **"Several dead banks"** — rhetorical; if the edit wants examples, credit-loss failures (not pure runs) are the right category — choose carefully (e.g., 1980s S&L lending losses) since 2008/2023 failures mix causes.
- **2008 backstop framing** — "the public backstops the magicians" is fair at prose altitude; any card should distinguish deposit insurance, bailouts, and central-bank liquidity rather than merging them.
- **Property-lending share** — "for decades they've pointed most of it at property" reflects the documented dominance of real-estate lending in modern bank credit (Jordà–Schularick–Taylor's "great mortgaging" work is the standard cite); pull that paper before any card, and keep "most" rather than a number until then.
- **Gold-standard deflationary seizures** — the 19th-century characterization is standard economic history (Long Depression, recurring panics); keep at prose altitude or source a named history for a card.
- ***It's a Wonderful Life* reference** — the "money's in Joe's house" speech is real and famous; ironically the Building & Loan in the film genuinely *was* closer to an intermediary — if a pedant thread matters, the script's use ("runs on the model") survives, but don't put the film on a card as an example of a modern bank.
