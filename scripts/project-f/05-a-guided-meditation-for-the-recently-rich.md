# A Guided Meditation for the Recently Rich — Full Script (Project F, #5)

**Thesis:** A lottery ticket is honestly priced for the product it actually sells: a licensed, vivid, three-day daydream — hope as entertainment, at two dollars among the cheapest pleasures on the market, and the "tax on people who can't do math" sneer misreads the purchase entirely. The scandal is somewhere else. Americans spend over a hundred billion dollars a year on tickets, the poorest zip codes spend a share of income more than thirty times the richest, and the seller is the state — which knows precisely which customers are buying the daydream as entertainment and which are buying it as a *plan*, and advertises to the second group anyway.
**Format:** Guided meditation. The video literally performs the product — a soft-voiced meditation into the winning fantasy — then opens your eyes, prices the session, and keeps returning to the pose as the numbers darken. The final meditation runs without the ticket.
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes (~1,950 words)
**Voice dial:** High and quiet. No profanity by design — the meditation register does the tonal work and a swear would shatter it for a cheap laugh. Carries the batch's second structural `[hold]`-adjacent silence (scored, not notated — see music).
**Facts:** $104.7B US lottery spend (2024), poorest-1%-of-zip-codes ~$600/yr (~5% of income) vs. wealthiest ~$150 (~0.15%), the 33× income-share figure, poorest-third-buys-half, and the 10%-income-drop→4%-spend-rise correlation all verified 2026-07-08 (Census, Fortune, Elm Wealth). Odds figures and the habitual-player stats flagged below.

## SCRIPT

Close your eyes.

[VISUAL: black screen. It stays black longer than is comfortable. A faint warm tone.]

No — really. This works better if you play along, and nobody can see you. Breathe out, slowly. Good.

It's Tuesday evening. You're standing in your kitchen with your phone in one hand and a small paper rectangle in the other, and you are checking the numbers, which you do as a joke, the way everyone does it as a joke. First number matches. Funny. Second number matches, and something in your chest goes very quiet. `[slow]` Third. Fourth. You put the phone down on the counter, face up, very carefully, as if it were suddenly made of something older and more expensive than glass. Fifth.

Notice what your mind is already doing. It's Wednesday morning now, in there. You're writing the message to your boss — polite, warm even; you can afford warmth now, you can afford anything — and notice that in the fantasy you don't gloat. You're gracious. You were always going to be gracious, given the chance; you've known that about yourself for years and nobody's ever had the budget to check.

Your mother's roof gets fixed first. That's simply true; it was always first on the list, and feel how *ready* the list was — the list has been waiting, fully drafted, for years. The friend whose debt you'd clear without a word. The brother you'd call. The town you'd leave, and the specific gentle way you'd leave it. Breathe. `[beat]` Stay as long as you like. The rate is very reasonable.

[VISUAL: the black screen warms faintly through the fantasy — the palest suggestion of morning light — then, on the next line, snaps to a fluorescent-lit gas station counter.]

Open your eyes.

» What you did just now — those ninety seconds — that's the product. `[beat]` That is what a lottery ticket is, what it's always been, and almost every serious person who has ever explained the lottery to you has explained the wrong object.

---

**Session two. What two dollars buys.**

Here's the standard lecture — you've heard it; there's a version on every finance channel. The odds of the big jackpot sit somewhere around one in three hundred million; you're likelier to be struck by lightning while being attacked by a shark; therefore the lottery is a tax on people who can't do math. Sneer, subscribe, next video.

One problem with the lecture. `[beat]` Everyone already knows. Ask the queue at the gas station whether they expect to win and they'll laugh at you — the astronomical odds are the single most famous fact about the product. A hundred million adults have somehow not been reached by the news that gambling is gambling in the way the lecture assumes. The lecture prices the ticket as a *probability*, finds the probability preposterous, and declares the buyer a fool. But watch the buyer. She folds the ticket into her purse, and for the next three days — at the sink, on the bus, in the fluorescent hum of a shift that goes on slightly too long — she owns a door. Behind the door: the kitchen, the phone, the roof, the graciousness. The ticket's *actual* function is a license to dream in the first person, concretely, with a straight face — and the license is valid until Saturday's draw.

Now price *that*. Two dollars for three days of a second life. `[quick]` A cinema ticket runs fifteen dollars for two hours of someone else's life; the paperback, again someone else's; the theme park wants a hundred and fifty to make your stomach drop for eleven seconds at a time. Per hour of genuine, first-person, custom-fitted hope, the lottery ticket is plausibly the cheapest entertainment ever sold — `[slow]` and it's the only one on the shelf where *you* write the script.

I want to be careful here, because this sounds like a defense of the lottery, and it's on its way somewhere else. The daydream is real value, honestly delivered — hold that thought genuinely, because it survives the whole video. The trouble starts when we ask who's in the queue. We'll do one more session at the end, by the way. It works differently. `[beat]` First, the lights.

---

**Session three. Who's buying.**

[VISUAL: a map of the United States assembling out of small paper tickets, county by county]

Americans spent about a hundred and five billion dollars on lottery tickets in 2024. Said differently — flagged for the card, since accountings vary — that's more than movies plus concerts plus major sports tickets, combined. It's among the largest entertainment products in the country, sold at a counter, next to the gum.

Now light the map by income, because this is where the product review has to stop being cute. `[slow]` In the poorest one percent of American zip codes, the average adult spends around six hundred dollars a year on tickets — about five percent of income. In the richest one percent of zip codes: about a hundred and fifty dollars, a fraction of one percent. As a share of what they have, the poorest households are paying roughly *thirty-three times* the price the richest pay for the same paper. The poorest third of the country buys half of all the tickets. And the relationship runs live, like a dial: when a region's median income drops ten percent, lottery spending rises about four. `[beat]` Hope, it turns out, is a countercyclical industry. Demand peaks exactly when everything else is failing.

Sit with what the map actually shows, because I've been calling the product "entertainment" and the map is shaking its head. Where lives have exits — raises, equity, inheritances, a plausible ladder — the daydream sells as a toy, a two-dollar weekend rental, and that's innocent. Where the exits are bricked up, the same paper is carrying a different cargo. There are polls — flagged, verify before the card — that keep finding a meaningful slice of the country naming the jackpot as their most realistic route to wealth. Read that sentence again, slowly, and notice it's an indictment of nothing in the queue. `[slow]` A rational person who has correctly concluded that the ladder is gone will pay for the door. The door is the only thing on sale.

---

**The turn. Two defenses, both serious.**

First, the defense of the buyer, stated at full strength because I mostly believe it: the sneer is a luxury good. It's remarkably easy to call hope irrational from inside a life that hasn't needed to purchase any. Adults are allowed their pleasures; poor adults, too, a category the personal-finance industry keeps needing reminding of; and a two-dollar daydream chosen freely beats a great many things sold to the same queue at worse odds — payday loans, say, or optimism about employers. Anyone whose entire commentary on the lottery is *expected value* has reviewed the wrapper and mailed back the gift. Fine. That case stands.

But now look behind the counter, `[beat]` because the seller changes the story. The lottery is run by the state — the same institution that writes the gambling laws, prosecuted the neighborhood numbers games out of existence, and then reopened the same game under its own seal, in many cases at *stingier* payouts than the racket it replaced — flagged, but the history is real. The state sets the odds, keeps the margin, and spends the proceeds under headlines about schools — while the accounting shuffles in the back room, since money is fungible and a dollar labeled "education" frees a dollar that was already there. And then it advertises. `[slow]` Billboards, saturation radio, scratch-card displays at the exact eye-level of the exact counters in the exact neighborhoods the income map lit up. The house has read the same research this video has. It knows which customers hold the ticket as a toy and which hold it as a lifeboat — and it budgets its persuasion toward the lifeboats.

So here's where I land, and it's an interpretation; grade it yourself. The ticket is honest. The daydream is honest — real product, fair price, delivered as described. `[beat]` The conflict of interest is the scandal: one seller holding the monopoly on manufactured hope *and* significant responsibility for the conditions that make hope scarce. A government that sells you the door makes a margin on every year the ladder stays broken. It doesn't have to be a conspiracy. It only has to be a revenue line — and revenue lines defend themselves.

---

**Final session. As promised. This one works differently.**

Close your eyes again.

[VISUAL: black. The warm tone returns.]

Same kitchen. Same Tuesday. But this time there's no ticket — just the list, because the list was never in the ticket. It's been in you the whole time, fully drafted, waiting: what you'd fix, who you'd call, what you'd finally put down, the specific gentle way you'd leave. `[slow]` Walk the list slowly, item by item, and notice — really check — how many entries actually require three hundred million dollars.

The roof does. Fair. Some of the list is money, real money, and no breathing exercise pays a contractor; this video will not insult you by pretending otherwise.

But the call to your brother is two dollars of nothing. The graciousness you were saving for the jackpot — you've known that about yourself for years, remember; that one's been in stock this whole time, no draw required. The daydream, it turns out, was an inventory all along — the most honest accounting you own of what's missing — and every Saturday it gets folded up with the losing numbers and thrown away, and rewritten from scratch on Monday, and thrown away again, when it was never the part that needed a winning ticket to come true. Some of it, sure. `[beat]` Never all of it.

People buy hope wherever it's sold. That's the whole finding; it isn't a flaw in the species — it might be the best thing about us, actually, that we keep a door drawn on every wall. The question this video can't close, the one worth carrying out of the meditation, is quieter:

» why, in some zip codes, is the gas station the only shop that stocks it?

`[hold]`

Open your eyes.

[VISUAL: the black screen cuts to the gas station counter one last time — then past it, out the window, ordinary daylight, a street. Hold two seconds. Cut.]

## MUSIC — by section
*Style briefs, not named tracks. Through-line: one warm sustained tone — a tanpura-like drone or soft synth pad, the "meditation tone" — that owns the fantasy sections. The analysis sections get no comfort music at all; the alternation trains the viewer's body to feel when they're inside the product.*

- **Cold open · the meditation (0:00–2:30):** the warm tone plus the sparest possible piano — two or three notes a minute, spa-adjacent but sincere, never parody. Swell almost imperceptibly through the fifth number.
- **Eyes open (2:30–4:00):** hard cut to fluorescent room tone — gas-station hum, cooler buzz, no music. The absence is the point; let the `»` line sit in it.
- **Session two · pricing the daydream (4:00–6:30):** dry, light, almost playful — brushed rhythm, muted keys — for the entertainment-per-hour ledger. Thin it to nothing on "who's in the queue."
- **Session three · the map (6:30–9:00):** a slow low pulse, subterranean; single cold piano notes marking each statistic like pins on the map. No melody. Duck fully for "the door is the only thing on sale."
- **The turn (9:00–11:30):** sustained neutral strings under the buyer's defense; behind-the-counter shift gets one new low element — a barely audible cash-register/coin texture in the ambience rather than the score. Resist any villain scoring; the section argues, the sound stays civil.
- **Final session (11:30–13:00):** the meditation tone returns exactly as before — same key, same warmth — so the body recognizes the pose; piano slightly fuller now, human. Every element out before "why, in some zip codes" — the `[hold]` is unscored silence. After "open your eyes," daylight ambience only: street sound, a passing car. No music over the cut.
