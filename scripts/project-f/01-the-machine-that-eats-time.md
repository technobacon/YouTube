# The Machine That Eats Time — Full Script (Project F, #1)

**Thesis:** Compound interest is a real, working time machine — the only one commercially available — sold for a century with a forged celebrity endorsement, and its fare is charged in the one currency it cannot pay out. The exponential is honest. The brochure is not. And the machine's deepest setting, the one the manual never mentions, is that it pays its jackpot to a version of you that the waiting itself uses up.
**Format:** Owner's manual. The video reads you the manual for a machine you already own: what's in the box, operating instructions, warning label, troubleshooting, warranty. The manual's bureaucratic calm is the deadpan; the content keeps escaping it.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,000 words)
**Voice dial:** High. Flat manual-voice through the mechanics, register breaks loose at the warning label and the warranty.
**Facts:** Einstein attribution debunked (Quote Investigator, 2019 — earliest "eighth wonder" use is an anonymous 1925 bank ad); Fidelity "dead investors study" confirmed apocryphal (Morningstar's Fidelity contact had never heard of it); rule-of-72 arithmetic exact. Return assumptions and fee-drag figures flagged below.

## SCRIPT

Congratulations on your purchase.

[VISUAL: white screen; a folded paper manual drops into frame. Cover text: OWNER'S MANUAL — MODEL: COMPOUND INTEREST. EST. ~3000 BC. NO MOVING PARTS.]

You may not remember buying it. That's normal. Most owners acquire the machine passively — a savings account opened for you at seven, a pension form signed on your first day of work while a man named Keith explained parking. It doesn't matter. You own a time machine. Everyone does. It has no moving parts, it was invented before iron, and it is the only device ever built that reliably moves value from one point in your life to another.

This manual will cover operation, warnings, and troubleshooting — including the strange matter of the machine's best users, all of whom are dead. `[beat]` We'll get there.

But first, a note about the advertising.

---

**Section one. About the endorsement on the box.**

You've heard the quote. Everyone's heard the quote. *Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. He who understands it, earns it; he who doesn't, pays it.* — Albert Einstein.

[VISUAL: the quote in elegant serif over a photo of Einstein, the way it appears on ten thousand finance blogs]

Einstein never said it. `[beat]` There's no letter, no lecture, no scrap of paper. The quote investigators have gone looking — properly looking, archives and everything — and the trail ends at an advertisement. In 1925, a bank ran an ad calling compound interest the eighth wonder of the world. No author. Just copy. Somewhere in the decades after Einstein died, the greatest scientific brand in human history got stapled onto it, because a sentence sounds truer wearing someone else's face.

» The most famous sentence in personal finance was written by an ad man, `[beat]` and it's been running on a forged testimonial for a hundred years.

Hold that. Because here's what makes this worth fourteen minutes of your finite existence: the product behind the fake ad is real. That's the strange part. Con artists usually forge endorsements for things that don't work. This one works exactly as described. Which raises a question the manual will spend the rest of its pages circling — `[slow]` if the machine is genuine, what did they need the forgery for?

[VISUAL: the manual's table of contents; a finger stops on "WARNINGS, p.8"]

Something on page eight, probably.

---

**Section two. What's in the box.**

One rule. That's the entire mechanism: your money earns a return, and then the return earns a return. The interest gets interest. That's it — you can stop taking notes.

But run the rule forward and it builds a ladder. There's an old mental shortcut called the rule of 72: divide 72 by your yearly return, and that's roughly how many years the machine takes to double your money. Seven percent, about ten years a doubling. And doublings do something to a number that your brain, a device tuned for spotting leopards, is genuinely unable to feel.

[VISUAL: a chessboard; one grain of rice on the first square, two on the second — the classic — but abandon it halfway with a title card: "you've seen this one. here's yours."]

Forget the rice. Here's the version with your name on it. Say you put money in and leave it alone. First doubling: pleasant. Second doubling: you now have four times your money, and it took twenty years, and honestly it doesn't feel like much because twenty years also happened to *you* in the meantime. Third doubling: eight times. Fourth: sixteen.

Now the sentence the whole machine is hiding in. `[slow]` Every doubling adds more than everything that came before it, combined. All of it. The first thirty years of patient, diligent, sensible saving — the fourth doubling matches the whole pile in one step, and the fifth matches *that*. The machine back-loads its entire payout. It is, in the most literal sense, a device that does almost nothing for decades and then everything at once.

Which brings us to the operating instructions, and to the first thing the brochure mumbles.

---

**Section three. Operating instructions.**

Instruction one: switch the machine on as early in life as possible. Instruction two: don't touch it. There is no instruction three.

The manual would like to be more sophisticated than that, but the mathematics refuses. The machine runs on exactly one fuel — elapsed decades — and it cannot be overclocked. Chasing a hotter return usually voids the warranty; we cover that under "troubleshooting," near the wreckage.

And notice what those two instructions actually demand of a human being. The machine is *almost stationary* for the first twenty years. The graph everyone shows you — the hockey stick, the beautiful elbow — the flat part of that stick is not a detail. `[beat]` The flat part is your thirties and forties. The elbow is scheduled, if you start on time and nothing bites you, for somewhere around your sixties. The machine asks you to shovel money into a device that visibly does nothing, for the exact stretch of your life when the money is hardest to spare and the doing-nothing is hardest to watch, on the promise that the miracle detonates near the end.

[VISUAL: the exponential curve drawn full-width; a small stick figure standing on the flat part, looking up the slope; the elbow annotated "you, at 64, probably"]

Every other machine you own does its job while you're around to enjoy the job being done. This one is different, and the difference is the whole design. `[quick]` The kettle boils now. The car drives now. The time machine pays *then*, and it defines "then" without consulting you.

Page eight.

---

**Section four. WARNING label. Read before operating.**

The machine pays out in money. `[beat]` It charges in time.

Those are different substances, and the manual has been pretending they're the same substance since 1925. Every dollar the machine multiplies, it multiplies by holding it — and you — for decades. The fare for the miracle is the years themselves. And here's the design flaw, the one etched so small on the label you need most of a life to read it:

» time is the one currency the machine cannot pay out. It converts in one direction only. `[hold]` Money has never once, in five thousand years of operation, compounded back into an afternoon.

So look again at who collects. The payout arrives — genuinely arrives, the math is honest — to a version of you the waiting itself has been spending. The seventy-year-old who receives the fourth doubling paid for it with the thirty-five-year-old who watched the flat part of the graph. Same account. Different man. The machine's true function, underneath the marketing, is to move wealth between those two people — and it lets exactly one of them set the dial.

That's what the forgery was for, I think — and this is interpretation, mine, not arithmetic. The genuine product has a real cost that no honest label could make attractive. So the ad man reached for the face of the century and let it vouch for the part he couldn't say out loud. Fucking hell, it worked, too. A hundred years and counting.

---

**Section five. Troubleshooting.**

*Problem: my machine underperforms everyone else's.* There's a famous fix for this. Fidelity, the story goes, audited its accounts to find which investors did best, and the winners were the dead ones. Accounts nobody could touch. No panic-selling, no tinkering, no genius. Corpses, outperforming the market.

[VISUAL: a leaderboard; top three entries marked DECEASED, DECEASED, FORGOT PASSWORD]

That study doesn't exist. `[beat]` Nobody can find it; Fidelity's own people hadn't heard of it; it's a campfire story finance tells itself. Which makes it the second forged testimonial in this manual — this machine apparently attracts them, like wasps — and yet, infuriatingly, the fake study encodes a true instruction. The machine punishes attention. Every intervention is a chance to sell the dip and buy the peak, and humans take those chances with terrible enthusiasm. The dead don't. You're not required to die; you're required to *behave as if you can't log in*, which for most of us is nearly as hard.

*Problem: my machine is making someone else rich faster.* That one isn't a malfunction. That's the next section.

---

**Section six. A note from the manufacturer's critics.**

Because there's an objection to this entire manual, and it deserves the floor rather than a footnote. Compounding rewards whoever already has a surplus. That's the mechanism, stated plainly: money makes money, so more money makes more money, so the curve that looks like a miracle from the bottom looks like gravity from the top. Telling someone who's underwater on rent about the beauty of the fourth doubling is worse than useless — it's a small insult wearing a math costume. The machine is real *and* it is a wealth-concentration engine; both facts fit in one hand. When people say "the rich get richer," they're describing the product working.

I don't have a resolution for that, and I'd distrust anyone selling one in a fourteen-minute video. The honest version is smaller: the machine exists, it doesn't care whether you own it or it owns you — *he who doesn't understand it, pays it* is the half of the fake quote that's true, because debt is the same exponential pointed at your throat — and the only unforgivable position is not knowing which end you're standing on.

---

**Section seven. Warranty.**

The machine is guaranteed for life. Which is the problem, if you read the terms slowly — the guarantee and the life run out together.

So here is the one setting the manual cannot set for you. Somewhere there's an exchange rate between a dollar at seventy and an afternoon at thirty-five. Nobody can quote it. It isn't published anywhere; it moves; it's different for every account holder, and you find out the true rate exactly once, at the end, when the position closes. Every paycheck, you set that dial anyway — whether you meant to or you didn't.

[VISUAL: the manual's last page. A single dial, unlabeled, no numbers on it. A hand hesitates on it.]

Feed the machine. Genuinely — feed it; future-you is real, and he's counting on present-you not to be an idiot. But every so often, take an afternoon the machine wanted, and spend it on someone, badly, on purpose. `[slow]` It's the only transaction the fourth doubling can't beat, and the only one that pays out now.

`[beat]` Keep this manual in a safe place. You'll want to reread page eight.

[VISUAL: the manual closes; cover fills the frame; under the title, small print we haven't shown before: "BATTERY INCLUDED. NON-REPLACEABLE." Cut to black.]

## Flags for the user

- **Einstein attribution / 1925 bank ad** — verified via Quote Investigator (2019 article): no substantive evidence for Einstein, Rothschild, or Rockefeller; earliest "eighth wonder" application is a 1925 bank advertisement, unattributed. Safe for a card; cite QI on screen.
- **Fidelity dead-investors study is apocryphal** — verified: Morningstar's Fidelity contact had not heard of it; no study has ever surfaced. The script's framing ("campfire story") is accurate — keep it; do not let the edit soften it back into "a Fidelity study found…".
- **Rule of 72** — standard approximation, exact enough for prose; at 7% it gives ~10.3 years/doubling. Fine unhedged.
- **"Every doubling adds more than everything before it, combined"** — mathematically exact for a clean doubling sequence (2ⁿ vs. sum of prior = 2ⁿ−1); the "more than" is off by one unit — technically it adds one unit *less* than everything-plus-original. In practice with continuous compounding and contributions it reads true; if a pedant-proof card is wanted, phrase as "matches everything that came before" (the script already uses "matches" in the follow-up sentence).
- **~7% real return assumption** — implied by the "ten years a doubling" line; that's the long-run US equity real return neighborhood, but it's an assumption, and the script never states it as a promise. Keep the machine's returns unspecified on cards; if pressed, "US stocks have historically averaged something like 7% after inflation" with a "past ≠ future" footnote.
- **"Invented before iron / est. 3000 BC"** — compound interest appears in Old Babylonian mathematics (interest-on-interest problems); the manual-cover date is a gag with a real basis, but verify the Babylonian detail before putting a specific date on a card.
- **Fee drag** — deliberately cut from the script to keep it lean; if a card on fees gets added in edit, verify the standard "1% annual fee ≈ a quarter of your final balance over 40 years" arithmetic first.
