# A Letter of Recommendation — Full Script (Project F, #3)

**Thesis:** Your credit score is a reference letter written about you, by your debts, for an audience of lenders — and it grades exactly one talent: how gracefully and predictably you stay in debt. Wealth doesn't move it. Income doesn't move it. Thrift can *lower* it, paying off your only loan can lower it, and paying cash your whole life leaves you invisible. The number has existed only since 1989, and its strangest achievement isn't surveillance — it convinced its subjects to read a lender's profitability metric as a moral grade, and to raise themselves accordingly.
**Format:** Reference letter. The video drafts the letter on screen — typewriter, cross-outs, redrafts. The essay's sections are drafts one through four; the final draft is the human version the number can't write.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,050 words)
**Voice dial:** High. Bureaucratic-courteous through the letter drafts, breaking loose at the absurdities and the close. One profanity.
**Facts:** FICO launch (Feb 1989) and Fair/Isaac founding (1956, engineer Bill Fair + mathematician Earl Isaac) verified 2026-07-08; score-factor weights and "credit invisible" counts flagged below — US-specific throughout, which the script says out loud once.

## SCRIPT

To whom it may concern.

[VISUAL: a sheet rolls into a typewriter. The words appear as they're spoken.]

I am writing to recommend the bearer of this letter, whom I have known for — let's check — every day of their adult life. I have watched them continuously. I was there for the first card at eighteen, the missed payment in the bad spring, the car loan, the other card, the balance that sat too long in the expensive year. I never blink. I never forget anything for less than seven years. And when the bearer wants somewhere to live, or a car, or a phone contract, or — in some places — an insurance rate, you will not ask *them*. `[beat]` You'll ask me.

Someone has been writing this letter about you since you were eighteen. You've never read a draft. You've never met the author. You know it only as a number — three digits, somewhere between three hundred and eight fifty —

» and that number is the most consequential document ever written about you, `[beat]` including by the people who love you.

This video is about who writes it, what it actually grades, and the strange day in 1989 when it was born. Because it was born. There are people older than the credit score walking around with bad credit, and I find that genuinely funny.

*(A housekeeping line, once: this is the American machine — FICO and its cousins. Your country runs a different model with the same soul. Adjust locally.)*

---

**Draft one. What the letter decides.**

Start with the reach. The score sits between you and nearly every large threshold of adult life. The mortgage rate that determines whether the house is affordable — the letter sets it. The apartment application stacked against forty others — the landlord reads the letter first. The car loan, the small-business loan, the phone plan, the security deposit that doubles or vanishes. In some states, versions of your file shade what you pay to insure a car. One three-digit opinion, consulted at every door.

[VISUAL: a hallway of doors; at each one, a hand slides the same envelope through a slot before you arrive]

And the audience for the letter never meets you. That's the design, and — hold this thought for the turn — partly the *point*. The loan officer across the desk sees the number before they see your face, and mostly they don't see your face at all; the decision happens in a system, in milliseconds, on the letter alone. You are, to the modern lending industry, a courier for your own reference.

So it matters what the letter says. More precisely — it matters what the author is *able* to say. Which brings us to the author.

---

**Draft two. The author is younger than you think.**

The general-purpose FICO score was introduced in February 1989. `[beat]` That's it. That's the birthday. The number that reads as eternal as a blood type is younger than *Die Hard*. Your parents got mortgages without it. Entire generations built houses, businesses, and lives before the letter existed.

The company behind it goes back further — 1956, an engineer named Bill Fair and a mathematician named Earl Isaac, two men with a very mid-century American conviction that human judgment could be replaced by an equation, if the equation tried hard enough. For decades they sold custom scoring models to individual lenders. Then in '89 came the general model — one score, standardized, printed by the credit bureaus, portable across every lender in the country. A single national opinion of you, where before there had been thousands of local ones.

And what was there before? A man. `[slow]` A loan officer, behind a desk, with your file and his gut. Remember the man. He looks like the hero of this story — the human touch, the handshake, the neighbor who knows you. He is going to come back at the turn, and he is not going to look like the hero.

But first: what does the author actually watch? What's *in* the letter? FICO publishes the recipe, roughly, and I want to read it to you slowly, because the trick of the whole thing is hiding in plain sight in the ingredient list.

---

**Draft three. The recipe, read slowly.**

Five ingredients, roughly weighted. Whether you pay your debts on time — about a third of the letter. How much of your available credit you're currently using — nearly another third. How *long* you've had debts — call it a sixth. Whether you've asked for new debt recently, and whether you carry a pleasing *variety* of debts — the remainder between them.

[VISUAL: the five factors typed as a recipe card, each ingredient underlined as it's named]

Read the list again and notice what every single ingredient has in common. `[beat]` Debt. The score watches how you borrow, and it watches nothing else. Your salary appears nowhere in it. Your savings appear nowhere. Your net worth, your discipline, the emergency fund you built sacrificing every small pleasure for six years — invisible, all of it. You could have a million in the bank and no file at all. The letter has exactly one subject, and the subject is your conduct *as a borrower*.

Which produces some genuinely deranged corollaries, and if you've brushed against the machine you've met them. Pay off your only loan — close the chapter, debt-free at last, the thing every money book tells you to want — and your score can go *down*. The account that aged so gracefully is closed now; the letter's author has less to watch, and an author with less to watch writes a shorter, colder letter. Never borrow at all — pay cash, live within your means, the full grandmother program — and you get the coldest letter there is, which is none. Tens of millions of American adults are "credit invisible": no file, no score, no letter. The system's word for a person who has never owed anyone anything is *invisible*. `[beat]` What the hell kind of grader flunks you for having nothing to confess?

This one, and — deep breath — on its own terms, honestly. Because the letter is a forecast, and a forecast is all it ever claimed to be. It exists to answer one question for one audience: *if we lend this stranger money, will the repayments arrive on schedule?* Everything about it snaps into focus once you read it as what it is —

» a supplier reference. The bearer reliably generates interest, `[beat]` and has never once surprised us.

That's the whole letter. Graceful, predictable indebtedness. The highest scores in the country belong to people who are *perpetually, beautifully* in debt — always borrowing, always current, utilization low, history long, never a surprise. The grade isn't for getting out of debt. The grade is for being magnificent *at* it, indefinitely.

---

**The turn. In defense of the number.**

Now, the man behind the desk. Because every instinct says the equation is the villain of this story, and the record says otherwise, and the record deserves the floor.

Before the score, your loan was the loan officer's call, and his gut was the algorithm. And his gut had opinions. About your accent. Your address. Your skin, your sex, your last name, whether he'd seen you at the right church. Redlining was the standard practice of that world, drawn on actual maps you can still look at. A woman in most of that era needed a husband's signature to hold a credit card at all. `[slow]` The handshake economy everyone gets nostalgic for was a members-only club, and the membership criteria were exactly what you fear they were.

Against that, the score is blind in precisely the places the gut was cruelest. It cannot see your face. It doesn't know your race, your sex, or your church — by law it isn't given them, and it has no gut to overrule the file. On the day it launched, the equation was, plausibly, the fairest reader most applicants had ever been judged by. Strangers could get loans in places their faces would have disqualified them. That's real, and any honest version of this video says it plainly.

The modern critique is subtler and also real — blindness isn't the same as fairness when the *inputs* carry the history; thin files and zip-code-shaped lives mean the old maps can leak back in through the data. Both things are true at once, and anyone selling you just one of them is campaigning.

But here's what I actually want to leave in the turn, because it's the part nobody disputes and nobody says. Whatever the number's merits as a lending tool — `[beat]` look what it became in *us*. We were handed a supplier reference and we swallowed it as a report card on our souls. People announce a good score the way they'd announce a clean bill of health. People carry a bad one like a criminal record, and feel *shame* — shame, before a profitability forecast. Nobody mandated that. The number's quietest, largest achievement is that its subjects internalized it, and now we raise ourselves toward it voluntarily, the way you'd tend any grade — never once asking who the school belongs to.

---

**Final draft. The letter it can't write.**

So — one last draft. Everything the author watched you do for twenty years, and here is what never made the letter.

[VISUAL: the typewriter again; a new sheet. The words below appear as typed, cross-outs and all.]

The decade of rent, paid in cash, on the first of the month, every month — invisible; historically, rent mostly never reached the file. The money wired to your mother. The friend you carried for a year, interest-free, no schedule, because schedules aren't what that ledger runs on. The raise you didn't chase so you could be somewhere at five o'clock every day for a small person who will never know it. `[slow]` Every generous, prudent, privately heroic thing you have ever done with money is off-file. The letter watches the loans. The life happens in the margins the author can't see.

Which is fine — truly, it's fine — as long as everyone remembers which document is which. The score is a real thing about you, the way your shoe size is a real thing about you. Tend it; the doors are real and the rates are real, and future-you pays retail for present-you's neglect.

Just — `[beat]` when you're deciding what you think of yourself, check who signed the letter you're reading.

Because the human one, the one your people would write — *lent to me in the bad year, never mentioned it again* — circulates too. No bureau keeps it. It compounds anyway.

[VISUAL: the typed letter pulled from the machine. At the bottom, where a signature goes: three digits. Hold. Then, over it, a handwritten scrawl — a name, any name, human — and cut.]

Sincerely.

## Flags for the user

- **FICO general score, Feb 1989; Fair & Isaac founded 1956 (Bill Fair, engineer; Earl Isaac, mathematician)** — verified 2026-07-08 (myFICO history page, Wikipedia, multiple secondary). Safe for cards with a myFICO cite.
- **Factor weights (35/30/15/10/10)** — FICO's own published approximation; the script deliberately says "roughly" and paraphrases ("about a third… nearly another third"). Keep the hedge, or card the official FICO chart directly.
- **"Paying off your only loan can drop your score"** — true via account closure/mix/age effects and widely documented; it's situational, and the script says "can." Never let a card say "will."
- **Credit invisibles** — the standard figure is CFPB (2015): ~26 million credit-invisible US adults, plus ~19 million with unscorable files. The script says "tens of millions," which is safe; verify whether CFPB has fresher numbers before a card.
- **Insurance and credit** — credit-based insurance scores are legal in most but not all US states (California, Massachusetts, Hawaii ban/limit them); the script hedges with "in some states, versions of your file." Keep the hedge exactly.
- **Employers** — deliberately absent from the script: employers can pull credit *reports* (with consent), never FICO *scores*, and several states restrict it. If an employment line gets added in edit, it must make that distinction.
- **Redlining / maps; women needing a husband's cosigner pre-1974** — historically solid (HOLC maps; the Equal Credit Opportunity Act 1974 ended the cosigner regime). Verify ECOA specifics before cards; the script keeps both at prose altitude.
- **"By law it isn't given them"** — ECOA/Reg B prohibit using race, sex, etc. in credit scoring. Accurate at prose altitude; a card should name ECOA.
- **Rent reporting** — historically absent from files, now partially reportable via opt-in services and some bureau products; the script says "historically, rent mostly never reached the file." Keep "historically" and "mostly."
- ***Die Hard* (1988) vs. FICO (Feb 1989)** — checks out; keep.
