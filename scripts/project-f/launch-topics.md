# Project F — Launch Topics (the three door-openers)

Pitched 2026-07-08, on the owner's brief: **before the specific essays (LTCM, barter myth), open the channel with generic, high-volume subjects** so the algorithm can classify the channel and viewers can classify the voice. Each entry below carries thesis, format concept, title directions, the door-opener rationale, and the facts to verify.

> **Status update (2026-07-09): all three are now scripted end to end** — `l1-the-tax-nobody-voted-on.md`, `l2-banks-dont-lend-money-that-exists.md`, `l3-a-biography-of-the-line.md` — full pipeline (outline, script, music, 5-set SEO, flags, §2 audit), same as the numbered five. The pitches below remain as the design record; where a script deviates from its pitch (L1 dropped the pengő-on-camera idea into a flag, L2 absorbed the "97%" into the method section), the script wins.

Selection logic: a launch topic has to do four jobs at once — (1) sit on a **huge evergreen search family** so a zero-subscriber channel can be found at all; (2) be a subject **every adult already has a feeling about**, so the click needs no context; (3) still support a **sharp, arguable thesis** (the channel's whole identity — no explainers); (4) open naturally onto the exurb1a register without forcing it. Dozens of candidates were screened against those four (debt, taxes, retirement, "why everything is expensive," behavioral finance, rich-get-richer); these three scored highest. Behavioral finance ("your brain predates money") was the near-miss fourth — strong, but its psychology overlaps the compound-interest and lottery scripts already in the batch.

## L1 — Inflation: The Tax Nobody Voted On

**Lane:** personal finance / concept. **The grievance topic.** Nothing else in finance is felt daily by every single viewer regardless of wealth, age, or country.

**Thesis (arguable):** Inflation is best understood as a tax — an involuntary transfer from holders of cash to issuers of cash — with three properties no legislated tax could survive: nobody voted on the rate, it falls hardest on people who hold their savings in cash (the poor and the cautious), and it silently rewards the indebted. A knowledgeable viewer can argue back ("it's a monetary phenomenon, calling it a tax is polemic") — which is exactly the fight the comment section should host.

**exurb1a angle:** money as a battery for stored time. You worked the hours; the token holds the hours; the token leaks. A savings account is a bucket someone drilled, politely, on a schedule. The tender turn: inflation punishes exactly the people doing what their grandmothers taught them was virtuous — and the essay should sit with what it does to a society when *saving* becomes the naive strategy.

**Format concept:** **plumber's inspection report.** The video is a callout — the plumber walks the viewer's financial house, finds the leak, traces the pipes back to where they leave the property. Keeps the register domestic and deadpan; the history section (see below) plays as "previous water damage on record."

**History beat (the retention spike):** hyperinflation as the leak becoming a burst main — and the strongest case on record is Hungary, 1946: the pengő, prices doubling in well under a day at the peak, the largest-denomination banknote ever printed. (Owner's home turf; if the channel ever shows a face or a hand, this is the episode where a real pengő note — buyable for a few euros, ironically — appears on camera.) Weimar wheelbarrows are the cliché; the pengő is the record. Verify: peak doubling time (~15 hours, from memory), the 10^20 pengő note, dates.

**Title directions:** "Inflation Is a Tax (You Just Never Voted on It)" / "Your Savings Have a Leak" / "The Only Tax With No Collector." Search family: *inflation explained, why is everything so expensive, what causes inflation* — enormous, evergreen, and spikes with every news cycle.

**Door-opener rationale:** highest ambient-relevance topic in all of finance; the algorithm's cold-start test audience (browse + search on "inflation") is the largest available pool. Also the cleanest one-sentence demonstration of the channel's difference: every existing inflation video explains the CPI; this one asks who drilled the bucket.

**Verify before drafting:** the tax framing's intellectual lineage (Keynes's "by a continuing process of inflation, governments can confiscate…" quote — real, *Economic Consequences of the Peace*, but pull the exact wording); Hungary 1946 figures; whose cash holdings skew poor (the unbanked hold cash; the wealthy hold assets — find the study); seigniorage basics for the turn.

## L2 — Where Money Comes From: Banks Don't Lend Money That Exists

**Lane:** concept. **The revelation topic.** The single most mind-blowing generic fact in finance, and it has the receipts.

**Thesis (checkable, explosive):** When a bank grants you a loan, it does not hand over other people's deposits. It creates the money at that moment, by typing the number into your account — new money, added to the economy's total — and the loan's repayment destroys it again. This is the mainstream, central-bank-endorsed description: the Bank of England published "Money Creation in the Modern Economy" (Q1 2014 bulletin) saying, in nearly these words, that lending creates deposits and the textbook money-multiplier story is backwards. The video's honest tension: this is simultaneously *not a conspiracy* (it's on a central bank's website) and *not what anyone was taught* (the piggy-bank model of banking is fiction).

**exurb1a angle:** this is the second-channel's "you (probably) don't exist" — the ground the viewer stands on dissolving under real, cited content. Money spends most of the video becoming stranger: most of it has no physical form, most of it was typed into existence by a commercial loan officer, and the whole arrangement works because everyone keeps agreeing it does. Lands naturally beside the barter-myth script (#2) as a two-part cosmology the channel can cross-link later — this one launches first *because* it's the broader claim.

**Format concept:** **magic trick, explained.** Open on the trick performed (a mortgage "moves" £300,000 that never moves anywhere); then the classic reveal structure — the pledge, the turn, the prestige — except the punchline is that the magician's guild published the method and nobody read the bulletin. Fresh format, and it licenses the essay to teach the mechanism *twice* (once as wonder, once as method), which this topic needs.

**Title directions:** "Banks Don't Lend Money That Exists" / "Your Loan Created the Money You Borrowed" / "The Bank of England Explained the Trick (Nobody Read It)." Search family: *how do banks work, how is money created, fractional reserve banking* — large, evergreen, and the existing results are either dry or crankish; the sane-but-astonished middle is wide open.

**Door-opener rationale:** the strongest pure overturn-title of any generic finance topic — the claim sounds unhinged, the source is a central bank, and the click-to-verify loop (the channel's proven engine: "92 is half of 99," "Einstein never said it") is at maximum strength. Defines the channel as "the one that tells you how the machine actually works."

**Verify before drafting:** the BoE 2014 bulletin (title, authors, exact key sentences — quote them verbatim on a card); the money-multiplier textbook history; broad money vs. base money shares (the "most money is bank deposits" figure — BoE's own number was ~97% bank deposits vs. ~3% cash in 2014; re-verify and date it); repayment-destroys-money mechanics; what limits creation in practice (capital requirements, profitability, regulation) for the turn — the turn matters here, because "out of nothing" without the limits section reads as goldbug bait, and the channel must not be mistaken for that shelf.

## L3 — The Stock Market: A Biography of the Line

**Lane:** concept / personal finance. **The staple topic.** The question every future subscriber eventually types: why does the line go up, and should I trust it with my life?

**Thesis (arguable):** The stock market chart — "the line" — is the most consequential drawing on Earth: pensions, universities, governments, and your retirement are all quietly indexed to its continued rise. The folk explanation for why it rises ("the economy grows") is at best half the story; the honest components — retained earnings compounding, risk premium as payment for endured terror, survivorship (the US line is the *winner's* chart; other countries' lines have gone to zero), and a civilization-scale bet that the future keeps buying — are each stranger than the folk version. The essay's refusal: it will not tell you the line must keep rising; it will tell you what has to stay true for it to.

**exurb1a angle:** the line as a biography — born in coffee houses, nearly dying several times (1929, 1987 in a day, 2008), each near-death now a small dip in the long chart, which is either deeply comforting or a survivorship illusion, and the essay holds both honestly. The tender turn: buying the index is the closest thing modern life offers to the old men planting trees — you are buying slivers of work that hasn't happened yet, done by people who haven't been born, and the whole thing runs on a kind of faith nobody wants to call faith.

**Format concept:** **biography.** Played straight — birth, wild youth, near-deaths, maturity, the open question of old age — of a line, with the chart itself as the only recurring visual character. (The format also quietly teaches chart literacy: log vs. linear, the 1929 cliff that log-scale flattens, why every "line goes up" screenshot is an editorial choice.)

**Title directions:** "Why the Stock Market Always Goes Up (So Far)" / "A Biography of the Line" / "The Most Important Drawing on Earth." Search family: *why does the stock market go up, how does the stock market work, should I invest in index funds* — the biggest commercial-intent search family in the niche, which also makes it the highest-RPM audience of the three.

**Door-opener rationale:** plants the channel's flag in investing-adjacent territory (where finance YouTube's audience and ad rates concentrate) without becoming an advice channel; pairs with the compound-interest script as a natural early one-two; and the "(so far)" honesty is the brand — every competitor either promises the line or fears it, and the gap in the middle is this video.

**Verify before drafting:** long-run US equity return figures and their standard sources; the survivorship record (markets that went to ~zero: Russia 1917, China 1949 — verify treatment in the Dimson/Marsh/Staunton "Triumph of the Optimists" data); 1987's one-day drop (~22%, verify); earliest exchanges (Amsterdam 1602 VOC — verify details); what share of US households own stocks directly/indirectly (Fed SCF figure, ~60% including retirement accounts — verify and date).
