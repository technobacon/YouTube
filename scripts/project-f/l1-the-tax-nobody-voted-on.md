# The Tax Nobody Voted On — Full Script (Project F, launch #1)

**Thesis:** Inflation functions as a tax — levied without a vote, collected without a collector, falling hardest on the people who hold their lives in cash — and the strangest fact about it is that it's *deliberate*: every major central bank targets a positive leak rate, on purpose, for reasons that are genuinely good and genuinely never explained to the people paying. The video's job is to hold both facts at once — the leak is a tax *and* the leak keeps the house alive — and to refuse the crank exit and the shrug exit both.
**Format:** Plumber's inspection report. A callout to the viewer's financial house: find the leak, trace the pipes off the property, check the records for previous water damage (Hungary, 1946), inspect the odd fixture (the thermostat set to 2%), leave the invoice. Domestic, deadpan, unhurried.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,050 words)
**Voice dial:** High. Working-register plumber voice; the register lifts at the pengő section and the invoice. One profanity. Carries a `[hold]` (the pengő).
**Facts:** Pengő hyperinflation (prices doubling ~15 hours at peak, July 1946 monthly rate ~4.19×10¹⁶%, forint reform 1 Aug 1946, 100 quintillion note) verified 2026-07-08. Keynes quote, NZ 2% origin story, cash-holdings regressivity flagged below.

## SCRIPT

Morning. You reported a leak.

[VISUAL: a toolbox sets down on kitchen tiles; the shot stays low, workboots and pipe-level angles throughout]

Well — you didn't, technically. Nobody on this street ever calls it in, and every house on the street has it. Same leak. Same wall. It's the slow kind, which is the worst kind, because the fast kind gets fixed and the slow kind gets *lived with*. You've been living with it your whole life. Your parents lived with it. There's water damage in this family going back generations, and someone has finally got to walk you round the property and show you where it's coming from.

`[beat]` That's me. I do inflation. Kettle on, this'll take about fourteen minutes.

---

**First: the leak itself. Let's do the bucket test.**

Say you put money in a savings account and leave it alone — the responsible thing, the thing your grandmother told you to do, and your grandmother was working from the best information she had. Call it a bucket. Now watch the bucket.

[VISUAL: a literal bucket filling; the number painted on its side stays constant while the waterline, very slowly, drops]

The *number* on the bucket holds. That's the trick of it — check your balance in ten years and every digit will still be there, plus a little interest, and the statement will look like nothing happened. The number is fine. What leaks is what the number *buys*. At the sort of inflation rich countries call normal, the same bucket of money loses roughly a fifth of its purchasing power over a decade — and in the sort of years we've had recently, considerably faster. Nothing on the statement will ever say so. `[slow]` The leak doesn't show up in the numbers, because the leak is *in* the numbers — the water level and the waterline are measured in the same units, and the units are what's shrinking.

So the first finding on the report: the safest place your culture told you to keep your work is a bucket with a hole in it, and the hole is code-compliant. `[beat]` Which raises the obvious question — where does the water *go*? Water doesn't vanish. It drains somewhere. Hold that; we'll follow the pipes in a minute.

---

**Second: what to call this thing. And here's where the report gets argumentative.**

The textbook calls inflation a general rise in prices, which is true the way "unscheduled water feature" is true of a burst main. I want to log it under a different name, and I'll defend the name: it's a tax.

Run the definition. A tax transfers purchasing power from you to the issuer of the currency. Inflation does that — every year the money in your bucket buys less, and the institution that issues the money settles its debts in the shrunken units. A tax has a rate. This one has a rate; it gets announced monthly; it just isn't *called* a rate on anything you signed. And here's where it beats every other tax ever designed, from the collector's point of view: `[quick]` no return to file, no collector to face, no line item anywhere, and no vote. Income tax passed a parliament. VAT passed a parliament. This one gets *set* — we'll meet the people who set it, they're actually quite reasonable — but it has never once appeared on a ballot.

Don't take the framing from a plumber. John Maynard Keynes — the most influential economist of the twentieth century, and no crank — wrote that by a continuing process of inflation, governments can confiscate, secretly and unobserved, an important part of the wealth of their citizens. Confiscate. Secretly. His words, flagged for the card, and he wrote them *approvingly of the insight*, a century ago, in 1919. `[beat]` The leak has been in the literature the whole time. It's just written up in a register nobody reads in bed.

Now — a good inspector tells you when the counter-reading exists. Plenty of economists will object that "tax" implies intent, and inflation is often just weather: supply shocks, wars, harvests, pandemics. Fair, and logged. But hold the objection until you've seen the thermostat, because the thermostat is where the weather turns out to have a setting.

---

**Third: the pipes. Where the water goes.**

[VISUAL: pipe-tracing shot — a dye packet dropped in the bucket; colored water moving through walls, out of the house, down the street]

Follow the purchasing power as it leaves your bucket, and it surfaces in two places.

First: anyone who owes money. Inflation shrinks debts in exactly the way it shrinks savings — same solvent, opposite luck. If you owe a fixed number and the units rot, you repay in rotted units; the leak that drains your bucket is, in the house across the street with the big mortgage, filling the bath. A steady leak is a slow, permanent transfer from savers to borrowers — and look at who that pairs up: the people doing the thing everyone said was virtuous, paying the people doing the thing everyone said was risky.

And second — follow the biggest pipe, the main line, all the way down the hill — it arrives at the largest borrower that has ever existed: `[slow]` the government that issues the currency. The state owes trillions, fixed in units, and the state's central bank has a hand on how fast the units rot. I'm not alleging a conspiracy; I'm reading a plumbing diagram. When the same household owes the water bill *and* sets the water pressure, you don't need malice to predict the direction of the seepage. You just need the diagram.

One more branch line, and it's the ugly one. Who holds wealth as *cash*? Not the rich — the rich hold houses, shares, businesses, the upstairs assets that float on inflation and often rise with it. Cash is the wealth of the young, the poor, the unbanked, the cautious, the recently arrived — the people whose whole net worth fits in the bucket. `[beat]` The tax nobody voted on is also the only tax whose rate is *highest on the people with the least*, as a share of what they've got. Flag that for the researchers; the studies exist. The pattern shows up in every one of them.

---

**Fourth: previous damage on record. Because you should see what a burst main looks like.**

The file on this street goes back a long way, and the worst page in it — the worst page in *any* country's file, ever — is Hungary, summer of 1946.

[VISUAL: archival — Budapest 1946; the pengő notes with their impossible strings of zeros]

The war had wrecked the country, and the government met its bills the only way left: it printed. The currency was called the pengő, and in July 1946 the pengő achieved something no money before or since has matched. Prices, at the peak, were doubling roughly every fifteen hours. Not yearly. Not monthly. `[beat]` Between breakfast and the following dawn. Workers were paid daily, sometimes twice daily, and families ran — literally ran — to spend the envelope before evening, because there was a saying that summer: spend your pengő before it loses its value by sundown. The state printed a note for one hundred quintillion — a one with twenty zeros — the largest denomination ever put in a human hand, and by the end it bought approximately what a coin had bought the year before.

`[hold]`

Every price is a little sentence that says *we agree*. That July, a whole country watched the sentences stop meaning anything — the bucket didn't leak, it ceased to be a bucket — and in August they carried the old money out in crates, introduced the forint, and started counting again from one. The record still stands. `[slow]` Nobody has ever broken it, and I'd like to keep it that way, which brings us — and I know how this sounds — to the argument *for* the leak.

---

**Fifth: the odd fixture. There's a thermostat on your leak, and someone set it to 2.**

Here's the part of the inspection that cuts against everything I've logged so far. If leaking is theft, why does every serious central bank on Earth — the Fed, the ECB, the Bank of England, all of them — publicly, officially *target* a leak of about two percent a year? Deliberately. In writing. Why would the plumbers' guild mandate a hole?

Because the one thing worse than a slow leak is water that refuses to move. Deflation — prices falling — sounds like the saver's paradise, and it's a house-killer. When money gains value by sitting still, everyone sits still: purchases wait, wages can't adjust (nobody accepts a pay *cut*, so employers cut *jobs* instead), debts swell in real terms, spending stalls, and the whole plumbing airlocks. The 1930s were that. Japan spent decades fighting a milder version of that. `[beat]` A small positive leak keeps the water moving — it nudges everyone, gently, perpetually, to put money to *work* rather than let it pool — and economies where the water moves are the only ones where anyone's bucket is worth anything at all. The leak is a tax, and the leak is also the circulation pump. Both findings go on the report, and the report is not required to feel tidy. That's the honest version, and I'd rather hand you an untidy truth than a tidy grievance — the internet will sell you the tidy grievance in bulk, usually attached to a coin.

And the number itself? Two percent, the sacred global constant? It started in New Zealand, around 1990, and — flagged, verify before the card, because the story is almost too good — the figure entered the world roughly as a round number floated in a television interview before it was ever a model's output. The world's most consequential dial, and the setting is, `[beat]` to a first approximation, a vibe that stuck. Fucking plumbing, mate. It's like this everywhere.

---

**Last: the invoice.**

So, what you owe, and what I'd do — standard disclaimer, I fix pipes, I don't manage wealth.

The leak is real, permanent, and set on purpose, so the practical finding writes itself: the bucket is for *this year's* water — the emergency fund, the near money; that's what it's *for*, and the leak is the fee for keeping it liquid and safe. But a life's savings left in the bucket for decades is a donation, made in installments, to the borrowers of the world — grandma's advice was right about saving and wrong about the container, through no fault of hers; the container changed under her. Long-term money wants to be upstairs, in the assets that float. That part's just the diagram again.

The deeper line on the invoice is the one I'll leave you with. Money is stored time — hours you already worked, sealed in tokens — and a leak in the tokens is a quiet ruling about whose time keeps its value: `[slow]` the past's, or the future's. Every civilization sets that dial somewhere, mostly without telling its citizens it's a dial. Two percent means: build, borrow, plant, *move* — the future gets the subsidy, and the leak is how it's paid for. Maybe that's even right. But you were enrolled without a signature, the rate is on nothing you ever signed —

» and the first rule of this trade is that the customer's entitled to see the diagram. `[beat]` Now you have.

Mind the toolbox on the way out. Same time next crisis.

[VISUAL: the toolbox clicks shut; the tap in the background, still dripping, exactly on the beat of the cut to black]

## Flags for the user

- **Pengő figures** — verified 2026-07-08: prices doubling ~every 15 hours at the July 1946 peak; monthly inflation ~4.19×10¹⁶%; ended by the forint, 1 Aug 1946; 100 quintillion (10²⁰) pengő note issued. One caution: a 10²¹ note (one milliard b.-pengő) was *printed but never circulated* — the script's "largest denomination ever put in a human hand" is worded to stay true; keep that wording. "Paid daily, sometimes twice daily" and the "sundown" saying — the saying is sourced (Planet Banknote et al.); the twice-daily pay detail is common in accounts but verify before a card.
- **Keynes quote** — "By a continuing process of inflation, governments can confiscate, secretly and unobserved, an important part of the wealth of their citizens" — *The Economic Consequences of the Peace* (1919), ch. VI; high confidence, flagged in-voice ("his words, flagged for the card"). Pull verbatim from the book before the card. Note the surrounding passage attributes the insight to Lenin; the script's "approvingly of the insight" is the safe characterization — don't turn it into "Keynes endorsed inflation."
- **"Loses roughly a fifth over a decade"** — that's ~2.2%/yr compounded; at exactly 2% it's ~18%. "Roughly a fifth" is safe; a card should show the compounding math.
- **Cash-holdings regressivity** — the script says "flag that for the researchers; the studies exist." They do (unbanked/low-income households hold disproportionate cash; inflation-incidence literature) — pull one named study (e.g., Fed/ECB distributional inflation papers) before the card.
- **NZ 2% origin** — flagged hard in-voice ("almost too good — verify"). The record: New Zealand's RBNZ Act 1989 / 1990 PTA set the first formal target (initially 0–2%), and the popular origin story traces the number to an off-hand remark by then-finance-minister-adjacent officials (the Roger Douglas TV interview anecdote, later described by RBNZ's Don Brash). Verify the specific anecdote and attribute carefully; the script's "roughly as a round number floated in a television interview" survives the honest version, but the card needs names and dates.
- **Deflation section** — 1930s debt-deflation and Japan characterizations are standard macro; keep at prose altitude. If a card is added, cite Fisher (1933) for debt-deflation and be careful with Japan dates ("decades" is safe).
- **"Every major central bank targets ~2%"** — true of the Fed, ECB, BoE, BoJ, others (some as ranges/medium-term). Safe as prose; a card listing banks should check each's current formulation.
- **"Considerably faster in recent years"** — deliberately unnumbered; if the edit wants the 2021–23 inflation spike on a card, pull CPI figures for the target market's country at that time.
