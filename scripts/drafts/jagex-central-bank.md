# Jagex Is Running a Central Bank — Outline

**Working title:** Jagex Is Running a Central Bank *(alt: "The Best-Run Economy on Earth Is Fake")*
**Thesis (one arguable sentence):** The Grand Exchange is a managed macroeconomy wearing a convenience feature's clothes — Jagex mints, taxes, burns, and imposes capital controls exactly like a central bank, and that unglamorous monetary work, invisible until it fails, is why OSRS dodged the hyperinflation that ate every other MMO economy.
**Target runtime:** ~14–15 min (~2,100–2,200 words)
**Voice dial:** Medium-high. The register is Bloomberg-deadpan: financial-news gravity applied to goblin currency. Cosmic zoom available at the close (money as consensual fiction). The Venezuela section demands the tonal downshift — it's real people, played straight.
**Facts:** Verified 2026-07-03 → `research/osrs-facts.md`. Time-sensitive numbers (tax rate, sink totals, bond price) must be re-checked at recording.

- The item sink is quantified: **~5.6 trillion coins of items deleted**, itemized — 399 twisted bows, 5,400 spectral shields, 253,000 occult necklaces. Buyback-and-burn, on the public record.

## Tension / the question

Your bank tab has roughly held its value for a decade. Sit that fact next to the rest of the genre. Diablo III's auction house collapsed into farce and was executed by Blizzard. WoW's gold has inflated until raid consumables cost more than epics did a decade ago `[VERIFY: soften to qualitative unless sourced]`. Every MMO economy in history trends the same direction — more money printed, everything worth less — because every MMO has the same disease: the world creates money out of nothing, forever, and never takes it back.

OSRS has the disease worse than almost anyone. Twenty-five years of alchs. And the patient is fine. Somebody is doing something.

**Open loop:** there's a country — a real one, with a flag and a seat at the UN — whose citizens concluded that this game's currency was more trustworthy than their own. Hold it for the turn.

## Thesis, stated

The claim: the Grand Exchange is a central bank wearing a convenience feature's clothes. Jagex mints money, taxes transactions, burns the proceeds, imposes capital controls, and operates an official foreign-exchange window to the pound sterling. Nobody calls it monetary policy because it arrived dressed as quality-of-life. It is monetary policy, it has rate hikes and open-market operations, and it's the most underrated design work in the game.

## Body — escalating sections

### Section 1 — The mint (where money comes from)
Gold in an MMO is printed, ex nihilo. Every high alch is a tiny act of quantitative easing; every boss gp drop, every shop sale, every coin stack in a gorilla's pocket is new money entering the world. Multiply by ~100,000 concurrent players, 24/7, for decades. The inflationary pressure is structural and permanent — the world *is* a money printer with a fantasy skin. This is the disease every MMO is born with, and most designers treat it as background noise until prices are unrecognizable.

`[VISUAL: alch animation looping with a running "new gp created" counter; zoom out to hundreds of players alching simultaneously]`

### Section 2 — The burn (where money goes to die)
The unglamorous ledger of sinks, escalating from quaint to industrial: shop purchases, construction (the entire Construction skill as a gold incinerator with furniture), Barrows repairs, sawmill fees, Death's Coffer — which pays you a 5% bonus to feed it items, a sink with a loyalty program. Banned accounts as wealth deletion.

Then the modern era. December 2021: a 1% tax on GE sales, gold removed from existence. May 2025: raised to 2%. That is a rate hike — same instrument, same purpose, same vocabulary as the real thing; Jagex's own newspost reasons about it in explicitly economic terms `[VERIFY: quote the blog's actual wording]`. And the tax revenue partly funds the item sink: the bank buys raid uniques, GWD gear, and Nightmare drops off the open market and incinerates them to hold prices up — a buyback-and-burn program. Five point six trillion coins' worth so far. The furnace from the cold open, revealed as policy.

`[VISUAL: the sink ledger scrolling — 5,400 spectral shields, 253,000 occult necklaces, 399 twisted bows; tax rate timeline 1% → 2%]`

### Section 3 — Capital controls and the border crossing
Buy limits: every item capped per 4 hours, with connected limits across variants (all prayer potion doses share one allowance) — anti-cornering rules, the same reason real markets have position limits. Jagex explicitly reserves the right to intervene against manipulation. And then the border: bonds, introduced March 2015, the only object in the game legally purchasable with real money — an official exchange window between sterling and gp, currently clearing around 11.6 million coins `[re-check at recording]`. Every economy with a strong currency and a black market eventually opens an official forex desk to compete with the smugglers. So did this one.

Cap the section with the founding document: the Grand Exchange itself was *polled*. February 2015, it passed at 76.3% — one and a third points above the threshold. The central bank was voted into existence by the citizens it would govern, which is more democratic legitimacy than any real central bank has ever had.

## The turn — the shadow economy

The strongest objection: Jagex doesn't control the money supply. The botnet does. Industrial gold farming runs the mint at scales no design intended; RWT sites run a black-market exchange rate that undercuts the official bond window; entire commodity prices (runes, ores) are set by software pretending to be people. On this reading the "central bank" is a night watchman — the counterfeiters print faster than the treasury.

And the case study is devastating, so play it completely straight (tonal downshift, no jokes): Venezuela, mid-2010s. The bolivar hyperinflates until salaries evaporate between morning and evening. Thousands of Venezuelans discover that killing green dragons in a 2007 video game produces a currency that holds value better than their own — NPR's Planet Money covered it — and gold farming becomes, for some families, the stable income. Then Jagex nerfs the Revenant caves, a routine balance patch that lands, in Caracas, as a sanctions event. A fake economy's central bank making decisions with real humanitarian weight, in the dark, with no mandate for it.

**Response, two moves, then a standing concession:**
1. Every real central bank faces exactly this — counterfeiting, black markets, capital flight. The Fed doesn't control the money supply either; it *manages* it, imperfectly, against adversaries. The tools are identical because the problem is identical. Twenty years of rough price stability *despite* industrial botting is the policy layer succeeding under fire, and the Venezuela story, read again, is the strongest evidence in the video: when a real central bank and a fake one competed for the same citizens' trust, the fake one won.
2. Honest concession, left standing: parts of the balance sheet are dirty. Staple prices genuinely are bot-set, everyone knows it, and the community has long suspected — this is interpretation, label it as suspicion, never fact — that tolerated botting flatters the game's population and revenue numbers. A central bank that quietly benefits from its counterfeiters has a conflict of interest it has never fully answered.

## Synthesis

Players experience the economy as weather — prices simply *are*, like rain. But it's gardened, continuously, by the least glamorous designers at Jagex, whose work is invisible except in the one scenario where it fails. Nobody makes a montage of inflation that didn't happen.

Zoom out for the close. A gp is a number that means something only because a hundred thousand strangers agree it does — which is also, awkwardly, the entire mechanism behind the pound in your pocket. We sort economies into real and fake, and the Venezuela story quietly breaks the sorting: the distinction that actually matters isn't which economy is real, it's which one is *kept* — tended, defended, taken seriously by its custodians. *(Check that construction at prose stage — rephrase to avoid the negative-parallelism shape.)*

Candidate closing move: return to the furnace. Somewhere in Gielinor the 400th twisted bow is about to go in, purchased at fair market price with tax money, incinerated so that yours stays worth something. Sincere beat: the number in your bank tab is a promise, and someone is keeping it. Undercut: *"Anyway. Sell your bond before the next rate decision."*
