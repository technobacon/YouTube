# Jagex Is Running a Central Bank — Full Script

**Thesis:** The Grand Exchange is a managed macroeconomy wearing a convenience feature's clothes — Jagex mints, taxes, burns, and imposes capital controls exactly like a central bank, and that invisible monetary work is why OSRS dodged the hyperinflation that ate every other MMO economy.
**Runtime:** ~16 minutes (~2,400 words)
**Voice dial:** Bloomberg-deadpan exurb1a — financial-news gravity applied to goblin currency. Venezuela section played completely straight. Cosmic zoom at the close.
**Facts:** Verified 2026-07-03 → `research/osrs-facts.md`. Tax re-verified as 2% (unchanged since May 2025) as of July 2026. Snapshot numbers flagged for refresh at recording.

---

Since December 2021, the people who run Old School RuneScape have bought, at fair market price, three hundred and ninety-nine twisted bows — the most coveted weapon in the game, five hundred and eighty-seven billion coins' worth — and destroyed them. No boss dropped better loot because of this. No player received anything. The bows went in, and they stopped existing, and the money that bought them had already stopped existing, because it was tax revenue, and the tax revenue gets deleted too.

If a player did this, you'd stage an intervention. But it's the bank. And today I want to explain why this furnace — permanently lit, entirely public, almost never discussed — might be the most important design work Jagex has done in five years. Because the Grand Exchange, which you think of as a convenience feature with eight slots, is a central bank. It has a mint. It has a burn. It has capital controls, a foreign exchange desk, and — as of last year — rate hikes.

---

Here's the fact to hold onto for the next fifteen minutes: your bank tab has roughly kept its value for a decade. If OSRS is the only game you've played seriously, you might not know how strange that is. Virtual economies die, and they die the same way every time. Diablo III opened a real-money auction house so corrosive that Blizzard executed it in public. World of Warcraft's gold buys less every expansion, forever, by design and by accident at once. Every MMO is born with the same congenital disease: the world creates money from nothing, constantly, and takes almost none of it back. Every goblin with a coin pouch is inflation.

OSRS has the disease worse than nearly anyone. Twenty-five years of high alchemy. A hundred thousand concurrent players, printing around the clock. And the patient is fine. That does not happen by luck.

There's also a country — a real one, with a flag, a UN seat, and at one point the largest proven oil reserves on Earth — whose citizens looked at their own national currency, looked at this game's currency, and chose the goblins. We'll get there.

---

So, the claim. The Grand Exchange is a managed macroeconomy dressed as a quality-of-life feature. Jagex mints, taxes, burns, restricts, and exchanges — the full central-banking toolkit, live since 2015, escalating since 2021. Nobody calls it monetary policy because it arrived wearing a trading interface. Watch the tools one at a time, and then tell me what else you'd call it.

---

Tool one. The mint.

In Gielinor, money is created the way money is created everywhere: by declaring it. Cast High Alchemy on a rune platebody and coins appear that did not exist one tick earlier. Nobody paid you. No wealth moved from one pocket to another. The universe simply agreed to pretend, and the universe has been agreeing to pretend, thousands of times a minute, since 2001. This is legal. This is, in fact, the design. Every gp drop, every shop payout, every alch is the mint running.

Real economies argue about whether the money supply should grow by four percent a year. Gielinor's grows every time anyone, anywhere, does nearly anything. A world of infinite monsters is a world of infinite money, and infinite money is worth fuck all. That's the whole problem, stated once. Everything else in this video is somebody trying to solve it.

Tool two. The burn.

The other half of the ledger, the half nobody screenshots. Start with the quaint sinks: shops. Barrows repairs. The sawmill. An entire skill — Construction — whose gameplay loop is paying to build the same mahogany table forever, which is to say: an incinerator with furniture. Death's Coffer, which offers a five percent bonus for feeding it your items — a garbage disposal with a loyalty scheme. And banned accounts, whose wealth exits the universe with them, meaning every bot ban is also, technically, deflationary policy.

Then it got serious. December 2021: a one percent tax on Grand Exchange sales. Seller pays, gold deleted. May 2025: the rate doubles to two percent. I want to be precise about what that is. That is a rate hike. Same instrument, same purpose, same press-release cadence as the real thing — Jagex's own newspost talks about removing gold from circulation, which is a sentence from a treasury briefing that has somehow ended up in patch notes. And look at the exemptions, because that's where it stops being deniable: bread is exempt. Lobster is exempt. Mind runes, bronze arrows, the hammer. The bank does not tax staple goods. Somewhere in Cambridge, a designer thought about regressive taxation in the goblin economy, and adjusted for it.

A slice of that revenue funds the furnace from the cold open. The item sink: the bank takes tax money, buys designated items on the open market — raid uniques, God Wars gear, Nightmare drops — and deletes them, to hold prices up and keep old content worth doing. A buyback-and-burn program, publicly itemized: five point six trillion coins' worth so far. When the price of a spectral spirit shield sags, the bank eats spectral spirit shields — five thousand four hundred to date — until it stops sagging. Alan Greenspan never had tools this direct.

One more detail, and it's the one that would make a constitutional lawyer sit up straight: the two percent hike shipped unpolled. The players voted the Grand Exchange into existence — hold that thought — but the rate decision came down from on high, no referendum. Which is, for better or worse, exactly how your species runs it out here too: central banks get created democratically, and then they operate independently, because no electorate in recorded history has ever voted for the medicine.

Tool three. Capital controls.

Every item on the Exchange has a buy limit — so many per four hours — and the limits are connected across variants: every dose of prayer potion draws from a single allowance, so you can't dodge the cap by buying the same liquid in different bottles. This is anti-cornering law. Real exchanges call them position limits, and they exist for the same reason these do: because somewhere out there is a man with a spreadsheet who would happily own every saradomin brew in the world by Thursday. Jagex also reserves the right to intervene directly against price manipulation, a power which, on a real exchange, is simply called being the regulator.

Tool four. The border.

In March 2015, Jagex introduced bonds — the only object in the game you are permitted to buy with real money and sell for gold. Consider what that actually is: an official exchange window between the pound sterling and the gp, with a floating market rate, currently clearing somewhere around eleven and a half million coins. Every country with a weak currency and a strong black market eventually opens a legal forex desk to compete with its own smugglers, and it does it for one reason: the smugglers were winning. So did this one.

And the founding document. The Grand Exchange arrived by ballot. February 2015, put to the whole electorate, passed at 76.3 percent — one and a third points above the threshold. The central bank of Gielinor has more democratic legitimacy than the Bank of England, which was founded in 1694 to fund a war against France and asked nobody.

Alright. The objection. It's the strongest one available, so it gets the full floor: Jagex doesn't control this money supply. The botnet does.

At any given hour, some meaningful fraction of the accounts online are not people. They're software, farming at industrial scale, and they are the real operators of the mint — runes, ores, dragon bones, entire commodity categories priced by scripts pretending to be teenagers. Beneath the official bond window runs a black-market exchange rate that undercuts it. On this reading, the central bank is a night watchman in a nice hat. The counterfeiters print faster than the treasury, and the treasury's grip is theater.

And the strongest version of this argument has real people in it, so I'm going to tell it straight.

[VISUAL: tonal downshift — plain footage, music out]

Through the middle of the 2010s, Venezuela's economy collapsed. Hyperinflation hit the bolivar so hard that a salary could lose its meaning between earning it and spending it. And Venezuelans — NPR's Planet Money put the estimate at 1.8 million who have played for money at some point — discovered that killing green dragons in a 2007 video game produced a more reliable store of value than their own national currency. A few dollars a day; eight on a good one. Rent, in places. When the Venezuelan grid failed in 2019, you could see the blackout from inside the game — player counts sagging as a real country went dark. There was a war over it, an actual one: a Venezuelan coalition against an established Western clan for control of the Revenant caves, a week of fighting in early 2020, and the Venezuelans took the territory. Then Jagex nerfed the caves. A balance patch. Routine. Except that in Caracas it landed as a pay cut, issued by a foreign power that owes you nothing and doesn't know your name. Jagex never said whether it was aimed at anyone. It didn't need to be, to land like it was.

So the objection stands there, fully armed: what kind of central bank presides over that?

Every real one. That's the answer, and I mean it precisely, so stay with me. Counterfeiting it can't stop. Black markets it can't close. Informal labor absorbing the world's desperate. Policy decisions that land hardest on people the policymakers will never meet. That is the job. The Federal Reserve doesn't control the money supply either — it manages it, imperfectly, against adversaries, forever. The realistic ask of a central bank was always modest: that the currency still works on Tuesday. And by that measure, read the scoreboard again. Two decades of rough price stability, under industrial counterfeiting pressure, while an actual sovereign currency — with an army, with oil — failed the same test so badly that its citizens migrated their labor into this one. When a real central bank and a fake one competed for the trust of the same human beings, the fake one won. That's the Venezuela story, read a second time, and it's the strongest evidence in this video.

But the concession stays on the table, because it's real. Parts of this balance sheet are dirty. Staple prices genuinely are bot-set — everyone knows the price of a nature rune has a botnet's fingerprints on it. And the community has suspected for years — and this is suspicion, I can't prove it and neither can they — that the tolerance for botting sits exactly where the population graphs look best. A central bank that benefits from its own counterfeiters has a conflict of interest it has never had to answer. Real ones have those too. It's the least flattering part of the resemblance.

You experience this economy as weather. Prices simply are — the whip costs what it costs, the bond costs what it costs, and all of it feels as natural as rain. It isn't. It's gardened, continuously, invisibly, by the least glamorous designers at Jagex, whose finest work is indistinguishable from nothing happening. Nobody makes a montage of the inflation that didn't occur. There is no clip of the crash that got prevented. The entire discipline is a rounding error right up until the day it fails, and then it's the only thing anyone has ever cared about.

And a gp, in the end, is a number that means something because a hundred thousand strangers agree that it does — which is also, and I'm sorry to bring this up, the entire mechanism behind the currency in your actual wallet. The pound hasn't been backed by gold since 1931. Since then it's been backed by, roughly speaking, mutual agreement and a building on Threadneedle Street. We like to sort economies into real ones and fake ones. The Venezuela story breaks the sorting. An economy turns out to be exactly as real as the institution keeping its promises — and some of those promises are kept in Threadneedle Street, and some are kept in Cambridge, by people with fantasy names in their email signatures, burning twisted bows so that yours still means something.

Somewhere in the queue right now is bow number four hundred. It'll be bought at fair market price, with tax money, from a player who will never know the buyer was the bank. Into the furnace it goes — so that the number in your bank tab stays a promise, instead of becoming a joke.

Anyway. Sell your bond before the next rate decision.
