# Sisyphus Would Have Loved Old School RuneScape

**Working title:** Sisyphus Would Have Loved Old School RuneScape *(strong as-is: standalone, contrarian-adjacent, promises a reframe)*
**Thesis (one arguable sentence):** OSRS is the most honest game ever made about what games are — it refuses to disguise its own pointlessness, quantifies the futility down to the XP point before you start climbing, and that honesty (which every design orthodoxy says should have killed it) is exactly why it works.
**Target runtime:** ~13–15 min (~2,000–2,200 words)
**Voice dial:** FULL EXPERIMENT. This is the most exurb1a-forward video the channel has attempted — cosmic register at the hook, the turn, and the close. The spine underneath stays rigorous: real numbers, real design history, real psychology in the counterargument. If this video works, the pivot works.

**Angles considered and rejected:**
- "The grind is secretly good" — worn out, half of game-design YouTube has made it, and it's a defense rather than a claim.
- "OSRS players empirically prove Camus right" — true and it stays in the video as Section 3, but as a thesis it's an observation nobody can disagree with, so it can't carry an argument.

Futile labor, forever, with the futility fully visible. That was the whole horror: he *knows*.

## Tension / the question

Grinding is supposed to be the flaw. It's the thing reviewers apologize for, the word "grindy" is a pejorative, and the entire modern MMO industry has spent twenty years sanding it away — leveling nerfs, catch-up mechanics, boost tokens you can buy with real money. Games now sell you *relief from the boulder they built*.

OSRS watched all of that happen and froze itself in 2007 instead. By the orthodoxy, it should be a museum piece. It is, instead, embarrassingly alive. **[VERIFY: current concurrents, OSRS vs RS3 comparison]**

**Open loop to plant here and pay off in Section 3:** there's one skill in this game that produces almost nothing, rewards you worse per hour than anything else, and is shaped — literally, architecturally — like the myth. Hold agility. Don't name it yet beyond a tease.

## Thesis, stated

Every game is a boulder. Every progress bar in every game ever made is futile labor dressed up as purpose — the loot decays, the servers close, none of it was ever going anywhere. Most games handle this by lying about it: story wrappers, season passes, the treadmill hidden behind a curtain. OSRS hands you the whole mountain in advance. 13,034,431 experience to 99. The wiki will tell you the exact hours of your sentence before you serve them. The claim: that refusal to lie is the design achievement — and what players do with that visible futility is a twenty-year accidental rerun of Camus's answer to the absurd.

### Section 3 — What players do at the summit (the proof)
Pay off the open loop first: **agility**. Worst XP rates in the game, reward is run energy, and the content is — architecturally — a *circle*. A lap that returns you to the tile you started on, thousands of times, producing nothing. The purest boulder in the game. And people love-hate it into 99 constantly. **[VERIFY: current agility rates; note the run energy rework changed its value — check date before referencing]**

Then escalate to the real behavioral evidence. You max. Fireworks, cape, forum post. If the grind were a cost — the Skinner-model prediction — this is where you log off forever, free at last. What actually happens: ironman mode. Hardcore. Ultimate. One-defence pures, chunk-locked accounts, snowflake restrictions. Leagues — Jagex now periodically offers everyone a fresh boulder and it produces the biggest player spikes of the year **[VERIFY: Leagues concurrency records]**. Players who reach the summit respond by building steeper mountains with worse weather, on purpose, for fun.

## The turn — steelman the counterargument

The Skinner box reading, taken seriously and by name. B.F. Skinner's variable-ratio reinforcement schedules are real, replicated psychology, and an OSRS drop table is *literally one* — a 1/5,000 pet is a slot machine with a weeks-long pull. The WHO put gaming disorder in the ICD-11 **[VERIFY: adoption 2019, in effect 2022]**. On this reading, Camus is the wrong philosopher entirely; the right one is a man with a pigeon and a lever, and "one must imagine Sisyphus happy" is precisely the sentence a hijacked dopamine system would produce. Give this real weight — the community's own "I regret my playtime" posts exist and are not jokes.

The response, in two moves:
1. **The model mispredicts.** Reinforcement-seeking can't explain ironman mode — a mode whose entire feature set is "the same rewards, but slower and with no help." It can't explain maxed players re-rolling to zero. The lever-pigeon doesn't ask for a heavier lever.
2. **Compulsion hides from itself; this community does the opposite.** Camus's whole distinction is *lucidity* — Sisyphus is the absurd hero because he knows. OSRS culture is the most self-aware in gaming: the memes, the "why do we play this game" threads, a wiki that quantifies the pointlessness to the decimal. A Skinner pigeon does not make memes about the lever.

Then the honest concession, kept: lucidity is a defense, and it isn't armor. Some people's boulders roll over them. Both things are true, in different players, on the same slope. Leave that friction in — don't resolve it.

## Synthesis

Zoom out; the tender turn. The gods made one design error: they assumed the summit was the point, so deleting the summit made hell. Humans, given the same setup voluntarily, revealed what the climb actually buys — a place to *be* while life happens. The lap as ritual. The podcast hours. Clan chat full of people pushing adjacent boulders, close enough to talk. A world where effort maps to result linearly, every time, forever — the one slope in your life that never moves under your feet. The game never once claims it means anything. Players bring the meaning with them, which is the entire absurdist project, executed weekly by people who'd never call it that.

End on sincerity, undercut once. Candidate closing lines: Camus insisted we must *imagine* Sisyphus happy. No imagination required anymore — he's at the rooftop course, two hundred laps into the evening, four levels from a cape he'll wear for a week before starting an ironman. And he'd like you to get off his tile.
