# Sisyphus Would Have Loved Old School RuneScape — Full Script

**Thesis:** OSRS is the most honest game ever made about what games are — it quantifies its own futility up front, and that honesty is the design achievement.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,100 words)
**Voice dial:** Full exurb1a experiment. Analysis spine stays rigorous; register goes cosmic at the hook, the turn, and the close.
**Facts:** All stats verified in `research/osrs-facts.md`, 2026-07-03. Remaining soft spots flagged at the bottom.

---

Somewhere around the eighth century BC, the Greeks needed to invent a punishment for the worst crime anyone had committed so far. A king called Sisyphus had cheated death. Twice, actually. The first time he chained up Thanatos, the god of death himself, and for a while nobody on Earth could die, which caused administrative problems. The second time he talked his way out of the underworld through a loophole involving his own funeral arrangements. So the gods wanted something special for this man. Fire wouldn't do; fire ends. The eagle-eats-your-liver-daily arrangement was taken. What they settled on, in the end, was a boulder and a hill. Push the boulder to the top. Watch it roll back down. Walk down after it. Begin again. Forever.

[VISUAL: classical paintings of Sisyphus, slow pans, deadpan captions in the channel style]

And the refined part, because the Greeks were artists about cruelty, is that he would know. No story to follow. No hope to slowly lose, because none would be issued to begin with. Complete information about the futility of your own labor, handed over on day one. That was the sentence. The knowing was the sentence.

[VISUAL: hard cut — Seers' Village rooftop course at night. XP counter on screen. Lap count ticking.]

This man has been running the same rooftop circuit for four hundred hours. He knows exactly how many laps remain, because there's a spreadsheet, and he made it himself, and he's proud of it. He's listening to a podcast about the fall of Rome. And by every measure we know how to take, he is doing fine. Better than fine. He renewed early.

Somewhere between ancient Greece and last Tuesday, humanity's worst imaginable punishment became a subscription service. I want to talk about why. Because I don't think it's an accident, and I don't think it's a joke, and I'm not convinced it's a mental illness either, though we're going to have to discuss that possibility properly before the end. I think Old School RuneScape might be the most honest game ever made.

[VISUAL: title card]

---

Grinding is supposed to be the flaw. Reviews apologize for it. "Grindy" is a pejorative in every language gamers speak, and the modern games industry has spent twenty years treating player effort as a disease to be managed. World of Warcraft compresses its own levels and sells you the skip for the price of a new game. Mobile games invented waiting purely so they could charge you to delete it. And understand what that business model admits: the boulder is still in every one of those games, top to bottom. They charge extra for the curtain.

In 2013, four hundred and forty-nine thousand people voted to resurrect a version of RuneScape restored from a 2007 backup. The slow version. The one with the boulder in plain view. Jagex made it members-only at launch, which means nearly half a million people specifically requested the privilege of paying rent on a six-year-old hill. Thirteen years later, that museum piece broke its all-time concurrent record — just over 240,000 players — on what I need to stress was a completely unremarkable Sunday in August. No update that weekend. No event. A quarter of a million people woke up and chose the hill.

[VISUAL: player count chart with the 3 Aug 2025 peak marked; cut against a WoW boost store page]

And there's one skill in this game that I think explains the entire phenomenon. We'll get to it. For now you should know that it has some of the worst experience rates in the game, that its reward for two decades was the ability to run slightly longer before stopping, and that its content is, literally and architecturally, a circle.

---

So here's the actual claim. Every game you have ever played is a boulder. Every progress bar ever shipped is futile labor wearing a little hat. The loot stops mattering, the servers close eventually, none of it was ever going anywhere — and this is as true of a raid completion as it is of a chess rating or, if we're being thorough today, a career. Most games handle this the way most people handle it: with set dressing. A story wrapper. A season that "ends," so the treadmill can feel like weather.

Old School RuneScape hands you the mountain up front. Thirteen million, thirty-four thousand, four hundred and thirty-one experience. That's one 99. The client shows you the full number any time you hover a skill, and the community wiki will quote your remaining sentence in hours, to one decimal place, before you've served a minute of it. My argument, this whole video, is that the refusal to lie is the design achievement. Everything people love about this game grows out of the honesty.

[VISUAL: skill hover tooltip; the wiki's "Efficient hours played" tables scrolling past]

---

Specify the boulder, then. Experience in this game is exponential. The curve doubles roughly every seven levels, which produces the most quoted piece of arithmetic in the community: level 92 is the halfway point to 99. You can be level 92 — a number that sounds like an ending, a number the game gave you a fireworks animation for — and be standing at base camp with exactly half the climb above you. The game does nothing to soften this. No renumbering, no level squish, no expansion that resets the scale so the wall looks closer. There's a number, and yesterday it was smaller. That is the entire relationship.

Which sounds like it should repel people, so here's why it does the opposite. The moment a game admits the climb is the whole product, a very modern anxiety dies. Destination games manufacture guilt out of travel. Level wrong and you've wasted the evening. Skip a season and you're behind. Log off and the meta moves without you. OSRS never promised arrival, so there is nothing to be late for. No such thing as caught up. Once the game stops pretending the boulder is temporary, the boulder stops hurting. What's left is the pushing. And the pushing, it turns out, was the game.

---

Now. Agility.

[VISUAL: overhead shot of the Seers' rooftop course; trace the lap as one clean glowing loop]

An agility course is a circle. You run the lap and you arrive back at the tile you started on, slightly better at running laps. Nothing is produced. Nothing is gathered. For most of the skill's existence, its entire reward was run energy — the ability to keep jogging marginally longer before your legs file a complaint. The community has hated it, loudly, for over twenty years, and Jagex spent a good chunk of 2024 rebalancing run energy essentially to make the prize less insulting.

There are people with two hundred million experience in this skill. Two hundred million. That's the cap, by the way. The game physically prevents you from loving agility harder than that. Two hundred million experience of running in circles, in fucking agility, where the reward for mastering the circle is that the circle asks slightly less of you.

I'm describing the myth. You see it, right? The Greeks conceived of this as the ceiling of divine cruelty, the one thing no soul could endure. Jagex shipped it as content, and the reception was warm.

But agility is only the purest sample. The real evidence, the part I'd show a researcher from another field, is what players do when they finish. Because people do finish. Max cape day. Fireworks, the clan gathered at Mac's, the screenshot that goes on the wall. And if the grind were a cost — which is the central assumption of every "grind is bad design" take ever written — this is the moment of release. Debt paid. Free at last. Log off.

What actually happens is the man makes an ironman.

Same mountain, and you surrender your tools at the door. No trading, no Grand Exchange, no help from another living soul. Thousands of finished players choose this. Some finish the ironman and make a hardcore one, where a single death demotes the account permanently. There are group modes, one-defence builds, accounts locked to a single chunk of the map from level 3, forever. And every year or so Jagex runs Leagues, a temporary game mode where everyone's progress is wiped to zero and the boulder is factory-fresh — and Leagues launches produce some of the biggest player spikes in the game's history. The December 2024 one pushed past 230,000 concurrent. The gods gave Sisyphus his boulder free of charge. Jagex ran a poll and discovered he'd have paid.

[VISUAL: max cape fireworks → smash cut to the account creation screen, ironman icon selected]

In 1942, in occupied France, Albert Camus wrote an essay arguing that the honest response to a meaningless universe is to keep pushing anyway, eyes open, out of something like defiance. It ends with two of the most famous sentences in twentieth-century philosophy: "The struggle itself towards the heights is enough to fill a man's heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy." Old School RuneScape players read that and asked whether the struggle could be made slower, and whether there was a mode where it's also lonelier.

---

Alright. The counterargument. It deserves the strongest version I can build, partly because it might be about you personally.

In the mid-twentieth century, B.F. Skinner put pigeons in boxes and mapped how reward schedules bend behavior. The strongest bend came from variable-ratio reinforcement: rewards at random intervals, unpredictable, so the next pull is always the one. It's the schedule casinos run on. It is also, precisely and without exaggeration, a drop table. A pet at one in five thousand is a slot machine where each pull takes a minute and the session lasts six weeks. The World Health Organization added gaming disorder to its diagnostic manual in 2019. And on this reading, Camus is the wrong philosopher for this video. The right one is a man with a lever and a pigeon, and "one must imagine Sisyphus happy" is exactly the sentence the pigeon says.

I can't wave that away, and I won't try, because parts of it are simply true. The drop tables are variable-ratio engines. Jagex employs people who understand retention curves. Some fraction of the hours logged into this game are compulsive hours, and the players logging them know it.

Two problems, though. First: the pigeon model makes predictions, and the predictions miss. A reinforcement engine chases reward and abandons the lever when the pellets stop coming. Nothing in that framework requests a heavier lever. Skinner has no account of the ironman, a mode whose entire feature list is the same pellets, slower, alone. No account of a maxed player deleting every advantage to climb the identical mountain again. The pigeon presses. The pigeon does not petition the lab for hard mode.

Second — and this is the part of Camus everyone skips — his entire distinction between damnation and revolt is lucidity. Sisyphus qualifies as the absurd hero because he sees his situation completely and pushes anyway. The scorn is the victory. Compulsion, meanwhile, hides from itself; gamblers famously lie about their losses. This community does the opposite of hiding. It might be the most self-aware culture in gaming — a population whose principal art form is the meme about the game being a prison, followed immediately by the meme about renewing your membership from inside the prison. The wiki quantifies the pointlessness to one decimal place. A pigeon does not make memes about the lever.

But I want to leave the uncomfortable remainder on the table rather than tidying it away. Lucidity is a defense, and it is not armor. Some people's boulders roll backward over them. You've read the posts. The played-time command returning a number of days that suddenly means something, written by someone who is not laughing. Revolt and compulsion happen on the same slope, in identical-looking sessions, sometimes in the same person in the same week, and I don't have a clean way to split them. I'd be suspicious of anyone who claims they do.

---

So what did the gods get wrong?

They assumed the summit was the point. That's the entire engineering failure in the myth. Take a creature that wants to arrive, deny it arrival forever, and you have built hell. Sound theory. Then you watch what the climb does for an actual human on an actual Tuesday, and the theory comes apart, because the climb buys things no summit ever could.

It buys a place to be while your life happens. Four hundred hours of laps is four hundred hours of podcasts, of half-thoughts, of decompression after shifts. A rosary with experience drops. It buys a clan chat full of people pushing adjacent boulders, close enough to talk while they push — which the punishment designers of ancient Greece never considered, because company ruins a damnation. And it buys the rarest thing on offer anywhere: a corner of existence where effort converts to result at a fixed, published exchange rate, every time, forever. Nothing else you touch does that. Your job doesn't do it. Your body, eventually, stops doing it. The rooftop pays out every single lap and will keep paying until the servers go dark, and if you want to know what that guarantee is worth to people, the number was 240,000 on a random Sunday last August.

The game never once claims any of this means anything. There is no lore justification for a max cape. The meaning gets carried in by the player, from outside, nightly — which is, and I'm sorry to be the one to tell you, the entire absurdist project. Executed weekly. In a medieval clicking simulator. By a population the size of a real city, approximately none of whom would ever call it that.

Camus said one must imagine Sisyphus happy, and that word — imagine — is doing real work, because from where Camus stood, it took imagining. You don't have to imagine anything. He's at the rooftop course right now. Two hundred laps into the evening, four levels from a cape he'll wear for about a week before he starts an ironman and gives the boulder its legs back. Look at him go.

And he'd like you to get off his tile.
