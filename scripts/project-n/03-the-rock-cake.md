# The Rock Cake — Full Script (Project N, #3)

**Thesis:** The dwarven rock cake — the only food in Old School RuneScape whose function is subtraction — and the Nightmare Zone, the rented dream where it's eaten, together form the game's strangest institution: a spa where players hurt themselves into invulnerability and bore themselves into strength, re-fighting enemies they've already beaten while watching television. Optimization will walk calmly through pain, absurdity, and anesthesia if the numbers ask it to; the cake is the proof you can hold in one hand.
**Format:** Fine-dining review. A restaurant critic files on the worst establishment in Gielinor — the venue, the signature dish, the tasting notes, the clientele, and a star rating the format has to earn.
**Runtime:** ~13.5 minutes (~1,950 words)
**Voice dial:** High. The batch's comedy lead with the darkest undertow.
**Facts:** Cake provenance (RFD), guzzle mechanics, NMZ systems (absorptions, points, imbues), release timing all flagged; no numbers in prose.

## SCRIPT

The restaurant is a tent outside Yanille, the maître d' sells dreams by the hour, and the tasting menu is one item long. `[beat]` I have eaten here, by conservative estimate, four hundred times. This is my review.

[VISUAL: elegant serif review-column mockup over footage of the Nightmare Zone tent; a pull-quote fades in: "…a mistake, served chilled…"]

Some establishing notes for readers unfamiliar with the venue. The Nightmare Zone is a business — an actual, in-fiction business, run by a man named Dominic Onion, which I remind you is a *food name*, who has set up outside the city walls and will, for a fee, put you to sleep. In the dream, you fight. Specifically — and this is the detail on which the whole review turns — you fight bosses you have *already beaten*. The dream is stocked exclusively from your own past: quest monsters you finished years ago, resurrected as harmless projections, on a loop, forever, while your body stands in a tent.

» It's a restaurant where the only thing on the menu is your own biography, `[beat]` performed by understudies, `[beat]` for a crowd of one who isn't watching.

Because — second establishing note — nobody watches. The Nightmare Zone is this game's most famously *ignored* activity: the customers queue up, fall asleep, and then alt-tab to something else. Netflix. Homework. A different video essay, possibly this one. The house knows. The house is *built* on it. But I'm ahead of myself, because you can't understand the dining room until you've met the signature dish.

---

**The dish. Course one of one: the dwarven rock cake.**

[VISUAL: the cake, presented on a critic's white plate, shot like a magazine spread; it is visibly a rock]

Provenance first, as with any great plate. The cake comes from a quest about a banquet — a long, beloved shaggy-dog epic in which you cater for a committee — and the dwarven leg of it involves a recipe whose ingredients I won't spoil and whose result is this: a cake with the density of geology and the approximate warmth of a customs form. The dwarves, to their credit, consider it cuisine. The item, to its credit, is honest — pick it up carelessly and it hurts you. `[beat]` Touching it. Hurts you. The plate comes with a workplace injury.

And then there's the instruction. Every food in this game says *Eat*. Thousands of items, one gentle verb, twenty years. This one says — and I want you to hear the menu saying it —

*Guzzle.*

`[slow]` Guzzle is a verb you use for poison, or for something a dog got into. No chef on Earth puts it on a menu. And the mechanical truth under the word is the entire reason this review exists: the rock cake is the only food in Old School RuneScape whose function is *subtraction*. You bite down, and your hitpoints go down, and you bite down again, and they go down again, and the diner — this is the part where my tasting notes just say *what* in increasingly large letters — the diner does this *on purpose*, rhythmically, dozens of times, driving their own health down, down, down, until it rests at one. `[beat]` One hitpoint. The width of a paper cut from nonexistence. And then, holding themselves there, at the absolute cliff-edge of being a character at all, they press the cake to their lips like a fucking harmonica, `[beat]` and keep it there, all night.

Tasting notes: chalk, regret, efficiency. Pairs well with whatever you're actually watching.

---

**Now the recipe — the *why* — because I've reviewed a lot of strange kitchens and this preparation is the strangest thing I've ever had explained to me by a sommelier in a nightmare.**

Alongside the cake, the diner drinks absorption potions — the house specialty, purchased with the house's own points, a currency we'll get to. Absorptions don't heal. Healing is for the daytime economy. Absorptions *pre-eat* damage: they wrap you in a thick buffer of borrowed hitpoints that soak everything the dream throws, while your real hitpoints — the one hitpoint, the paper cut — sit untouched underneath.

And here's why the cake and the potion together constitute, I'll just say it, one of the most elegant exploits of a rules system I've ever reviewed. In this game, many things scale with your *current* hitpoints or interact with your real health bar — and a certain class of defensive math gets very quiet when there's almost nothing left to defend. Meanwhile the dream's damage lands entirely on the absorption buffer. Run the two rules side by side and the kitchen has produced something genuinely deranged: `[slow]` a creature that cannot meaningfully be hurt *because it is almost dead*. Maximum fragility, arranged just so, becomes functional invulnerability. The safest diner in Gielinor tonight is the one at one hitpoint, `[beat]` and he has been safe like that, motionless, harmonica to his lips, for six hours.

[VISUAL: the classic NMZ setup shot — 1 HP, absorption counter fat and green, a boss flailing uselessly; frame it in the review column like a food photo]

The chef did not intend this dish. I checked — the ingredients were introduced separately, years apart, for unrelated reasons, and the pairing was discovered by the clientele, the way port and stilton was. It's outsider cuisine. Folk molecular gastronomy. The kitchen shipped a cake that hurts and a potion that pre-forgives, and the dining public, unsupervised, invented `[beat]` painless immortality, served chilled, one bite at a time.

---

**The clientele. Because a critic reviews the room too, and the room is the actual story.**

Who eats here? Everyone, at some point. The draw is the points — the house currency, spent mostly on *imbues*: upgrades to rings and items that this dining room dispenses more painlessly than anywhere else, plus experience rates that, for certain combat styles, at certain attention prices, out-compete almost everything. And there it is, the phrase this channel keeps circling from different videos — the attention price. The Nightmare Zone's true product is combat experience *at the lowest attention cost in the game*. The cake and the potions aren't the meal. They're the *anesthetic* — the preparation that makes the patient stable enough to safely stop paying attention. The actual transaction: you rent a dream so that your character can work the night shift `[beat]` while you go live your life in the other window.

I want to be fair to the diners, because I'm one of them. The imbues are close to mandatory for serious play; the alternative training rooms charge more attention for similar pay; the meta is the meta. Every individual at this restaurant is making a locally reasonable choice. `[slow]` It's only when the critic steps back and describes the establishment *as a whole* that the review gets cold: a room full of heroes, each at one hitpoint, each gnawing a stone cake in their sleep, each re-fighting a museum of their own defeated past on a loop that pays out precisely because they've stopped watching. `[beat]` I've reviewed prisons with more joie de vivre. And every table is booked till March.

---

**The turn, and then the rating. Because the obvious verdict — *degenerate design, shut the kitchen* — has been proposed for years, and I no longer sign it.**

The case for closure writes itself: this room deletes the game. Combat — the thing the whole system of gear and prayer and positioning was built for — reduced to a stationary man and a cake, the encounter literally unwatched, victory certified by the fact that the customer left. Jagex has circled the place with a nerf bat more than once; the community periodically demands it. And the deeper charge, the one with teeth: the Nightmare Zone is the game showing you, with unusual honesty, what a chunk of all progression actually is under the costume — numbers accruing while a human is elsewhere — and *that's* why people want it shut. Nobody likes the restaurant that serves the sauce separated.

Here's my dissent, as filed. Every game economy needs a floor — a baseline rate for the hours when a player has love for the game and nothing left for it that day. The Nightmare Zone is that floor, honestly priced and honestly labeled: it's called the *Nightmare* Zone, the proprietor is openly selling you a stupor, the menu says *Guzzle*. No dark pattern anywhere — the darkness is printed on the sign. And the diners know exactly what they're buying, which is the thing almost no attention economy will sell you at any price these days: `[slow]` permission. Permission to be present somewhere else while still, in some small ledger, becoming.

My confession, for the record, as the format requires: four hundred visits. Some of my levels were earned wide awake in beautiful places, and some were guzzled in a tent with a documentary on, and I can no longer tell you which numbers on my character are which. Nobody can. `[beat]` That's either horrifying or forgiving, and on different Tuesdays I file it differently.

---

**The rating, then.**

[VISUAL: the review column's final box; a single star stamps down]

Ambience: a tent. Service: the proprietor charges you to lose consciousness. Menu: one item, geological, hostile to the touch. The signature preparation reduces the diner to the thinnest possible sliver of existence and holds them there, numb, safe, absent, productive — and if you think that's a review of a video game minigame, `[beat]` you haven't looked around your own commute lately.

One star.

`[slow]` I'll be back tomorrow.

[VISUAL: the critic's table — the cake untouched on its white plate, the chair already empty; cut]
