# The Stronghold of Security — Full Script (Project N, #2)

**Thesis:** The Stronghold of Security is the only dungeon in gaming built as a public-health intervention: four floors themed after the horsemen of the apocalypse, doors that quiz you on password hygiene, and a cash bribe for finishing the syllabus — designed in the middle of an account-hijacking epidemic, and quietly the most honest room in the game, because it's where the game admits its most dangerous monster was never on the bestiary. It was other people.
**Format:** School field trip. The narrator is the chaperone; the viewer is the class. Buddy system enforced; worksheets distributed; one child is always missing.
**Runtime:** ~13.5 minutes (~1,950 words)
**Voice dial:** High. The comedy carrier of the batch; the landing goes tender at the bottom floor.
**Facts:** Release year, reward amounts, floor names, question mechanics all flagged; no numbers in prose.

## SCRIPT

Alright, class, listen up. Permission slips in the tray, lunches stay on the bus, and find your buddy — if you cannot see your buddy right now, raise your hand. `[beat]` One hand. Every time. We'll come back to that.

[VISUAL: a school-trip framing card — clipboard, name tags — over the Barbarian Village well; children's-museum font: "TODAY'S DESTINATION: THE APOCALYPSE"]

Today we're going down the well in the middle of Barbarian Village, into a dungeon called the Stronghold of Security, and before you groan — yes, this is an educational trip. That's the astonishing part. Everything else in this world was built to entertain you or kill you or both, and in the mid-two-thousands, in the middle of the worst crime wave this game ever suffered, somebody at Jagex built four floors of dungeon whose actual function — stated, unhidden, load-bearing in the architecture — was to teach children not to get robbed.

» It is, as far as I can tell, the only dungeon in the history of the medium constructed as a public-health intervention. `[beat]` And they themed it after the end of the world, which sounds like a strange choice for a safety course until you understand what was happening to the accounts of the people walking in.

Single file at the well. Mind the rope.

---

**Some history while we climb down, because the context is the whole exhibit.**

Picture the game's economy in that era. Items had become money — real money, the kind adults would pay for — and wherever value pools, predators arrive at the waterline. Password phishing. Fake login screens. "Free membership, just tell me your details." Keyloggers in downloads that promised gold. The game's forums and playgrounds filled with a crime that the bestiary had no entry for, because it didn't live in the game. It lived in chat windows and inboxes, and it hunted the one creature in Gielinor with no combat level: `[slow]` the person holding the mouse.

And Jagex's response — alongside the bans and the systems — was this place. A dungeon. Because they knew their audience: nobody reads a security bulletin, *especially* nobody twelve years old. But everybody opens doors.

[VISUAL: the first gate of the Vault of War; the door's dialogue box rising]

---

**Floors one and two. War, then Famine. Watch what the doors do.**

Every door in this place is locked with a question. Nothing about the game — about *you*. Will Jagex staff ever ask for your password? Should you use your RuneScape password on other websites? A stranger says he can double your money if you trust him first — what do we think, class?

`[beat]` And a room full of children who cannot be made to read a single line of quest dialogue will sit there, at a door, and answer. Because the question is *in the way*. That's the entire pedagogical technology of this dungeon: it puts the syllabus between you and the loot, one bite at a time, door after door, floor after floor, until the answers wear a groove. No pop-up. No tutorial voice. Architecture. You don't read the lesson here; you *walk through it*, which is how the oldest teaching in the world works — ask anyone who learned "hot" from a stove.

And the questions themselves are magnificent in their bleakness, when you actually listen to them, because each one is a tiny portrait of a specific betrayal that had already happened to thousands of real kids. Every door is a crime scene, `[beat]` rewritten as a quiz. Somebody in a chat room is promising your classmate free membership right now, this week, and the correct answer — class, repeat after me — is *no, fuck off.* `[beat]` The door phrases it more politely. The door has to. I don't.

Buddy check. Count of hands. `[beat]` Still one short. Moving on.

---

**Floor three, Pestilence, and here's the design argument of the trip, so worksheets out.**

Why does this work when every other form of the same lesson fails? Because — and the whole discipline of security has spent twenty years catching up to this — safety information doesn't transfer through *reading*. It transfers through *rehearsal*. The dungeon doesn't tell you about phishing; it phishes you, gently, with training wheels: door after door presents a small temptation-shaped question, you practice the refusal, and the refusal — this is the part the textbook version never gets — the refusal pays out. Progress. Depth. Treasure below. The dungeon rigs the slot machine in honesty's favor. Say the safe thing, and the world opens.

Compare the alternative universe where this is a splash screen — "TIP: never share your password!" — dismissed in a hundred milliseconds by every human who has ever seen a splash screen. Jagex knew their bulletin would lose to a chat message from a friendly stranger, because the stranger had *engagement* and the bulletin had a paragraph. So they gave the lesson what the stranger had. `[slow]` A world, a reward, and your own hand on the door.

[VISUAL: montage of doors opening down the floors, each question flashing briefly, the pace building]

---

**Floor four. Death. The bottom of the syllabus. And the bribe.**

At the end of each floor there's a chest of actual money — a modest fortune, if you're new, which is exactly who this place is for — and at the very bottom, a choice of boots. Two styles. Fancy or fighting, fashion or function, and generations of players have stood at that final cabinet agonizing over what is, mechanically, almost nothing — which I choose to read as the dungeon's last exam question, ungraded: *what kind of person are you going to be up there?* `[beat]` There is no wrong answer. There was never a wrong answer on any of these doors, in a sense — you can miss and try again forever. The dungeon has infinite patience. It was built by people who understood that the stakes upstairs were real, so the stakes down here should be zero: the one classroom in the world where failing the phishing test costs nothing at all. Fail it in your inbox instead and it costs everything you've banked. `[slow]` Practice rooms are mercy. That's the whole reason civilization builds them.

So: the game pays you — pays you, the direction of that transaction should stop you cold, the *game* pays *you* — to complete a literacy course about the game's own predators. Somebody priced a child's attention, honestly, in gold, and it worked. Class, that's the exhibit.

---

**Now the turn, and I'll deliver it the way the school inspector would.**

Did it work, though — outside the well? Because the honest ledger says: phishing did not end. The epidemic evolved, moved platforms, professionalized; account theft outlived this dungeon by decades and thrives today, upgraded, everywhere. Meanwhile the doors aged into trivia. Ask the veterans in the comments how they experience the Stronghold now: spam-click, spam-click, answer memorized by position rather than meaning, a speed bump on the way to the money, the sacred syllabus reduced to a rhythm game. And some of its questions read, today, like warnings about threats from a museum — the world moved to authenticators and token logins while the doors kept asking about chat-room strangers. By the cold measure — infections prevented per hour of design effort — nobody can even produce the number. The intervention was never measurable. It was a gesture with a floor plan.

Two things back, and then we surface. First: rote is what success looks like in this business. The multiplication table also became a rhythm game; that's what "learned" *is* — the groove wearing so deep the meaning stops needing to surface. Every veteran spam-clicking those doors is spam-clicking the *correct answers*, twenty years later, from memory. `[beat]` Show me another security bulletin that can say that. Second: the apocalypse theming, which I promised to explain. War, Famine, Pestilence, Death — you walk the four horsemen top to bottom, and I used to think it was just dungeon-flavor gothic. I don't anymore. The people who built this had watched actual twelve-year-olds lose actual years of their lives to a stranger with a fake login page — and I think the theme is the designers telling the truth at the only volume children respect. `[slow]` This is what it feels like, the theme says. When it happens to you it will not feel like a customer-service issue. It will feel like the end of the world. `[beat]` Ask anyone it happened to.

---

**Alright. Surface. Line up at the well rope. Final head count.**

[VISUAL: the class lined up in Barbarian Village dusk; the clipboard; one name tag unclaimed on the tray]

And here's where I tell you about the buddy we've been one short all trip, because you noticed, and a chaperone owes the class the truth. Everyone my age knows the missing kid. The friend whose account just — stopped. Password given away for a promise, or a keylogger from a gold site, and then the character you'd stood next to for two years belonged to someone in a chat log, and the person was still at school on Monday but the *account* was gone, and eleven-year-olds don't have the vocabulary for what was taken, so we said "hacked" and went to the cow field short one buddy.

That's who the dungeon was built for. Not you, the survivor, spam-clicking doors. Them. `[beat]` The bestiary in this game lists every monster with a spawn point. The Stronghold of Security is the one place that names the other kind — and it puts a classroom, a bribe, and infinite patient doors between that monster and the next kid down the well.

» Field trip's over. Hold your buddy's hand at the crossing. `[beat]` And never, ever tell anyone your password — the door phrases it more politely, but the door and I agree.

[VISUAL: the well at night; the worksheet blowing against it; cut]
