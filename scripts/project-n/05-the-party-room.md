# The Party Room — Full Script (Project N, #5)

**Thesis:** The Falador Party Room is the game's only machine for giving wealth away *badly on purpose* — anonymous deposits, randomized balloons, an undignified scramble — and its near-total emptiness, one city over from the frictionless perfection of the Grand Exchange, measures something the efficiency ledger can't: what it cost when giving stopped being weather and became a transaction. This is the essay that argues with the minigame-graveyard video on its own channel, and loses on the numbers, and files a dissent anyway.
**Format:** Weather forecast. A meteorologist covers the only room in the world where wealth precipitates — current conditions, how the weather system works, historical climate, the drought, and the extended outlook.
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes (~1,900 words)
**Voice dial:** High. Deadpan broadcast carrying warmth; the memorial beat is the batch's most careful minute.
**Facts:** Party Room mechanics (chest, lever fee, balloon randomization, knights), its pre-GE history, relocation lore, and memorial-party culture all flagged; no numbers in prose.

## SCRIPT

Good evening. Here's the weather.

[VISUAL: a broadcast-graphics pastiche — satellite map of Asgarnia, isobars, a suited silhouette gesturing at Falador; chyron: "GIELINOR TONIGHT — WEALTH SYSTEMS"]

Over most of the continent: clear, dry, economically settled. High pressure sitting on Varrock as usual — we'll get to Varrock. But tonight's story is a small anomaly cell over central Falador, where instruments have detected the only weather system of its kind in the world: an enclosed chamber, roughly ballroom-sized, in which — under specific and increasingly rare conditions — `[beat]` it rains money.

» The Falador Party Room is the one machine in Old School RuneScape built to give wealth away *badly on purpose*. `[beat]` And this forecast will argue that the badness is the point, that a room one city from the most efficient market in gaming history preserves the exact thing that market deleted — and that the current drought over that room measures the deletion to the decimal.

Conditions at the top of the hour: empty. `[beat]` Let's go to the mechanism.

---

**The weather system. How, precisely, does it rain?**

[VISUAL: cutaway diagram of the Party Room drawn like a storm-cell cross-section — chest at the base, updraft arrows, balloon layer at the ceiling]

A citizen — any citizen, anonymously — deposits items into a chest. The goods vaporize upward into a balloon layer that accumulates at the ceiling. Then someone pays a fee to pull a lever, and the front comes through: balloons descend over the whole room, drifting, and burst *only when popped* — by anyone. Whoever pops holds. The knights employed on the premises dance for tips throughout, which has no bearing on the meteorology and I simply refuse to cut it from the broadcast.

Now, as your meteorologist, let me catalogue what this system refuses to do, because every refusal is a design decision and the ledger-minded will be screaming by the third one. The deposit is *anonymous* — the rain doesn't credit its cloud. The distribution is *random* — balloons drift where they like; need, merit, and seniority carry no weight with weather. The collection is *undignified* — grown adventurers in end-game armor scrambling and popping like children at a fair, and the room's acoustics are pure chaos. And the transfer is *lossy* — against simply handing your wealth to a chosen person, the balloon method wastes fees, time, and precision on an outcome you can't even aim.

Four refusals. Anonymity, randomness, indignity, loss. `[slow]` Hold all four. Because every one of them has another name, and the historical climate section is where I say it.

---

**Historical climate. Before the high-pressure system.**

Rewind the charts to the early era, before the Grand Exchange existed — and if the "before" surprises you, this room is one of the oldest structures in the game, older than the market by years, a fact flagged for the researchers like everything tonight. In that climate, transferring wealth was *hard*. Selling meant standing in a bank shouting; giving meant finding, meeting, trading, hand to hand. And in that climate of friction, a folk practice emerged that the Party Room merely institutionalized: `[beat]` the drop party. A player grown rich — or, more often and more movingly, a player *leaving* — would announce a time and place, and a crowd would gather, and the wealth would be scattered into it. Deliberately. Festively. Inefficiently, in every one of our four catalogued ways.

Because look at the four refusals under the old weather. Anonymity is *modesty* — the gift that doesn't invoice you for gratitude. Randomness is *fairness of a particular kind* — the kind rain has, that falls on whoever's standing there, that can't be lobbied. Indignity is *festivity* — the scramble is a party game; the party game is the difference between receiving and *winning*. And loss — the fees, the waste, the imprecision — loss is *ceremony*, the same way a wedding is catering you could have skipped and a toast nobody needed. `[slow]` Ceremonies are inefficient on purpose. The inefficiency is the signature on the gift; it's how you can tell it from a payment.

Anthropologists have a whole literature on this — gift economies, the potlatch, wealth destroyed or scattered as a social act — and the finding that matters tonight: in most of human history, giving was an *event*, not a transfer. The Party Room is that finding, built in stone, with a balloon machine. `[beat]` A little temple to throughput-zero generosity.

Then the front moved in.

---

**The front. You can see it on the satellite: one city east.**

[VISUAL: the weather map again; a massive stable high-pressure graphic over Varrock labeled "G.E."]

The Grand Exchange. There's a whole broadcast on this channel about it — the buy limits, the taxes, the monetary policy; the central bank in a fantasy costume. Tonight it appears only as climate: the day the market opened, moving wealth between any two citizens became instant, precise, dignified, and free of ceremony. Perfect efficiency, and this forecast has *no complaints* — the market is a marvel; the channel's said so at length.

But watch the anomaly cell's readings after that day. Once transfer costs nothing, scattered wealth stops reading as festivity and starts reading as *error*. Why balloon-randomize a gift you could route exactly? Why scramble when you can list? The Party Room wasn't out-competed for anybody's hour — there's a video on this channel about mechanics that died that way, and this isn't one of them; the room never paid an hourly rate to lose. `[slow]` What the market repriced was the *meaning* of the act. Giving-as-weather became, overnight, a rounding error with confetti — and the room began the long drought it sits in tonight. Current occupancy, again: nobody. The knights dance for the furniture.

---

**Now the turn, because a forecast that romanticizes historical weather is a calendar, and you deserve journalism.**

It was always dumb. Here's the honest climate record. The advertised mega-drops were, a famous fraction of the time, lies — "huge drop party, world whatever" pulling crowds to Falador for a ceiling of junk; you sprinted across two zones for the promised millions and the balloons opened on cabbages, `[beat]` and the meteorological term for that is drizzle, and the community term is *bullshit*, and both are accurate. When the drops were real, the scramble rewarded the fastest clickers and the best positioned, which correlates with need not at all and with being a warehouse-district goblin quite strongly. And the potlatch literature I cited so warmly cuts both ways: anthropologists will tell you the great wealth-scatterings were *also* dominance displays — generosity as flex, the rich buying status with visible loss. The room's anonymity was optional, and the famous droppers rarely opted in. `[beat]` So: theater, vanity, fraud, and clicking. The graveyard video's cold standard — what does this pay per hour, and who shows up — returns a verdict of *nothing* and *nobody*, and unlike my colleague on the Tears of Guthix beat, I can't even claim the room aims its nothing somewhere profound. The rain falls on the fast.

`[slow]` All conceded. Here's the dissent I file anyway, and it rests on one observation the drought data keeps producing.

---

**Because the room isn't quite empty. There's one weather event left, and it's the most human in the game.**

When a player dies — the real kind, the kind with an obituary in a clan Discord — this community still, sometimes, walks to Falador. You may have seen one and not known: a crowd in a ballroom at an odd hour, someone's bank being fed into the chest by the people who knew them, `[beat]` and then the lever, and the balloons coming down over everyone — friends and total strangers alike, because that's the machine's whole nature; it cannot target; it never could. The scattered wealth of the missing person falls on whoever came, which by then includes people who never met them, popping balloons at a memorial for a stranger, `[slow]` which is somehow the exact and only correct way for it to work.

No system message marks these. The room doesn't know it's a chapel now. The instruments just record: anomalous precipitation over Falador, cause unlisted. `[beat]` I said the machine gives wealth away badly on purpose. At a memorial, every one of the four refusals — the anonymity, the randomness, the indignity, the loss — stops being a design quirk and becomes the entire liturgy. You cannot buy that with a buy limit. There is no slot on the Exchange for *scatter him over the crowd*.

---

**The extended outlook, then, and we'll let you go.**

[VISUAL: the forecast panel — seven days of small suns over Falador; on one day, unexplained, a tiny balloon icon]

Continued drought, with occasional memorial rain. No change expected; do not expect the room to fill again — the climate that filled it is gone, and this forecast doesn't trade in comfort. `[beat]` But if you're ever in Falador and you feel a balloon bump your shoulder — pop it. It's legal. It's the whole idea. Someone fed the sky on purpose, once, `[slow]` for whoever happened to be standing under it — which, tonight, was you.

That's the weather. `[beat]` Back to you.

[VISUAL: the broadcast desk; the map behind showing one balloon drifting across an empty ballroom; hold; cut to static, then black]
