# Tears of Guthix — Full Script (Project N, #1)

**Thesis:** Tears of Guthix is the only counter-cyclical mechanic in Old School RuneScape — everywhere else the game pays your strengths, because you choose what to train and you train what you're already good at; once a week, in a cave under Lumbridge Swamp, a dead god's grief is collected and paid, automatically, without consultation, to your *weakest* skill. Five minutes, negligible XP, skipped by nearly everyone — and the gentlest design decision in the game.
**Format:** Guided cave tour. One visitor (the viewer), hushed guide's voice, single file, mind the light creatures. The tour's stations are the essay's sections.
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes (~1,900 words)
**Voice dial:** High and quiet. The batch's heart; carries its single `[hold]`. No profanity — the register stays unbroken by design.
**Facts:** Quest requirement, tear mechanics (blue/green), time-scaling rules, lore specifics all flagged; no numbers in prose.

## SCRIPT

Keep your voice down, please. And watch the drop on your left.

[VISUAL: black; then torchlight on wet stone; the Lumbridge Swamp cave entrance from inside, the circle of daylight receding above]

We're going under the swamp. Under the castle, under the cow field, under the first hour of every account you've ever made — there's a crack in the floor of the world down here, and at the bottom of it, a god is crying. `[beat]` Has been for a very long time. Longer than your account. Longer than the game, if you take the lore at its word, and today, for thirteen minutes, we're going to take the lore at its word.

This is a tour of the smallest famous place in Old School RuneScape. Most players have been here somewhere between zero and one times. The reward, and we'll get to the reward, is close to nothing. And my claim — the thing I'll have earned or not by the time we surface —

» is that this cave contains the gentlest design decision in the entire game, `[beat]` and that its gentleness is *structural*, written into the mechanic itself, visible nowhere else in twenty years of updates.

Single file now. The bridge is a living thing and it prefers not to be startled.

---

**Station one. The chasm.**

[VISUAL: the light creatures — drifting motes over a black gap; a player alights on one and floats across]

To cross the dark down here you ride a light creature — a drifting lantern-thing with no dialogue, no drops, no bestiary entry worth the name, that exists to carry you over a hole and be beautiful. Notice, as we drift, what the game is already doing: nothing down here is optimal. No resource nodes. Nothing to kill for profit. The cave asks a quest of you before it admits you at all, and then it offers — architecturally, deliberately — an experience with no numbers in it yet. In a game whose every other cave is a spreadsheet with rock textures, this one opens with a *ferry made of light*. `[slow]` The design is clearing its throat. Something down here runs on different physics, and the cave wants you slowed down before you meet it.

Mind the landing. She's ahead.

---

**Station two. The serpent. And the strangest toll in the game.**

[VISUAL: Juna, coiled across the cave mouth, enormous and patient]

Her name is Juna. She has guarded this cave, per the lore, for thousands of years, and she controls the only admission to the tears — one visit a week, no exceptions, and we'll return to the weekliness, because I think it's the mechanic's quietest genius. But first, the toll. She takes no gold.

She asks you to tell her a story.

`[beat]` That's the door charge. You, the player — the same creature that has clicked through ten thousand dialogue boxes at speed, that treats every NPC as a vending machine with a face — are required, mechanically, to *narrate your week to a snake*. What you've done. Where you've been. The game reaches into its own logs and turns your recent activity into a told tale, because the guardian of a god's grief is bored, or lonely, or — the reading I prefer, and it's mine, flagged — because the design wants to establish, right at the threshold, what currency this room runs on. Down here the medium of exchange is *attention*. You pay some to a serpent. `[beat]` And then the cave pays some to you, in a way no other room in the game has ever dared copy.

---

**Station three. The tears themselves. Please don't drink anything until I explain the colors.**

[VISUAL: the tear streams along the walls — blue threads and green threads, shifting between them; a player with a bowl, chasing]

The walls of the far chamber weep. Two colors run in the same streams: blue tears, which fill your bowl, and green tears, which *empty* it — grief and something bitterer braided together, moving, swapping channels without warning, so collecting is a small frantic dance of approach and retreat. You get a sliver of time in there — how much depends on what you've done with your life; the cave checks your record and portions the minutes accordingly, and the exact accounting is a flag for the wiki pass. Then it's over, for a week.

And then the cave does the thing this whole video exists to point at. It takes your bowl of a dead god's grief, weighs it… and pays the experience — automatically, no menu, no choice offered — `[slow]` into your *lowest* skill.

Watch where that lands. Not your best skill. Not your chosen skill. Not a lamp you get to aim, like every other reward of this shape in the game — and the game has dozens; generosity is usually steerable. The tears find the skill with the least experience in it — the one at the bottom of the list — and they raise *that*.

---

**Now surface with me a little, because to feel what that means you have to remember how the rest of the game works.**

» Everywhere else in Gielinor, strength compounds. You choose what to train; you train what you like; you like what you're already good at — and so every account, over years, becomes a portrait of its own preferences, sharpened. The XP curve video on this channel called the skill list a ledger of what you've paid. Look at the *bottom* of that ledger, though. Your lowest skill is a list of your aversions. It's the thing you've decided you're bad at, or beneath you, or not worth the hours — Runecrafting, probably, or Agility, and the fact that I can guess is the point: the neglect is systemic, because the whole game's physics *reward* neglecting it. Compounding is the game's gravity. Strength attracts investment attracts strength.

One mechanic in the entire game pushes the other way. One. A weekly trickle, from a crying god, aimed by design at the part of you that everything else — the meta, the efficiency guides, your own taste — has agreed to abandon. `[beat]` The lore says Guthix is the god of *balance*. Most of the game treats that as a costume. This cave is the only room where somebody made it a *rule*.

---

**The turn, because you've been doing the math this whole tour, and the math is rude.**

The XP is negligible. Truly. A week's tears round to a rounding error against one hour of actually training the skill; no efficiency guide on Earth routes through this cave past the one visit that unlocks the diary tick. By the standards this channel itself set — there's a whole video about minigames dying precisely because players learned to price their hour — Tears of Guthix is dead content. Measured in the ledger's units, it's a puddle with a queue of zero, sentimentality with a respawn timer, and a design essay about it is a man pointing at a wet wall and calling it a cathedral.

`[slow]` Here's what I'll hold against that, and it's the whole tour, really. The graveyard video was about mechanics that *competed* for your hour and lost. This room never entered the competition. Its reward was never the payment; its reward is the *direction* of the payment — the once-a-week fact of something in this ruthless, compounding, preference-sharpening world looking at your record, finding the weakest line in it, and deciding, without asking you, that the weakest line is the one that deserves the water. That's worth almost nothing an hour. `[beat]` Some things are cheap because they're worthless, and some things are cheap because they refuse to charge. The skill of telling those apart has no XP bar either.

---

**Last station. Back up the way we came. You can talk normally now — actually, no. Keep it down a moment longer.**

The weekliness. I promised we'd return to it. Every other faucet in this game refills on ticks and days and dailies engineered for retention. This one runs on a *week* — the unit of ordinary human ritual, of services and visits and phone calls to people we're bad at calling. And here's the detail I can't put down, the one that made this video: the schedule belongs to the tears, and the tears don't need you.

[VISUAL: the chamber, empty of players; the blue and green threads still moving on the walls; hold the shot well past comfortable]

The cave doesn't pause between visits. The god's grief has no respawn timer, no aggro range, no interest in your calendar. It fell before your account existed. It falls right now, this second, while you watch this — under the swamp, past the serpent, in the dark, collected or not.

`[hold]`

Grief works like that, on the surface too. It doesn't wait for the visit. The visit was never for *it*.

`[slow]` So: once a week, if you like. Tell the snake a story — she counts it as payment, and she may be the only one keeping score correctly. Hold out the bowl. And let the one room in the world that plays by different physics do the thing nothing else will:

pay your weakest self first.

`[beat]` Tour's over. Mind the drop on your right, this time. They swap sides, like the tears.

[VISUAL: the circle of daylight growing overhead; the sounds of the swamp returning; cut]

## Flags for the user

- **Access requirements** — the Tears of Guthix *quest* gates the minigame; verify current requirements and the one-visit-per-week rule's exact reset (fixed weekly reset vs. rolling seven days) before any card.
- **Time-in-chamber scaling** — collection time scales with account progress (quest points historically; verify the current formula — it has been adjusted in OSRS's lifetime). The script deliberately says "the cave checks your record and portions the minutes accordingly" — keep that altitude until verified.
- **Blue/green tear mechanics** — blue fill, green subtract, streams shift: high confidence; verify subtraction specifics (does green reduce collected tears or waste time?) before the on-screen explainer.
- **XP goes to lowest-XP skill automatically** — the thesis pillar; high confidence; verify tie-breaking and whether it's lowest by XP (it is, from memory) rather than lowest by level, since the distinction could matter on a card.
- **"Tell Juna a story" toll** — the entry interaction is story-telling flavor drawn from your recent activity; high confidence on the story framing, medium on how dynamic it actually is — verify before claiming the game "reaches into its own logs" (soften to "asks for your tale" if the dynamism is overstated).
- **Lore: Guthix wept / god of balance / the tears' origin** — keep at the script's current altitude ("per the lore," "if you take the lore at its word"); verify against quest dialogue and wiki lore pages before any lore card. Do not import RS3 lore (Guthix's later story diverges) — OSRS-available sources only.
- **Light creatures require a light source / While Guthix Sleeps era details** — the ferry description is high confidence for the ToG cave; verify the light-source requirement detail if shown.
- **Diary tick** — "the one visit that unlocks the diary tick" — verify which achievement diary (Lumbridge & Draynor) includes a ToG task before keeping the aside.
