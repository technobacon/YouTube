# Spot Taken — Full Script (Project N, #4)

**Thesis:** "Spot taken" — two words typed on a beach — is a functioning land registry written in nothing. OSRS players hold, transfer, and respect crab-training real estate with zero enforcement mechanics, among anonymous strangers they'll never meet again, under conditions where every standard theory of commons governance predicts collapse. The system's fragility, its known failure modes, and the fact that it mostly holds anyway make it one of the best small datasets on human nature that any game has ever accidentally collected.
**Format:** Surveyor's report. A land surveyor is dispatched to map the property lines of the Hosidius shore; the report proceeds through site description, boundary determination, dispute law, and a final filing.
**Runtime:** ~13.5 minutes (~1,950 words)
**Voice dial:** High. Deadpan-bureaucratic carrying the comedy; the filing goes tender.
**Facts:** Crab aggro/reset mechanics, world counts, Crabclaw Isle fee, "crashing isn't reportable" all flagged; no numbers in prose.

## SCRIPT

I was dispatched on a Tuesday to survey the parcels of the Hosidius shoreline. Standard commission. Boundary determination, dispute review, final filing.

[VISUAL: a yellowed cadastral map unrolling over footage of the Hosidius coast; theodolite crosshairs settle on a beach]

Preliminary finding, filed within the hour: there are no parcels. No deeds. No fences, markers, registry, or law. The beach is open to all citizens equally, per the rules of the world, and nothing on it can be owned, per the same rules.

Second finding, filed by sundown, `[beat]` and the reason this report runs thirteen minutes: everyone knows where the parcels are.

» I have surveyed borders in this world that are patrolled by guards and marked in stone, and none of them held better than a stretch of sand governed entirely by two words, typed by strangers: `[beat]` *spot taken.*

This report concerns those two words. What they hold, how they fail, and what a beach full of crabs has to say about the largest open question in political philosophy. The commission was for a week. `[slow]` I've been here a month. You'll see why.

---

**Site description.**

The subject property is a training beach. The local wildlife — sand crabs — disguise themselves as rocks, wake when a citizen walks near, and attack with the enthusiasm of wet cardboard. They hit almost nothing, they die generously, and so this shore is where the world sends its young to grow: park yourself among the rocks, wake the crabs, and let combat experience accrue at low attention prices while the crabs achieve nothing against you at their leisure.

The geology matters to the economics. Crab rocks cluster unevenly — most stretches wake one or two, but certain formations wake several at once, and those formations are the *premium lots*, the beachfront acreage, known to every resident by shape and count. `[beat]` And the wildlife has a lease clause: after roughly ten minutes of being farmed — figure flagged for verification, like everything in this report — the crabs stop rising. They've seen you. To renew their interest you must walk meaningfully away and return, a little pilgrimage of reset, during which — note this carefully, it becomes the crux of the dispute law — `[slow]` your lot stands empty, visibly unoccupied, legally unclaimed by any mechanism the world provides.

A beach of scarce premium lots, periodically vacated by their holders, in a world with no property system. Every economist reading this report just winced. This should be a war zone.

[VISUAL: the surveyor's map annotating the shore — "2-ROCK LOT", "4-ROCK LOT (PREMIUM)", tide lines, a hand-drawn skull over nothing in particular]

---

**Boundary law. The instrument itself.**

The deed to a sand crab spot is established as follows. A citizen arrives at an occupied or ambiguous lot and types a single word, usually with a question mark: *taken?* The current holder replies *yes* — or replies nothing, absence of reply within some communally understood interval constituting abandonment — and the matter is settled. That's the entire land law. One interrogative. One reply. No signature, notary, or witness; the parties are anonymous, unguilded, mutually invisible in every future sense; they will never meet again and both know it.

As a legal instrument, I want you to appreciate how strange the question is, because I've surveyed a lot of jurisdictions and I've never seen its like. The asker is voluntarily creating an obligation that did not exist. Nothing compels the question. The asker could simply *start* — walk up, wake the crabs, take the lot; the world's rules permit it completely, the way physics permits queue-jumping. Instead they pause at the boundary of an invisible parcel, `[beat]` and *ratify it*. The question "taken?" is a small act of legislation: it votes the property system into existence, one asking at a time, and binds the asker to a "yes" they didn't have to invite.

Current compliance rates: unmeasurable, but as your surveyor in the field a month, I'll attest — the question gets asked, up and down this beach, all day, by strangers with nothing to lose by not asking. Hold your cynicism for the appropriate section. It's coming. First, the appeal court, because it's the most remarkable structure on the survey.

---

**Dispute resolution. Or: the multiverse as zoning policy.**

When two citizens want one lot, this world offers a resolution mechanism no terrestrial jurisdiction has ever possessed: `[slow]` there are dozens of this beach.

Parallel worlds — whole duplicate Earths, a menu away — each with its own identical shoreline, its own premium lots, its own crabs pretending to be rocks. The full count is flagged for verification, but the structure is the point: scarcity here is real on any one world and *illusory across the stack*, and every resident knows it. So the standard dispute proceeding runs: *taken?* — *yes* — and the loser… hops. Thirty seconds of loading, and they're standing on the same sand under a different sky, alone, at the exact lot they wanted.

I've been a surveyor a long time. Every land dispute in history — every fence war, every inheritance feud, every border with a trench in it — has run on the same substrate: *there is only one of this place.* This world quietly deleted that premise, `[beat]` and the deletion is doing enormous, invisible work in the dispute law. "Taken?" costs so little to honor *because* honoring it costs a hop, and a hop costs less than a fight. The infinite beaches subsidize the manners. File that. It matters in a minute, when we get to what the manners are actually made of.

---

**Jurisprudence review. Why, by the book, none of this should hold.**

There's a body of research on exactly this situation — commons without governments. Ostrom's fieldwork, the famous cases: fisheries, alpine pastures, irrigation systems that ran for centuries on norms alone. And that literature comes with conditions, the load specifications under which normed commons hold: small groups. Stable membership. Repeated interaction. Reputation that follows you. Monitoring. Graduated sanctions. The fishermen of the case studies knew each other's fathers.

Now audit this beach against the specification sheet. `[quick]` Group: enormous, global, anonymous. Membership: churning by the minute. Repeat interaction: essentially none — the stranger you ask "taken?" will never appear in your life again, and no reputation attaches to a name you won't remember on a world you're leaving. Monitoring: none. Sanctions: none exist; crashing a spot is — verify this, but I believe it firmly — against no rule at all, reportable to no one, punishable by nothing.

`[slow]` Every condition fails. The theory, read honestly, predicts this beach collapses into a crab-waking free-for-all where asking is for suckers.

`[beat]` And the beach mostly holds. That's the finding, and I've checked it for a month, and I don't fully understand it, and I want you to sit in the not-understanding with me before the cynics arrive, because a norm surviving outside its load specifications is the most interesting thing a surveyor ever gets to file.

---

**The cynical read, then, as the format requires — and the crash report, because the failures are real and a survey that omits them is a brochure.**

The deflationary theory goes: this has nothing to do with goodness. Hopping is *cheap* — thirty seconds against a contest you might lose. "Politeness" here is just arithmetic wearing a smile; starve the beach of spare worlds — peak hours, a fresh league, the good lots on the busy world — and watch the manners evaporate. And the record supports the pressure test: ask anyone who trains here. Some days a stranger in expensive gear walks onto your lot mid-reset like weather, no question, no reply, just *starts* — and the feeling, which as your surveyor I am trained to name precisely, is: `[beat]` *you absolute fuck.* Noted. Measured. Filed. There is even a market verdict on the norm's limits: offshore sits an island of premium crab acreage that charges an entrance fee — when the customs buckle, someone always builds a toll booth, and people pay it, which tells you exactly what the free beach's frictions cost.

So the cynics explain the hopping. Here's what the cynics' model leaves unexplained, and it's the crux of the report: hopping away is cost-benefit. `[slow]` *Asking* is not. The question "taken?" is pure overhead — it costs time, invites a binding "yes," and defends a stranger's claim that no power on this beach would ever enforce. The efficient move is to start waking crabs and let the other party hop. Yet the question persists, ambient, thousands of times a day, maintained by nobody, taught by nothing — there is no tutorial for beach law; every player learned it by *being asked* — `[beat]` a custom passing from stranger to stranger like a candle flame, in the one environment theory says candles can't stay lit.

---

**Final filing.**

[VISUAL: the cadastral map complete now — the shoreline parceled in dotted lines labeled in a careful hand: "HELD", "HELD", "DISPUTED, RESOLVED BY HOP", "HELD BY SILENCE"]

To the commissioning office. I have surveyed the beach. Findings as follows. The property lines exist. They are drawn in nothing, recorded nowhere, enforced by no one, and honored — imperfectly, daily, at scale — by anonymous strangers with no reason beyond the asking. The infinite beaches subsidize the manners; the manners still had to be *invented*, and reinvented every time someone new pauses at an invisible line and types a word they owe nobody.

Recommendation: no action. `[beat]` The system is working. Do not formalize it. A deed would kill what the question keeps alive — the daily, voluntary, unpoliced *choosing* of it, which is the entire cargo.

Personal addendum, unbecoming of a surveyor, filed anyway: somewhere on that beach tonight, a stranger is standing at the edge of somebody's spot, typing a question mark they could omit, `[slow]` and waiting. The waiting is nothing. Half a minute of a Tuesday. But I've surveyed every border in this world, and I can report that the whole thing — the entire settled, unfenced, functioning map of it — `[beat]` is made of exactly that wait.

Survey complete. `[beat]` Lot's taken, by the way. `[beat]` But there's another world along in a minute.

[VISUAL: the beach at dusk, crabs settling back into rocks; one player stands at a respectful distance from another, a chat bubble forming; cut before the reply]
