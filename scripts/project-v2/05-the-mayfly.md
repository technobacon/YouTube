# The Mayfly — Full Script (Project V2, #5)

**Thesis:** Leagues is OSRS admitting what players actually love: the *rate* of becoming, and never the possessions. A scheduled apocalypse — a character born with its deletion date printed on its birth certificate — produces braver, looser, more generous play, and its existence is what lets the permanent game stay slow. The mayfly protects the river.
**Format:** Nature-film biography. One temporary character followed womb to tomb — hatching, metabolism, behavior, swarm, death — in wildlife-documentary voice that keeps slipping into second person, because the mayfly is you.

## SCRIPT

In the second week of November, in a server farm it will never see, a creature is born with two dates on its birth certificate.

[VISUAL: nature-documentary grade — extreme slow zoom on a character-creation screen; then a certificate mock-up: NAME, then HATCHED: NOVEMBER, then a second field, blurred]

It hatches fully formed, in a starting town, in weather it has no time to complain about. It is identical in every visible way to the ordinary residents of this world — same body, same map, same gods. One difference, and the difference is everything: `[slow]` it knows exactly how long it has. The second date is printed right there. Eight-ish weeks out. The creature reads it, absorbs it the way mayflies absorb the concept of evening…

`[beat]` and then it *moves*.

This is a film about the temporary version of Old School RuneScape. Leagues: the seasonal mode where the whole world is copied, cranked, and handed to you with an expiry date — a fresh character, enormous experience multipliers, wild rule-bending powers, and scheduled, certain, non-negotiable deletion at the end. A video game that ships its own apocalypse on a calendar.

» And I want to argue the mayfly is the most honest creature in this ecosystem — that the temporary mode understands what players love better than the permanent one does, `[beat]` and that the permanent game survives *because* the mayfly exists, and dies on time, every year.

Two numbers before we follow the life cycle, both from this channel's ledger. At the end of one recent league, the most players ever recorded simultaneously in this decades-old game — the all-time high-water mark of its entire history to that point — were logged into *the version scheduled for deletion*. That record stood for eight months. `[beat]` And the thing that finally broke it is the strangest fact in this video, so I'm keeping it for the turn. Patience. Nature films are slow.

---

**Life stage one: metabolism.**

[VISUAL: XP drops cascading like the river footage in every wildlife doc; a level counter blurring upward]

The defining adaptation of the mayfly is speed. Experience arrives at many times the natural rate — the exact multiple varies by season and how far the creature progresses — which means the famous, punishing arithmetic of this game, the doubling staircase a whole other video on this channel spent fourteen minutes on, compresses from years into afternoons. The player who swore they'd never max — the one with the job, the kids, the forty-minute evenings — watches numbers they'd budgeted a decade for arrive before dinner. Whole lifetimes of progression, the granite kind, the kind measured in *seasons of your actual life*, `[quick]` folding past the screen in an evening, and I need you to understand what that feels like from inside: `[beat]` it's glorious. It's fucking glorious, there's no adult way to say it.

And the permanent game's defenders — I'm one, most Tuesdays — will tell you the speed is fake, that numbers without cost are sugar. Hold the objection; it's the turn's job. For now just log the observation like field scientists: when the cost of becoming drops to nearly nothing, players do not become bored, as the it-was-never-about-the-numbers theory predicts. `[slow]` They become *ravenous*. The appetite was real the whole time. It was the price that was doing the rationing.

---

**Life stage two: behavior. Because the fascinating data was never the speed. It's what the dying do differently.**

Watch the mayfly play, and compare it to its long-lived cousin — the main account, that careful, anxious, estate-planning animal. The main hoards; there's an entire video in this batch about the museum it curates. The mayfly *cannot* hoard, structurally: these seasonal worlds run on iron rules, no trade, no market, and there is no tomorrow to sell to anyway. No estate. No legacy. Every acquisition is for *use*, this week, or it's nothing.

And freed from the estate, look at the behavior change. The mayfly is **braver** — it fights bosses the main has been politely avoiding for years, because the downside is a rounding error on a life already scheduled to end. It's **looser** — it builds absurd, joyful, wrong builds, the meme loadouts, the one-skill pilgrimages, experiments the main would never risk on the permanent record. `[beat]` And here's the field note that got me writing this film at all: it is *kinder to its own hours*. No mayfly grinds a season it hates "for later." Later is canceled. Every evening spent is spent on purpose, on appetite, on the thing itself — which, if you've been keeping score across this batch, is the exact virtue every other essay kept circling: the attention paid on purpose, the task that releases you, the luck that has to be lived through. The mayfly does all of it natively. `[slow]` The death date, it turns out, is a *tutor*.

[VISUAL: split field-footage — a main banking cautiously; a league character diving into a boss room in clown gear]

---

**Life stage three: the swarm.**

Mayflies hatch together — that's the whole reproductive strategy, and it's this game's too. For a few weeks, everyone is new at the same time. The starting zones are shoulder to shoulder. The chat is dense with strangers comparing routes, because everyone's route is different and everyone's route is *this week*. Veterans describe the feeling with a specific, dangerous word — it feels like 2007 — and a previous video on this channel, the lab-report one about a different game entirely, would remind them that 2007 was never a year you can visit. `[beat]` Correct. But a *synchronized beginning* — the actual ingredient, the thing 2007 accidentally had — that can be manufactured. That's what a league launch is. Fog, on a schedule. The swarm is the point of the season, and the season is short *because* swarms are: stretch it to forever and it's just the regular game again, alone.

---

**Now the turn, and it has two heads.**

Head one: everything I've praised has an uglier name, and the name is FOMO machinery. A limited-time mode is urgency marketing with a world attached; the death date I've been calling a tutor is also a whip — *play now, or miss it forever* — the oldest lever in the retail playbook, aimed at the demographic with the least spare time and the most nostalgia to exploit. The mayfly isn't wise. It's being farmed.

Head two, the sharper one: the sugar ruins the meal. Train a player at enormous multiples for two months, hand them back their 1x main, and the permanent game — the whole granite cathedral this channel keeps defending — suddenly plays like walking through setting concrete. The community has a name for it: the league hangover. By this argument the mode is a slow poison that teaches the player base to resent the very slowness the main game is *made of*.

`[slow]` Both heads deserve a real answer, and the real answer is the number I've been saving.

The record that finally beat the league's record — the actual all-time population peak of this game, the most people ever simultaneously in Gielinor in its entire history — was set eight months later, on an ordinary Sunday, `[beat]` with no league running. No event. No update that weekend. The permanent game, the slow one, the 1x cathedral of setting concrete, quietly filled past every party the temporary mode ever threw — after five leagues, not despite them. Correlation is doing some lifting here and I'll flag it plainly: population has many parents. But if the hangover theory were the whole truth — if the mayfly really taught contempt for the river — the data should at minimum *lean* that way. It leans the opposite. The appetite the league wakes doesn't seem to die with the character. `[beat]` It walks back to the slow world, and stays.

» The mayfly protects the river. The version that dies on schedule is a load-bearing part of why the permanent one is more alive than it has ever been.

---

**Which leaves only the death, and the reason this is a nature film instead of a eulogy — this channel already did the eulogy, for something else, and anyway nobody here is sad.**

In Tibet there's a practice this ending was always going to arrive at: monks spend weeks building a mandala out of colored sand, grain by grain, geometry you could lose a day inside. And when it's finished, they sweep it. That's the practice. The sweeping is in the design spec. The point — as close as an outsider gets to saying it — is that the impermanence never subtracted from the making; the impermanence is what the making was *about*.

The league ends in the same second for everyone. The character you inhabited at sixteen-times the speed of your ordinary life — the brave one, the loose one, the one that was kinder to its hours — gets swept, with every level and every acquisition, back into sand. `[beat]` And the players log off, and they are, overwhelmingly, observably, *fine*. They post their little trophy screenshots like pressed flowers. They say "good league" to strangers. They walk back to their mains — to the museum tabs and the slow staircase — carrying nothing.

Carrying, technically, one thing. The game converts your finished league into rewards for the permanent world, and the rewards are cosmetic. Ornaments. Nothing with stats; nothing that helps; a coat of paint that says *I was there, the season it all went fast.*

[VISUAL: the birth certificate from the cold open, unblurred at last — the second date legible; beneath it, stamped: SWEPT. Then a trophy cosmetic, rotating slowly in the dark]

You already know where this goes, so let's go there together, gently. Every account is the mayfly. `[slow]` Yours too — the main, the museum, the granite staircase. The servers just haven't printed the second date yet, and their not-printing-it lets you live like the river runs forever. The league is the one version of this world honest enough to show you the certificate at the door. And look — *look* — what the honesty did. Nobody played worse for knowing. Everybody played braver, looser, kinder to their hours.

`[hold]`

The rewards for a finished life are cosmetic. `[beat]` They always were. The mayflies knew, the monks knew, and now it's on your screen in a font a dwarf would find readable: it was never the sand. It was the weeks of placing it, `[beat]` shoulder to shoulder, the season everyone began at once.

Good league. `[beat]` See you at the next hatching.

[VISUAL: the login screen, a new league's title card glowing; hold; cut to black before the button is pressed]
