# Take as Prescribed — Full Script (Project V2, #2)

**Thesis:** Slayer's genius is the removal of choice. A weighted random to-do list, dispensed by NPCs, defeated the open world's total freedom so decisively that modern OSRS is scaffolded on it — and the reason the mechanism stays benign while every real-world equivalent curdles is that the dwarf's list never learns from you, never profits from your staying, and always ends. Slayer is a recommendation engine from 2005, and the ways it's primitive are the ways it's humane.
**Format:** Doctor's appointment. The Slayer master as GP; tasks as prescriptions; the video is structured as a consultation — symptoms, prescription, mechanism of action, side effects, and a second opinion.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,050 words)
**Voice dial:** High. The comedy engine of the batch, with the cosmic turn aimed at the viewer's feeds.
**Facts:** Release year, task counts, point costs, and which monsters are task-locked all flagged; no numbers in prose.

## SCRIPT

The doctor will see you now.

[VISUAL: a waiting room — actual folding chairs composited into Burthorpe; a Slayer master behind a desk with a clipboard; soft fluorescent hum]

He's a dwarf. He has no medical training, wears a horned helmet indoors, and his entire diagnostic method is looking at you once and writing a number on a slip. The prescription reads: *ninety-four dust devils.* Take with prayer. Come back when you're done. `[beat]` Next patient.

And here's the thing this video has to explain: that prescription works. It has worked for twenty years. It works so well that this — a stranger assigning you chores from a weighted list — is arguably the single most successful piece of content design in Old School RuneScape, the spine the modern game hangs off. Millions of hours a week, dispensed one slip at a time, by a dwarf.

» Because Slayer's genius is the removal of choice. `[beat]` And you cannot understand this game — possibly this decade — until you understand why removing choice is a thing people will queue for.

First, the symptom. Then the medicine.

---

**The symptom presents like this.**

You log in. You have — and I want you to feel the size of this — *total freedom*. Hundreds of quests, twenty-something skills, a continent, no main story ordering you around, no daily chores stacked at the login screen. The most player-directed game in the genre. You can do anything.

[VISUAL: a high-level account standing at the GE, camera slowly orbiting, doing nothing; timestamp in the corner climbing]

So you stand at the Grand Exchange and spin the camera. For eleven minutes. You open the skills tab and close it. You check prices on an item you have no plan for. You are a creature with ten thousand hours of available content experiencing the one grind that awards no XP, which is *deciding* — and eventually you log out, having done nothing, vaguely ashamed, planning to try freedom again tomorrow.

You can do anything. `[slow]` That's the fucking problem.

The psychologists call it choice overload — the finding, replicated in supermarkets and pension plans and dating apps, that past a certain point every added option subtracts. The philosophers got there first and said it darker: Sartre called man *condemned* to be free — condemned, his word, because every unmade choice is yours to carry and there's no one to hand it to. Sartre never played this game. Sartre would have lasted four minutes at the Grand Exchange before rolling a task.

---

**Now the medicine, and notice how strange it is when you read the label.**

You walk to the dwarf. He gives you a random assignment from a weighted list — you don't pick the monster; picking is precisely the service being removed. If you hate the task, you can refuse it… for a fee, paid in points you earned by *complying* with previous tasks. Sit with the shape of that: obedience generates a currency whose main use is buying back tiny amounts of disobedience. And players *love* it. The system's entire pitch is "I will tell you what to do tonight," and the response, for twenty years, from the most independence-obsessed player base in the genre — ironmen, the people who voted against a game update becoming too convenient — has been: *yes. Please. Thank you. One more.*

[VISUAL: the task interface; the skip/block costs highlighted; then a montage of task after task rolling]

Why does the medicine work? The mechanism of action is the part I find genuinely beautiful, and it's this: the assignment changes the *sentence* your grind lives inside.

Killing two hundred of a thing for no reason is a behavior pattern with a clinical name. Killing one hundred and eighty-six of a thing *because a dwarf requires it* is a Tuesday. The activity is identical — same cave, same clicks, same drops. What changed is the grammar. One version is an open question the whole time — is this worth it, should I be elsewhere, who am I — and the other version has been *closed* by an authority, however absurd, and a closed question is restful in a way this era has almost no other access to. The task list converts existential time, which is heavy, into assigned time, which is light. `[beat]` Homework did this. Chores did this. It turns out we missed it.

---

**And on that mechanism, Jagex built the actual skeleton of the modern game — this is the design-history claim, so cameras out.**

Look at what ships through the prescription pad. New monsters slot into the task pool. Iconic drops sit behind Slayer levels — the whip alone has moved more players through this system than any quest ever moved anywhere. Whole bosses live inside the skill. And the subtler function, the one nobody thanks it for: *distribution*. The random list is a pump that pushes population out of the three optimal corners players would otherwise huddle in and through the whole bestiary — the forgotten caves, the mid-level dungeons, the content that would be a ghost town under free choice. The world stays visited because a dwarf keeps sending people to all of it.

Which means the honest name for Slayer — and I mean this as high praise, though it won't sound like it for another minute — is a *recommendation engine*. A system that watches nothing, knows nothing, and simply says: you seem lost; consume this next. OSRS built one out of a dwarf in 2005, years before that sentence became the load plan for the entire internet. `[beat]` And that parallel is where the second opinion comes in, because it's not a flattering one.

---

**Second opinion. The case against the medicine, at full strength.**

A random reward schedule, dispensed in variable doses, that keeps the patient pulling the lever and calls it contentment — there's a name for that, and the name is Skinner box, and dressing the lever in a horned helmet changes the furniture and none of the wiring. The harder version: I just spent a section praising a system for solving your freedom by *taking it*, and that's the exact sales pitch of every engagement algorithm currently strip-mining your attention span. The feed also noticed that choosing is painful. The feed also says: you seem lost; consume this next. If Slayer is beautiful, the feed is beautiful, and if the feed is a machine for eating your one finite life, then the dwarf is just the same machine with worse graphics, and this entire video is Stockholm syndrome with chapters.

`[slow]` That's the strongest shape of the argument, and I'm going to answer it carefully, because I think the differences are the whole point — and there are three of them. The dwarf earns his helmet on three counts.

One: the list never learns. Your task is drawn from the same weighted table as everyone's, tuned once, adjusted rarely, by humans, in public, through polls. There is no version of the dwarf that studies your behavior and discovers that you specifically can't resist abyssal demons at 1 a.m. Regular viewers have heard this argument on this channel before, about a certain experience curve that hasn't moved since 2001 — a system that cannot personalize is a system that cannot *hunt you*, and in 2026 that property is rarer than any drop in the game.

Two: the incentives point at the world, and the money faces away. The feed's recommendation exists to hold you where the advertising is; every second it retains you, someone is paid. The dwarf's recommendation *ejects* you — go away, go to a cave, be somewhere else, live. Jagex collects the same subscription whether you take a task or stand at the GE spinning the camera. Nobody's bonus vests when you extend.

Three — and this is the mercy the feed will never copy — » the task ends. There is a number on the slip, and the number goes down, and when it reaches zero the system's entire claim on you expires. It doesn't refresh. It doesn't auto-play the next one. You walk back to the dwarf *by choice* or you log off, complete, released, done — a word the infinite scroll has spent fifteen years surgically removing from your evenings. `[beat]` Slayer is a recommendation engine with a finish line. That's either a primitive version of the modern thing, or the last humane version of it. I know which way I vote.

---

**Consultation's nearly over. One more patient in the waiting room, though, and you've probably recognized them by now.**

[VISUAL: the waiting room again; the chair by the door; slow push-in]

Because the reason this system works on you has very little to do with dust devils. Some nights you have total freedom and it sits on your chest like a wardrobe. The whole day was decisions — the job, the money, the messages you owed people — and the last thing, the *last* thing you want from your evening is an open world. What you want, and almost nothing in adult life will say it plainly, is for someone with no stake in your answer to look at you once and say: *this next. Just this. Then rest.*

The game found a dwarf willing to do it for free. `[slow]` That's the whole medicine. It was never about the monsters.

Take as prescribed. `[beat]` Come back when you're done.

[VISUAL: the prescription slip on the desk, camera settling on the words "then rest"; hold; cut]
