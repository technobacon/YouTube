# Dry Anonymous — Full Script (Project V2, #3)

**Thesis:** OSRS's refusal to add meaningful bad-luck protection is the correct design decision, and the cruelest one: the moment a drop is guaranteed it becomes a wage, wages are fair and forgettable, and the entire emotional economy of the game — the screenshots, the stories, the transcendent lucky day — is funded by the possibility of genuine, unfair, statistically ordinary suffering. The dry streak is grief with a kill counter, and it is what luck costs.
**Format:** Support group meeting. Folding chairs in a circle, bad coffee, name tags; members share kill counts as confessions; the narrator facilitates. The meeting's rhythm (shares → the literature → the hard question → closing) is the essay's structure.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,050 words)
**Voice dial:** High. The batch's emotional core; the math section must stay genuinely rigorous or the tenderness collapses into vibes.
**Facts:** All math in prose is pure probability (no game stats); "1/512" is used as an explicit hypothetical. WHO entry is ledger-verified. The "no pity timers" pillar carries the batch's most important flag — see below.

## SCRIPT

Alright, everyone grab a chair. There's coffee at the back — it's bad, that's traditional. `[beat]` Let's get started.

[VISUAL: a circle of folding chairs composited into a bank interior; a hand-lettered banner: "DRY ANONYMOUS — all KCs welcome"; steam off a paper cup]

Hi. Welcome to Dry Anonymous. Everything shared in this room stays in this room, except the parts I'm putting in a video essay. We have some new faces tonight, so let's go around. Say your name, and your number.

"Hi. I'm — yeah. Twelve hundred kills. The rate is one in three-eighty-three. I've stopped telling my clan."

"Hi. Two thousand four hundred, on a one in five-twelve. I did the math, you know? I sat down one night and I actually did the math, because I wanted the math to say something to me. I wanted the math to say *fuck you*, at least — something I could push against." `[beat]` "It doesn't say that. It says: *next kill, same odds.* That's all it ever says. You can't even get the dice to hate you."

Thank you for sharing. `[slow]` That's the whole disease in one sentence, and we'll come back to it.

One more: "Hi, I'm — I got it first kill. The thing my friend's been camping for a year. I don't know why I'm here." You're here because you can't tell him, and he can't hear it. Sit anywhere.

» This meeting is about the strangest design decision in Old School RuneScape: the refusal — twenty years, mostly unbroken — to promise you *anything*. `[beat]` And why that refusal, which is currently ruining the year of several people in this circle, might be the most important thing the game never built.

But first, the literature. Page one. The math is the medicine tonight, and I'm warning you now, it's not a painkiller.

---

**The literature: what "one in five hundred and twelve" actually promises.**

[VISUAL: a photocopied support-group handout, coffee ring, titled "SO YOU'RE DRY: THE NUMBERS"]

Take a drop at one in five-twelve — a nice round hypothetical; the game sells several like it. Here is everything that number promises: on each kill, independently, a die with five hundred and twelve faces gets rolled once. `[beat]` That's it. That is the entire covenant. Everything else you believe is something you brought.

It does *not* promise the drop by kill five-twelve. Run the numbers honestly — and this is the number I promised, the one that makes it worse before it makes it better — a player who does exactly five hundred and twelve kills has about a *thirty-seven percent* chance of still having nothing. More than a third. That's the geometric distribution, and that thirty-seven percent isn't a quirk of this rate — it's close to a mathematical constant; it shows up at one-in-a-hundred after a hundred kills and one-in-five-thousand after five thousand. Call it the universe's house rate: `[slow]` do exactly what the label says, and about a third of you walk away empty. Every time. By design of arithmetic itself, before Jagex touches anything.

And the property that's doing the real damage in this room has a name in probability theory, and the name sounds like a diagnosis: **memorylessness**. The dice do not know you've killed twenty-four hundred. There is no cosmic ledger filling toward your name. Kill twenty-four-hundred-and-one is exactly, precisely, the first kill, from the system's point of view — every kill is the first kill, forever. Our friend wanted the math to say *fuck you*, and the horror is that the math can't even do that, because hostility would require the dice to remember you exist.

[VISUAL: the handout's last line, underlined in pen: "the dice have no memory. that's what memory is for."]

Hold that sentence. It comes back at the end of the meeting, reversed.

---

**Now — the rituals. And I say this with love, because I've done every one of them.**

Watch what humans do when raw randomness touches them for long enough. They world-hop, because *this* world feels cold. They change gear "for luck." They kill exactly to a round number and stop. They bring the pet out, or put it away, depending on last time. An entire folk religion, complete with rival denominations, practiced daily inside one of the most rigorously deterministic systems ever built — a game that runs on published tables and fixed ticks, worshipped like weather.

Anthropologists saw this coming. Put people in front of outcomes they can't control, and ritual grows on the uncertainty like moss — it always has, on sailors and farmers and gamblers, and now on a man in a fishing hat hopping worlds at two a.m. The rituals don't touch the dice. They were never for the dice. `[beat]` They're for the person, because a rain dance is a way of doing *something* when the sky ignores you, and doing something is survivable in a way that pure waiting is not.

Which raises the question this meeting exists to answer. The game can see all of this. Jagex can read the same math, the same forum threads, the same 4 a.m. confessions. Every competing game has answered it with mercy — pity timers, bad-luck protection, currencies that turn grinding into layaway. `[slow]` Why does this one keep saying no?

---

**Here's the design case, and it's colder and better than "tradition."**

A pity timer converts luck into a wage. That's its entire function — cap the variance, guarantee the payday, turn "maybe today" into "definitely by Friday." And wages are *good*; wages are how civilization runs; wages are also — be honest — forgettable. Nobody screenshots their salary arriving. The moment the drop is guaranteed by kill N, every kill before N is a shift at the loot factory, and the drop, when it lands, is *payroll*.

Now look at what the unfair version buys, because the whole economy of this game's feelings runs through it. The first-kill screenshot that makes a clan chat explode. The one-year grind that ends on stream with a grown adult screaming. The story — and notice, `[quick]` every great loot story you have ever been told is a variance story, nobody has ever gripped your arm to tell you they received an item on schedule — the story *is* the payout. The game mints stories, and variance is the mint, and the dry streak, the miserable, unfair, thirty-seven-percent dry streak, is what the mint charges.

» Luck that cannot hurt you cannot arrive. It can only be delivered. `[beat]` And this game decided, twenty years ago, that delivery wasn't worth building a religion on.

That's the argument. I find it beautiful. I believed it the whole time I was dry, and it helped for about an hour a day. `[beat]` Which brings us to the part of the meeting we don't skip.

---

**The hard question. Because there's a word for reward schedules like this, and you've been waiting for me to say it.**

Variable-ratio reinforcement. The rarest-strongest schedule in the behavioral literature — the one from the pigeon experiments, the one the slot machine industry built its floor plans on. That's what a drop table is, clinically. And this community's vocabulary already testifies: *camping* a boss, *chasing* a drop, going *on tilt*. The World Health Organization has had gaming disorder in its diagnostic manual since 2019. I can stand in this circle and praise the mint, and some chairs here belong to people the mint is grinding — the ones at four in the morning, at triple rate, past the point where any story is worth the person it's happening to.

I'm going to give you the honest version instead of the absolving one. The machinery is real, and it's the same machinery. The defenses this game has are real too: the odds are published to the decimal, for free, forever — no casino on Earth does that; there's no per-spin charge, no whale pricing, no odds that shift when your engagement dips — the dice can't see you, which in 2026 is a moral feature; and the subscription costs the same whether you roll or don't. But time is a currency, the game does bill in it, and "the house takes no rake" is only mostly true of a business that profits by the month. `[slow]` So the honest sentence is this: the design deserves the defense, and the defense has a limit, and the limit is you. If the counter is hurting you — actually hurting, the kind you'd mention in a room like this — log off. The dice won't notice. `[beat]` That's the point of them. Nothing about them will ever notice, and so nothing about them will ever miss you, and you are free in a way no pity timer could ever make you.

---

**Closing circle. And the empty chair — I saw you all notice it.**

That's the chair for the drop. It's empty tonight; it's empty most nights; that's the arrangement. Somebody's, somewhere, is landing right now, and next week that person brings the story, and we'll all hate them for exactly as long as it takes to start asking questions about the kill.

Here's the reversal I promised, the one on the handout. The dice have no memory. `[slow]` You have nothing but. Every kill you've ever rolled is stacked up inside you — the count, the rituals, the nights — and when the drop finally lands, the system will mint one ordinary integer, same as it mints for everyone… and *you* will attach twenty-four hundred kills of carried history to it, and that attachment, that weight, that story — the math didn't make that. The math can't make that. Memory made that, and the dice outsourced the entire job to you, `[beat]` which means every meaningful drop in this game's history was co-authored by the only party in the transaction capable of suffering.

That's why there's no pity timer, if you want my real answer. Pity lives on your side of the screen. `[beat]` It was never the system's to give.

Meeting's over. Stack the chairs. Same time next week —

`[beat]` unless it drops.

[VISUAL: the circle of chairs, empty now, one knocked slightly askew; the banner sags at one corner; hold; cut]
