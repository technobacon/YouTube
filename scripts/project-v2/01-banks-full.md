# Bank's Full — Full Script (Project V2, #1)

**Thesis:** Your bank is the truest autobiography the game keeps. The finite bank forces triage, triage forces valuation, and valuation is autobiographical — the items you cannot bring yourself to drop are a record of who you've been, written in a medium you thought was storage. The 28-slots video argued the inventory's constraint is mechanical genius; this one argues the bank's constraint is a *literary* device.
**Format:** Archaeological dig. The bank gets excavated tab by tab as sediment strata — surface layer (the present), middle layers (abandoned projects), bedrock (the mementos) — with the dig frame returning at the turn and the close.
**Runtime:** ~13.5 minutes (~2,000 words)
**Voice dial:** High, tender-leaning. The batch's warm one.
**Facts:** No bank-size numbers, placeholder dates, or monetisation-survey specifics in prose; all flagged.

## SCRIPT

Somewhere in your bank is an item you will never use, cannot sell, and will not drop.

[VISUAL: black screen; then a single bank slot, spotlit: one burnt lobster]

You know the one. I said the sentence and something specific appeared in your head — a burnt lobster from 2006, a gnome scarf, a rusty sword, one (1) casket you never opened for reasons you couldn't explain under oath. Every bank cleaning of your entire account's life, that object has watched the cursor pass over it and survived. It has outlasted gear worth a thousand times more. It will outlast gear you haven't bought yet.

This video is about why. And the claim underneath it, so you can start sharpening something:

» Your bank is the truest autobiography this game keeps. `[beat]` And the reason it works as one is that it's too small.

There's a video on this channel about the inventory — twenty-eight slots, the argument for the constraint as mechanical genius. This is the sequel that goes the other direction: past the mechanics, into the archive. Because a bank in this game is a finite space you must curate for decades, and anything a person curates for decades stops being storage. So today we do archaeology. Your bank, excavated properly, tab by tab, top layer down to bedrock. Bring a brush. `[beat]` And somewhere down there, we're going to find the game's politest sentence, and I'll show you why it's also its most honest one. Later. Digs take time.

---

**Stratum one. The surface layer — the present tense.**

[VISUAL: a real bank interface, top tab; the dig-site framing device — grid squares overlaid, archaeological tags on item stacks]

Every dig starts in the topsoil, and the topsoil is legible. Tab one: the working self. Current gear. Consumables at the ready. The potions decanted, the runes counted, teleports in stacks like a commuter's travel card. This layer updates weekly; it's the person you are *right now*, and archaeologists would call it an active occupation layer. Someone lives here.

And already the medium is doing its recording trick, because look closer. The surface layer holds your *intentions*. The gear says what you're training. The supplies say what you're scared of. The row of teleports says where your life actually happens, as opposed to where you tell your clan it happens. You could hand tab one to a stranger and they could write your week. `[beat]` No other interface in the game confesses like this. The stats panel says what you've done; the bank says what you *meant* to do.

Hold that difference. It gets sadder one layer down.

---

**Stratum two. The middle layers — the abandoned selves.**

Scroll down. You know what's coming.

[VISUAL: the scroll downward played like a descent — lighting shifts, the dig gets deeper, dust]

The birdhouse seeds for a routine you kept for eleven days in 2021. The herb setup from the farming phase. Half a set of graceful you were definitely going to finish. Barrows gloves — no, wait, those are the *ingredients* for barrows gloves, still ingredients, four years on. A stack of teleport tablets to a place you no longer remember deciding to care about.

Archaeology has a word for this: abandonment layers. A settlement gets left in a hurry, and the pots stay where they stood, and four thousand years later someone with a brush can tell you the day the plans changed. Your middle tabs are wall-to-wall abandonment layers. Each little cluster of items is a self you were about to become — the efficient farmer, the birdhouse-on-timer person, the one who finishes graceful — preserved at the exact moment you stopped becoming them.

`[slow]` Nobody keeps a diary of the things they didn't do. It turns out you've been keeping one for fifteen years. It's alphabetized.

And here's the design detail doing the work, the thing I want you to actually notice: the game *made* you keep it visible. A bigger bank — an infinite bank, the kind other games hand out like napkins — would let every one of these little failures sink out of sight forever, and the diary would go unwritten, because a diary with infinite pages and no need to reread is a landfill. Scarcity is what forces the rereading. Every "bank cleaning" is you, cursor hovering, holding a small object that is secretly a decision you once made, going: *is this still me?* `[beat]` The game has no journaling feature. It has something better. It has a shortage.

---

**Stratum three. Bedrock. The mementos — and the lobster's answer.**

At the bottom of the dig, under the working self and the abandoned selves, there's a layer where the logic changes. The items down here have no future tense at all. The burnt lobster. The holiday junk. The first rune scimitar, kept while its price fell to nothing, kept while power crept past it, kept.

Why does the lobster survive every cleaning? I promised an answer at the top, and it's this: `[slow]` the lobster is the only item in your bank with a *date* attached. Everything else is priced in gold or usefulness, and by both measures the lobster is worthless, which means the cleanings can't touch it — it isn't playing their game. Its value is denominated in a currency the Grand Exchange has never listed: it's the physical remains of an afternoon. A specific fire, a specific failure, a person you were at eleven years old, still faintly warm.

[VISUAL: the lobster slot again; beneath it, in small text, a date instead of a price]

Museums keep objects like this in every civilization's wing — the worn shoe, the child's toy, the things that survive precisely because nobody could assign them a value. Your bedrock tab is that wing. The stuff money can measure comes and goes above it, priced and repriced and eventually sold. The stuff at the bottom just accumulates, one object per era of you, `[beat]` and the game — this is the part I find genuinely moving about the design — the game lets it. Twenty-plus years of ruthless economic simulation, item sinks, deflation management, and there has always been room in the ledger for one burnt lobster, carried at a book value of zero, worth more than the rest of the tab.

---

**Now the turn, because an appraiser has to appraise, and the cold reading of everything I just said is: this is hoarding.**

Clinically, structurally, hoarding. Sentimental attachment to objects with no function, resistance to discarding, distress at the "bank is full" prompt — I'm describing a diagnostic checklist and also your tab nine. Marie Kondo would clear your entire archive in an afternoon and you'd thank her through tears. And the harder edge of the argument: the attachment has a beneficiary. An account you can't bear to abandon is an account that stays subscribed. Every memento is a tiny anchor, and the company that hosts your memories bills monthly for the visits — and the one time the idea of *selling* extra space was so much as floated to this community, the reaction resembled a structure fire. Players understood instinctively that something was wrong about pricing the archive, even if they couldn't say what. That instinct deserves a response, and "it's just pixels, calm down" is the response of someone who's never held their grandfather's watch, which is also just brass.

So here's mine, and it's an interpretation, labeled. Hoarding is attachment that has stopped meaning anything. The shoebox of ticket stubs in your actual closet is worthless to an appraiser too — its entire value is that *you* can read it, and only while you can. The bank works the same way, and the game's constraint is what keeps the reading honest: because space is short, everything that stays had to be *chosen*, over and over, cleaning after cleaning, against real pressure. `[slow]` An infinite archive preserves everything and means nothing. A finite one is a series of small votes about what your past is worth. The lobster has won every election for eighteen years. That's the difference between clutter and a diary. Clutter is what survives neglect. `[beat]` The lobster survived *attention*.

---

**Which brings us back down the dig, to the sentence I promised.**

*Bank's full.*

Two words. The game's politest refusal — and I'd argue the most honest sentence in it, because every other message in this game tells you about the world, and this one tells you about yourself: your past has a capacity, and you've reached it, and something old has to be worth less than something new, and *you* have to say which. No algorithm curates it for you. No cloud tier solves it for a fee — the community burned that idea on the front lawn. Just you, the cursor, and a museum where you're the only registrar. `[beat]` It's the game's whole philosophy in an error message: it will give you a world, and it will absolutely not tell you what any of it is worth.

One day — and I say this with love, the way the dig report says it — this account goes quiet. All accounts do; that's what makes them accounts of something. And when it does, the bank will hold its arrangement in the dark, every object exactly where the last cleaning left it. The working tab, the abandoned selves, the lobster at bedrock with its invisible date.

» A small museum with no visitors, curated by hand for twenty years, of a person. `[beat]` There are national archives that can't say as much.

[VISUAL: the bank interface, slowly desaturating to the dig-site sepia; the lobster slot last to fade]

Appraisal complete. Final valuation: nothing, on every market the game runs. `[beat]` Keep all of it.
