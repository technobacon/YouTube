# It Does Nothing — Full Script (Project B, #5)

**Thesis:** Boss pets — astronomically rare, untradeable, and functionally useless — are the purest status economy in gaming, and the uselessness is engineered: give a pet any utility and it acquires a gold-per-hour value, enters the spreadsheet, and dies as an object of desire. Untradeable plus useless equals incorruptible — the only receipt for time and luck that can neither be bought, faked, nor priced — and the fact that the most optimization-poisoned player base in gaming grinds hardest for the one item with zero yield is the cleanest evidence available of what everyone was actually playing for.
**Format:** Antiques appraisal table. Owners bring small creatures to be valued; the appraiser examines, asks the trade's two questions ("what does it do?" / "what did it cost?"), and the essay assembles itself out of the valuations. The final appraisal delivers the batch's second `[hold]`.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,000 words)
**Voice dial:** High. Appraiser's courtly precision over barely-contained wonder. One profanity (the peacock).
**Facts:** Untradeable/follower-only/no-stats status is definitional and safe. Individual pet drop rates, Probita's insurance mechanics, and skilling-pet specifics flagged below — rates stay hedged in prose per house style.

## SCRIPT

Bring it up. Set it on the felt — gently, there we are. Let's have a look at what you've carried in.

[VISUAL: appraisal-show staging — green felt, warm lamp; a tiny snakeling curls in the center of the table, inspecting the appraiser right back]

Mm. Yes. `[beat]` For the viewers at home: what we have here is a snakeling — juvenile form of a swamp god, roughly the length of a baguette — obtained, the owner tells me, on his four thousand one hundred and ninth kill of its mother. The trade has two questions we put to every object, so let's put them.

What does it do? `[beat]` Nothing. It follows you. That's the entire feature list — it walks behind you at a polite distance, forever. It has no stats. It grants no bonus. It cannot fight, carry, fetch, or farm. In four thousand kills' worth of a man's evenings it has produced, functionally, a small green shadow.

What would it fetch at market? Nothing again — beautifully nothing: it *cannot be sold*. Untradeable, permanently, to anyone, at any price. There is no market to fetch anything at.

So. An object with no function and no price — which, by every law of the appraisal trade, is a worthless object — `[slow]` and the owner would not part with it for a twisted bow, and he is not unusual, and there are people watching this who have done *ten thousand* kills chasing one and would trade him lives to have it.

» That contradiction is the lot we're actually appraising today. `[beat]` The most desired drops in Old School RuneScape do nothing, on purpose, and the nothing is the engineering.

Next item to the table, please. We'll work up to the theory.

---

**Lot one: the mechanics of nothing, catalogued.**

[VISUAL: a slow parade of pets across the felt — boss pets, skilling pets; hold the parade wide]

The category, for the record. Nearly every boss in the game carries, at the very bottom of its drop table, at odds I'll keep hedged in speech and exact on screen — broadly the one-in-thousands neighborhood, some far crueler — a miniature of itself. The skilling pets extend the madness to peaceful labor: chop enough trees across enough years and a beaver may simply decide you're worth following. All of them share the same three properties, and the properties are the design: untradeable — it can only be *earned*, at the source, by you; stat-less — it confers nothing; and a *follower* — its one behavior is public. It exists to be seen beside you.

Note what the designers withheld, because restraint this consistent is authorship. No pet buff. No pet battle system — the industry's favorite way to monetize companionship. No perk, however marginal, that would let a spreadsheet assign the creature a number. Two decades of updates, hundreds of pets, and the feature list has stayed at *walks behind you* with the discipline of a vow. `[beat]` Somebody understood something. The next lot is the something.

---

**Lot two: the peacock's tail.**

[VISUAL: one image — a peacock in full display — held through the section, slow drift]

A biologist named Amotz Zahavi spent the seventies arguing a thing that sounded backwards, about this bird. The peacock's tail is a catastrophe. It's heavy, it's conspicuous to predators, it's expensive to grow and worse to maintain — by every engineering standard the tail is a *defect*. Zahavi's insight — the handicap principle, flag the name for a card — is that the defect is the message. Any bird can be alive; it takes a magnificent idiot to be alive *while dragging a fucking chandelier*. The tail is unfakeable proof of surplus — only genuine fitness can afford genuine waste — and so waste, honestly displayed, becomes the most trustworthy signal in nature. Peahens read it fluently. So do humans: most of what your species calls luxury runs on the same protocol. The watch that does less than your phone and costs a hundred times more does less *as the point* — uselessness is where the signal lives, because useful things are merely *needed*, and anyone can need.

Now set the snakeling next to the peacock and the appraisal writes itself. A pet is expensive — thousands of hours of proven engagement at the drop source. A pet is conspicuous — it follows you through every bank, every lobby, visible at forty paces. And a pet is useless — *therefore* the expense can't be explained away as practical, `[slow]` which is precisely what makes the display trustworthy. Give the snakeling +2 damage and every snakeling in the world becomes ambiguous: earned trophy, or grudging efficiency purchase? The uselessness is what keeps the signal *pure*. The designers, whether they'd cite Zahavi or not, built the peacock's tail into a drop table — and the player base, whether they'd admit it or not, reads it as fluently as any peahen. Nobody has ever seen a pet and asked what it does. Everyone, always, asks the only question it was built to answer: `[beat]` *how long did that take you?*

---

**Lot three: the receipt.**

Which brings us to why it must never, ever be tradeable — and to my favorite building in this entire game.

Consider what happens to the signal the moment a pet can be sold. Instantly it has a price; instantly the price has a gold-per-hour route; instantly the question "how long did that take you" has a second possible answer, which is *none of your business, I bought it* — and the object dies. Every partyhat-shaped asset in gaming history tells the same story: tradeable rarity signals *wealth*, and wealth is a fine thing to signal, and it is a completely different thing from what the pet certifies, which is *presence*. The pet is a receipt for hours — your hours, at that boss, in that chair, with those evenings — and receipts are only worth anything if they can't be forged. Untradeability is the anti-counterfeiting measure. The snakeling is a document, `[slow]` and the document says: *I was there. No one can have been there for me.*

And the game — this is the detail I'd frame and hang — takes the documentation *seriously*. In East Ardougne there is an office, an actual NPC with an actual ledger, a woman named Probita, flag her mechanics, whose entire profession is *insuring these pets*. A registration fee, a reclaim fee if your pet is lost with you in the field. `[beat]` Insurance. For the stat-less. The economy that will price a feather has no number for this creature, so the game built it the other kind of institution — the kind civilizations reserve for things whose loss can't be reimbursed, only grieved. You don't insure a pet's *value*. There isn't any. You insure the years it notarizes. The premium is trivial and everyone pays it, and if you want one sentence that explains this entire category of object, it's a queue of millionaires outside a small office in Ardougne, paying real fees to protect nothing at all.

---

**The turn. Because the auction house has a skeptic, and she's been patient.**

Here's the case against everything on this table, at full strength. The pet is a Skinner ornament — a lottery ticket Jagex prints at zero marginal cost, stapled to content you were already grinding, that converts finished bosses into infinite treadmills. It costs the company nothing, extends engagement by *thousands of hours per player*, monetizes via subscription time, and outsources its entire value to the players' own status anxiety. The kill counts stop reading as passion and start reading as pathology — the dry streaks, the account-years spent at one boss for a green shadow. On this reading the pet is the most cynical item in the game: pure engagement machinery, wearing a bow. And the status economy it powers is just cosplay hierarchy — pixels certifying pixels, peacocks all the way down.

Two answers, one small, one the actual close. The small one: every charge above applies with more force to items that *do* things — the treadmill's true engine is the gear ladder, where the grind is *mandatory* for progression; the pet treadmill is the only one in the game you can simply decline, and most players do, and the ones who don't have chosen their altar with open eyes. Informed, optional, honest about its odds — as lotteries go, this one is nearly a gentleman.

The larger answer needs the whole table cleared. `[slow]` Look at who chases these. The efficiency-brained. The spreadsheet people. The population that has optimized *everything* — who know the hourly value of every activity in the game to three significant figures — voluntarily sinking their thousands of hours into the one item their own framework values at exactly zero. The turn's accusation is that the pet exploits them. Stand somewhere else and the same facts read as the opposite: the pet is where the optimizers go to *defect* — the single line item their religion cannot audit, the one grind that is provably, mathematically, not for anything. Strip a game to pure rationality and the rationalists themselves will carve out one shrine to the useless and guard it like a border. `[beat]` The demand for the meaningless is the most meaningful data this game has ever produced. People needed a place to put the part of themselves the spreadsheet couldn't hold. Jagex sold them a snakeling. Everyone understood the transaction perfectly, and no one has ever once said it out loud at the bank.

---

**Final appraisal.**

The owner would like his valuation. Of course. It's a valuation show; there must be a number. Let me total the lot.

[VISUAL: the snakeling on the felt, alone, the lamp warm on it; nothing else in frame]

Function: nothing. Market price: unpriceable — no market exists or ever will. Materials: some green pixels, a follower script from 2014. Provenance: `[beat]` four thousand one hundred and nine evenings' worth of one man's finite attention, notarized by odds no forger would sit through, insured in Ardougne against every death but the real one. Final value:

`[hold]`

Everything you did to get it. Which is to say: nothing. `[beat]` Which is to say — everything this game has.

We waive the fee for lots like this. The trade has a rule: you don't charge for appraising the priceless, partly out of respect, `[slow]` mostly because the appraisal was never for the owner. He always knew. The appraisal is for the crowd — for everyone standing behind the rope who's spent twenty years asking what things *do* and what things *fetch*, and who needed, just once, to watch both questions fail in public and the value survive.

» Take it home the long way, would you. Through the bank, past everyone. `[beat]` That's what it's *for*.

Next item to the table, please.

[VISUAL: the owner walks off through a crowded bank, the snakeling trailing him at its polite distance; hold the follow shot all the way out. Cut.]
