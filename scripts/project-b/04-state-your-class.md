# State Your Class — Full Script (Project B, #4)

**Thesis:** OSRS has no classes — no builds, no specs, no respec, no choice at creation that closes a single door — and instead of dissolving character identity (the thing classes exist to manufacture), the omission produced the strongest identity in the genre: an OSRS character is not a *pick*, it's a *record*, and the difference between those two words is the most radical design decision the game never announced. The turn faces the real cost: classes force interdependence, and a world of complete accounts is a world of the profoundly alone-together.
**Format:** Census. A census taker at your door with a form, and the CLASS field will not accept any of your answers. The essay advances by the form failing — each rejected answer opens a section — until the field is resolved in the only way it can be.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,000 words)
**Voice dial:** High. Bureaucratic patience fraying into wonder. One profanity (the trinity's history).
**Facts:** Structural claims (one character, all skills available, no respec, combat styles as gear rather than identity) are definitional and safe; genre-history claims (trinity origins, class-lock conventions) kept at prose altitude and flagged; RuneScape Classic's classlessness flagged for a history card.

## SCRIPT

There's a knock, and it's the census. Sensible coat, government clipboard, the tired kindness of someone on their four-thousandth door. The form is short. Name of account — fine. Date of creation — you have to count backwards, and the number is upsetting, but fine. Then the third field.

[VISUAL: the form itself, filling the frame — NAME, CREATED, and then: CLASS: ______ — the cursor blinking in the empty field]

*Class.*

And if you play almost any other game with levels in it, this field is nothing. It's the *first* thing you know about a character — before the name, sometimes. Warrior. Mage. Priest. Necromancer. The word you clicked at minute zero, the word that decided your buttons, your gear, your role, your friends, arguably your personality. Forty years of role-playing games run through that field. The census taker's pen is ready.

`[beat]` And you play Old School RuneScape, so you stand in your doorway, and you realize the question doesn't parse.

» There is no answer. There has never been an answer. A twenty-year-old game in the most class-obsessed genre ever built simply *does not have the field* — `[beat]` and the empty box, I'll argue, is the most radical design decision in the game, made so early and with so little ceremony that nobody remembers it being made at all.

The census taker waits. You're going to attempt three answers. The form is going to reject all three, and each rejection is a section of this video, and by the end the field gets resolved — just not the way forms like to be.

---

**Attempt one. You say: "all of them."**

The pen doesn't move. *"All of them" is not a class,* says the census taker, correctly. But your answer was accurate: your one character can train every skill in the game. Melee today, magic tonight, and there's no *character* reason not to — no lockout, no penalty, no rebirth required. To feel how strange that is, you need to know why the class field exists at all, and it exists for three load-carrying reasons that most players have never seen stated.

Reason one: **identity, fast.** A new character is nobody. A class is a personality you can wear in the first minute — the game hands you a noun, and humans will die for a noun. Reason two: **interdependence.** This is the deep one. The classic trinity — tank, healer, damage — is a social technology: make each class incomplete on purpose, and players *need* each other; the healer cannot tank, therefore the healer has friends. Whole communities, whole marriages, got built on engineered incompleteness. It worked. It fucking worked, for decades — that's the embarrassing part for everything I'm about to argue. Reason three: **a balance surface.** A designer can't tune ten million individuals; she can tune twelve classes. The class is a handle. Remember all three, because the turn comes back for the second one with feelings.

So the class field is structural engineering, wearing a menu's clothes. Which makes the game that shipped without it either negligent `[beat]` or onto something. The census taker taps the form.

---

**Attempt two. You say: "I'm… a maxed main? A mid-game zerker-ish thing? I'm whatever I've done?"**

*Closer,* says the census taker, *but "whatever I've done" is not a class.* And here — right here — is the whole video.

Because "whatever I've done" is exactly what an OSRS character is, and the game has a document that proves it. Open the stats page. `[slow]` Twenty-three numbers, each one a fossil record of hours — and no two long-running accounts have the same shape, because no two players lived the same years. Here's the account with 99 fletching from 2006 because that's what you did while your mum watched television; the quest cape earned in a fever-month; the agility level that is, frankly, a confession. Now run the comparison that kills the class system's whole premise: take two maxed accounts. Identical stats, every number the same, the class-designer's nightmare of total homogeneity. Talk to their owners for thirty seconds. `[beat]` They are not remotely the same character. One is an ex-pker gone straight; one has never left a bank without a spreadsheet; their kill counts, their firsts, their scars — the *identity* survived identical stats completely intact, because the identity was never in the numbers. It was in the *route*. A class answers "what did you pick?" An account answers "what did you do?" — a pick takes one click and means nothing yet; a record takes fifteen years and means nothing *else*.

The genre spent four decades manufacturing identity at character creation because it assumed identity had to be *issued*. OSRS demonstrates it can be *accreted* — that if you refuse to hand players a noun, and wait, they will each grow one, unique, non-transferable, spelled in gamma-ray detail across two decades of choices. The class was a shortcut to a self. The account is the long way. The long way turns out to be the only way that produces the real thing —

and it has a practical consequence that class-game refugees describe like a religious experience. Attempt three.

---

**Attempt three. You say: "depends on the boss, honestly."**

*That,* says the census taker, *is a schedule, not a class.* True. Also: the single greatest quality-of-life fact in the game, and nobody frames it this way.

In a class game, your pick can be *wrong*. Not morally — mechanically, later, retroactively: the meta shifts, the patch lands, your spec is dead weight in the group finder, and the noun you chose at minute zero has become a tax. The industry's fix is the respec, the reroll, the boost — each one a small institutionalized apology, each one whispering that some of your invested self needs deleting to stay current. `[quick]` Class games run on invalidation the way economies run on depreciation; there is always a wrong thing to be, and it is regularly you. `[/quick]`

OSRS cannot invalidate you. There is nothing to invalidate — no spec to nerf into the ground, no pick to regret, no wrong character, only a character that hasn't trained *that* yet. The meta shifts and you… swap gear. Ranged is broken this year? You already own ranged; it's a stat, and it's yours, sitting at whatever number your history left it. `[slow]` Nothing you have ever trained stops being yours, and nothing the balance team does can reach back into your years and mark them wasted. Jagex tunes items — the handle problem solved without touching a single identity — and twenty years of sunk time compounds instead of depreciating. The class game asks *who do you want to be?* and then charges you rent on the answer. This game asks nothing `[beat]` and keeps everything.

The census taker has stopped writing. She's listening now, which is how you know the turn is here — because everything I've said has a shadow, and she's been standing in it.

---

**The turn. What the trinity bought, and what the empty field costs.**

Go back to reason two. Engineered incompleteness — the healer who cannot tank, and therefore has friends. Grief it properly: that design manufactured *need*, and need manufactured society. You logged in because five incomplete people were incomplete without you specifically, tonight, at eight. The class was a chair at a table.

An OSRS account is complete. All twenty-three skills, one body, no gaps for another person to fill — and a world of complete beings needs a reason to gather that mere mechanics no longer supply. Be honest about the result: Old School is massively *single-player* for enormous stretches; a thousand adjacent solitudes, each grinding its own biography, alone together at the same bank. The empty class field costs the game the trinity's automatic warmth, and anyone who's stood in a city of players needing none of them has felt the price. That loneliness is real, and it's structural, and I won't argue it away.

Two things anyway. First: the need didn't vanish — it moved *up a layer*. Nobody needs your spec, so what OSRS players trade instead is knowledge, prices, labor, company: the economy needs thousands of strangers; the wiki is need, formalized; even the essay-about-a-beach on this channel is about players inventing law because they need each other at a *social* level the mechanics refuse to script. Chosen need, so to speak, rather than engineered need — thinner, but voluntary, and voluntary warmth has a different temperature. Second, and stranger: watch what players *do* with the freedom. They build cages. Skillers who never fight; one-defence accounts; snowflake rules of every flavor; an entire prestige mode about renouncing the economy — there's a whole video on it, one door down. Handed a game with no classes, a visible fraction of the population immediately *invents* them, hand-carves their own constraint, and wears it as a name. `[beat]` Which suggests the class was answering a real hunger after all. The empty field doesn't deny the hunger. It just refuses to pre-chew the answer.

---

**The field, resolved.**

So the census taker is still at your door, and the form still wants its word, and here is the only honest thing left to write in it.

[VISUAL: the form again, the blinking field; then the pen writes — slowly, in full — the account's name]

Your name. The field takes your *name* — because in the absence of a class, the account became one: a class with exactly one member, its skill priorities set by your teenage boredom, its strengths shaped like your commutes and your heartbreaks and your one obsessive summer, its build guide unwritable because its build was a life. Forty years of games asked *warrior or mage* because it was fast. This one handed you a blank form in 2004 `[slow]` and has been watching you fill it in ever since — every level a letter, every quest a line — the longest character-creation screen ever built, still open, still saving.

» You never chose a class. `[beat]` You became one.

The census taker closes the folder. She doesn't thank you; it's government work. But at the gate she stops, and looks back at the door, and says the only editorial sentence she's allowed per household:

*Most forms are finished when they're filled in. Yours is the other kind.*

[VISUAL: the stats page, all 23 skills, slow drift — held long enough to read your whole self into it. Cut.]
