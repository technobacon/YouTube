# In Defense of the Boat — Full Script (Project W, #2)

**Thesis:** Distance was vanilla WoW's invisible systems designer. Travel time priced every decision, gave zones a felt temperature, and made the world big in the only unit that matters — minutes — and every convenience shipped since (summons, portals, flight, finders) has traded those minutes for weightlessness at an exchange rate nobody audited. A world you can be anywhere in instantly is a world you're nowhere in, ever.
**Format:** Real-time voyage. The video lasts exactly one boat crossing (Auberdine → Feathermoon, flagged); an on-screen timer runs the whole way; the essay's beats are timed to the trip; we dock as the video ends.
**Runtime:** ~13 minutes (~1,950 words) — final cut must match the verified crossing time; see flags.
**Voice dial:** High, and *slow*. This is the batch's meditative one; the pace is the argument. exurb1a's "trust the meditative pace" rule applies literally.
**Facts:** Boat route, crossing duration, and hearthstone cooldown all flagged — none stated precisely in prose.

## SCRIPT

This video lasts exactly one boat ride.

[VISUAL: the dock at Auberdine, night; the boat pulls in; a small timer fades in top-left at 00:00 and never leaves]

That's the deal. The boat behind me runs from Auberdine, on the coast of Darkshore, to Feathermoon Stronghold, off the far side of Feralas — close to the longest scheduled journey in vanilla World of Warcraft, and we are taking all of it. No cuts in the crossing. When we dock, the video ends. If you're the kind of person who checks a video's runtime before committing… `[beat]` you've just done the exact thing this essay is about.

Board, please. We're leaving.

In 2005 you took this ride because there was no other option. No flight connected these coasts. Your hearthstone was set somewhere useful, which meant somewhere else. If a guild group needed you in Feralas, you walked to a dock, and you waited, and then you stood on a boat while an entire ocean happened to you in real time. Tens of thousands of people did this every day and called it playing a video game.

» And I want to argue that this — the waiting, the water, the nothing — was the best systems designer Blizzard ever employed. `[beat]` Distance. And they've spent twenty years firing him a little at a time.

We're going to pass a lot of nothing on this trip. Keep an eye on it. The nothing is the argument, and I'll pay that off before we land.

---

**First — check the timer, we've got a while — distance prices decisions.**

[VISUAL: the open sea; the timer ticking; a map inset tracing the route so far]

Here's a question that has disappeared from modern games without anyone filing a report: *is it worth going?* In vanilla, Feralas is this boat plus the walk on both ends. Forty-odd minutes of your evening, round trip, give or take your faction and your corpse count. So when someone says "we need you in Feralas," a real calculation runs. What's there. What it costs. What you're giving up. Going somewhere *means* something, because it forecloses going somewhere else.

That's pricing. Every economist on Earth will tell you the same boring, latheringly true thing: a cost is information. Vanilla's travel costs told you, constantly, involuntarily, what things were worth to you — and the vocabulary players invented proves they could hear it. "Is it worth the hearth?" An entire idiom, native to this game, meaning: this teleport exists once an hour, and I am deciding whether *you* deserve it. The hearthstone matters precisely because it barely exists. One charge. One home. A cooldown long enough that spending it is a small confession of priorities.

[VISUAL: the hearthstone tooltip; then chat logs — "worth the hearth?", "save your hearth", "hearthing, gnight"]

Remove the cost and the information goes with it. A world with free instant movement still has places, technically — coordinates, biomes, very nice skyboxes. What it no longer has is *far*. And "far" was doing work no skybox can do.

---

**Because — second thing — distance is what gave zones a temperature.**

You felt it and you know you felt it. Stranglethorn was hot with danger and far from help in a way no tooltip stated. Winterspring felt like the edge of the map because getting there cost you an afternoon the first time. Every zone's identity was partly its art and partly its music and largely — I'd say mostly — its *remoteness*, the accumulated minutes between it and safety. Distance was a lighting rig. It lit some places cozy and some places lonesome, and the map meant something because the lighting never lied.

And the game's answer to distance, the flight path, is my favorite piece of accidental cinema in the medium. You paid a small fee to be strapped to a bird and shown the world in continuous shot — no loading screen, no cut, the camera swinging low over zones you'd bled in and zones you hadn't earned yet. People screenshotted the *commute*. `[beat]` Think about that. The most photographed loading screen in history, because it had the decency to actually be the world.

[VISUAL: period flight-path footage, the long swing over Thousand Needles; let it breathe with no narration for a few seconds]

I'll tell you the other thing the flight path and this boat gave you, and then I'll tell you about the day it went wrong. They gave you *legal boredom*. Sanctioned, scheduled, unpunishable idleness. Go make a sandwich. Do the reading you were avoiding. Talk. The game held your place in the world while you went and briefly had a life — which sounds like a flaw, and functioned like mercy.

And when it went wrong — you know this scene. You come sprinting down the hill to Auberdine, you can *see* the boat, sails full, pulling out with your evening on board, because you alt-tabbed for thirty seconds at the wrong moment. And there's a word for what you feel, alone on the pier, and the word is: *fuck.* `[beat]` Ten more minutes. Ten more minutes of standing in a beautiful place with nothing to do.

`[slow]` There are worse sentences a game can hand you. I'm not sure there are better ones.

---

**Because here's the third thing — check the timer — nobody stood on that pier alone for long.**

Docks in vanilla were social condensers. So were flight masters, and gryphon landings, and the zeppelin tower with its terrible ETA. Anywhere the game made people wait together, conversation grew — because talk is the natural fauna of a queue. Trades happened. Duels happened. Guilds recruited off piers. Strangers compared gear because the alternative was silence, and twenty minutes of shared silence is socially illegal in every culture on Earth.

The previous video in this batch called warlock summoning a test of whether anybody likes you; the boat was the control group. No test at all. Just time, held in common. You cannot design "community" — I keep arriving at this from different directions — but you can design *waiting rooms*, and vanilla built them everywhere, and the community grew in them like moss in the grout.

[VISUAL: dock crowds, duel circles outside Orgrimmar, the zeppelin tower cluster]

---

**Now the turn, and it's the one this whole essay has been dreading: every word of this is easy for me to say.**

Every love letter ever written to "the journey" was written by somebody whose time was worthless. We were fourteen. Boredom was our native climate; an evening cost us nothing because we had thousands of them in the bank and no idea. The person watching this now has ninety minutes, a job, and someone asleep upstairs. Telling that person the boat was good is very close to telling them their scarcity is a character flaw. Retail's portal rooms are accessibility for adulthood, and sneering at them is a luxury belief.

And it gets worse for me: the people who actually lived in 2005 spent the whole era trying to leave it. Mages sold portals in trade chat. Warlocks ran taxi services. Everyone logged out at the flight master, parked alts at both ends, begged summons. The residents of the museum were tunneling out the entire time — so the revealed preference argument from video one shows up here too, wearing a life jacket.

`[slow]` I don't think I get to fully win this. Here's what I'll hold instead.

The tunneling was part of the system. A shortcut is only a shortcut where there's a long way — the mage's portal had a price *because* the boat existed, the summon was a favor *because* it saved something real. Vanilla's escapes from distance all cost gold or goodwill, which is to say they got priced back into the world. Modern travel is free, and free is the one price that carries no information at all.

» And on the time question — the fourteen-year-olds were right by accident. Not about having time to burn. About what burning it together turns into.

---

**We're coming in. You can see Feathermoon from here. Last thing, while they tie us up.**

There are two ways to measure a world. Meters and minutes. Meters survive anything — every teleport, every portal, every design regret; the coastline never shrinks in meters. Minutes are fragile. Minutes die to a single patch note. Azeroth is exactly as many meters across as it was in 2004, and it has been getting smaller in minutes for twenty years, and only one of those numbers is the size of a *place*.

This boat still runs, by the way. Classic Era servers, right now, tonight. Same route, same schedule, mostly empty. A ghost with a timetable. `[beat]` It'll run whether you're on it or not — which, if you've been watching this batch in order, you already know is the whole point of that other essay.

You knew this schedule once. Some part of you still does — some part of you has been checking our timer against a memory this entire ride and finding it accurate.

[VISUAL: the boat slides into Feathermoon dock; the timer stops as the hull touches]

`[slow]` We're here. Thanks for riding.

Everybody off.
