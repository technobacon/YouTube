# Raiding Was a Job — Full Script (Project W, #3)

**Thesis:** Molten Core was an easy video game and a brutally hard institution. The real boss of vanilla raiding was the calendar — forty humans, same night, every week, for months — and the systems players built to beat it (DKP, attendance law, loot councils, officer hierarchies) are the best game design of the era. Blizzard shipped the dungeon; the players shipped the civilization.
**Format:** Corporate onboarding video. HR-handbook deadpan — the viewer is a new hire being walked through their unpaid position; the pastiche frames every section (benefits, org chart, payroll, exit interview).
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,100 words)
**Voice dial:** High. The deadpan does the comedy; the exit interview does the tenderness.
**Facts:** No boss counts, dates, or clear-times in prose; subscription price, 2019 clear speeds, DKP origin all flagged.

## SCRIPT

Welcome aboard. We're delighted you've accepted the position.

[VISUAL: 1990s corporate training-video pastiche — grainy, warm VHS colors, a smiling stock-footage HR woman gesturing at a conference room that slowly reveals itself to be Molten Core]

Your shift runs Tuesday, seven to eleven. Also Thursday. Also Sunday, if progress requires it, and progress always requires it. The role is unpaid. You will in fact be paying *us* — a monthly fee, plus expenses, and the expenses are potions. Dress code is mandatory and drops from your workplace, which is on fire. Performance reviews are continuous, public, and conducted by thirty-nine of your closest friends.

Please don't sign anything yet. `[beat]` There's an exit interview at the end of this orientation, and I'd like you to make it that far first.

Because here's what this video is actually about. Molten Core — the raid, the game-design artifact, the encounter list — was easy. Genuinely, measurably easy, and I'll defend that. What was hard, what was in fact one of the hardest things the medium has ever asked of ordinary people, was the *institution* required to walk forty humans into it every single week.

» Ragnaros was arithmetic with a health bar. The real boss was the calendar.

And the systems invented to fight the calendar — the points, the policies, the courts of loot law — came from the players. All of it. Keep that in your packet; we'll return to it in payroll.

---

**Orientation, part one: your org chart.**

[VISUAL: an actual corporate org chart assembling — Guild Master at top, officers, class leads, raiders, with titles like "Director of Not Standing in Fire"]

Forty people is a strange number of humans. It's past the point where friendship scales — past the size where everyone can know everyone — and right at the size where organizations grow bones whether you ask them to or not. Hierarchy condenses out of forty people like weather. You get leadership, and delegation, and a class-lead layer, and an officer channel, which is just middle management with demon summoning.

Now here's the part younger viewers won't believe. Vanilla shipped almost no tools for any of this. No built-in calendar for years. No raid planner. No logs worth the name, at first. The game asked guilds to solve a forty-body scheduling problem across school nights and time zones and gave them a chat box.

So players built the rest themselves, off-site, out of whatever the mid-2000s internet had lying around. Guild forums on borrowed hosting. Attendance spreadsheets. Sign-up threads with rules-lawyering in the replies. Phone trees — actual phone trees — for when the main tank's internet died at 6:45. Somebody's older brother ran the Ventrilo server, and that made him infrastructure. `[beat]` An entire shadow IT department, staffed by volunteers, administering a volcano.

The raid was three hours. The institution was all week. You didn't join a guild to raid; the raiding was the *excuse*, the gravity that held the thing together — though nobody would've said so at the time, and I'm getting ahead of the exit interview.

---

**Part two, and roll the disclaimer, because this is the one that starts arguments: the game itself was easy.**

Look at the encounter design with cold eyes. Most bosses in that raid ask for a handful of behaviors. Stand there. Don't stand there. Kill the small ones first. Cure the curse when the curse appears. The mechanical vocabulary of the entire tier fits on an index card, and I mean that with affection. The proof arrived in 2019, when Classic relaunched and guilds walked into Molten Core in their leveling gear — greens, blues, fishing hats, fifteen years of accumulated knowledge — and the tier died inside its first week. `[beat]` The encounter, held constant since 2005, had been waiting all that time to testify.

[VISUAL: side-by-side — 2005 footage of a wipe on Magmadar, 2019 footage of the same boss folding to players in quest greens]

So what, exactly, was hard in 2005?

Simultaneity. `[slow]` Getting one human to not stand in fire is a tutorial. Getting forty humans to not stand in fire, at the same moment, at eleven p.m., for the ninth consecutive Tuesday, through disconnects and homework and new babies and one guy whose cat is *always* on the keyboard — that's the actual encounter, and no strategy guide on Earth could carry you through it. The fights tested execution barely. They tested *institutions* absolutely: recruitment pipelines for when people quit, discipline for when people slack, morale for when the volcano wins.

And the volcano did win, some nights. You know the night. Four hours. Zero bosses down. The same hunter pulled the room twice — the same hunter — and the raid leader's voice has gone quiet in the way that's worse than yelling, and at midnight you all agree to try again Thursday. And you log in Thursday. Of course you do. `[beat]` It's your fucking job.

Every wipe night was a stress test of the org chart, and the guilds that cleared weren't the ones with the best players. They were the ones whose institution didn't crack.

---

**Part three: payroll. This is my favorite department.**

Forty people, one flaming sword. The loot problem is the oldest problem in cooperative play — scarce reward, collective effort — and it is genuinely hard: give the sword to the wrong person and the institution you just built starts dissolving in the acid of perceived injustice.

Blizzard's shipped solution was a dice roll.

The players' solution was an economy. DKP — dragon kill points, a system older than this game, carried over from the EverQuest raiding diaspora like a legal tradition crossing an ocean. Show up, earn points. Want the sword, spend points. And the moment guilds implemented it, they inherited every problem real economies have, and — this is the astonishing part — they reinvented real solutions, from scratch, unpaid, on forums, between shifts.

[VISUAL: an actual period DKP spreadsheet, names and balances; treat it like an archival document, slow pan]

Inflation, when points flow in faster than loot flows out — so guilds invented decay, which any economist would recognize as a wealth tax, applied weekly. Hoarding, when a rogue sits on a mountain of points for one specific dagger — price theory arriving uninvited. Zero-sum systems versus fixed-price systems versus silent auctions. Some guilds abandoned markets entirely for loot councils, which is central planning, complete with central planning's failure mode: the politburo's nephew gets the sword, and the appeals process is a forum thread with two hundred replies.

» Ordinary people — teenagers, plumbers, grad students — ran fiscal policy experiments on their own friendships, every week, for years. Nobody assigned this. The vacuum assigned it. Blizzard shipped a scarcity and forty people at a time built a state around it.

There's a video on this channel about a game whose developer runs its economy like a central bank. Vanilla ran the same experiment with the sign flipped: the developer abdicated, and the citizens turned out to be better economists than anyone would've bet.

---

**Before your exit interview, the part HR would rather skip. The dark reading, and it's strong.**

All of this — the attendance sheets, the points, the Tuesday liturgy — has another name, and the name is *coercion*. An attendance policy is a threat with formatting. The points you accumulated were golden handcuffs: leave the guild, lose the balance, so people stayed years past the fun. Miss a raid for your own life and feel a guilt that no game should be able to generate — except the game didn't generate it, did it. The guild did. Forty people you'd have to look at afterward did.

Here's the version of the accusation I can't fully dodge: Blizzard collected a subscription every month, and the thing that actually kept people subscribed — the retention mechanism, the churn-killer — was unpaid emotional labor performed by the customers on each other. Your guild leader was doing workforce management for a corporation, for free, out of love, and the corporation banked it. Name another industry where that sentence is even possible.

`[slow]` I have two honest things to say back, and neither one is a rebuttal.

First: every institution that has ever mattered runs on exactly this fuel. Obligation, attendance, guilt, showing up when you'd rather not — that's a church, a union, a family dinner, a Tuesday raid. We don't call those systems coercive until we've decided they're not worth it. So the question was never whether raiding used obligation. It's what the obligation bought.

Second: we ran the experiment. Modern WoW removed the coercion — flexible sizes, matchmade raiding, cross-server everything, log in whenever, owe nobody anything. Every individual change was humane and correct, and I'd defend most of them. And the institution, offered optionality, dissolved on contact. Attendance stopped meaning anything, so it stopped binding anything. The game got kinder and the village got thinner, and both of those are just true, at the same time, and it would take a braver essayist than me to tell you the trade was wrong. `[beat]` What I can tell you is that nobody priced it before paying.

---

**Alright. Exit interview. Sit down, there's cake — someone's mage made it.**

The guild you're leaving produced nothing, in twenty years, that the world can see. No product. No revenue. The forum is a parked domain now. The DKP database — those balances everyone fought over, hours of your one finite life, banked as numbers in a spreadsheet — lives on a hard drive that hasn't spun since the Bush administration. By every metric on your onboarding paperwork, total loss.

And the people are still here. That's the thing the paperwork never had a field for. The Ventrilo brother is godfather to a raider's kid. Half the officer channel is in a group chat that has outlived three games, two marriages, and the concept of Ventrilo. People flew across countries for guildmates' weddings, and — the part I'll say once and move past — some of them, eventually, for funerals. Twenty years of Tuesdays, and the only deliverable that survived is each other.

» It was the worst job you ever loved, and the benefits package was people.

[VISUAL: the HR set again, but the lights are half down; the stock-footage woman is gone; on the conference table, a raid roster with most names highlighted and a few gone grey]

Please initial here to acknowledge that your workplace was on fire. Turn in your badge — you can keep the sword, nobody else can use it now anyway.

`[beat]` See you Tuesday.
