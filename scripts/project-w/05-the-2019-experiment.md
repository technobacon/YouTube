# The 2019 Experiment — Full Script (Project W, #5)

**Thesis:** WoW Classic was, accidentally, one of the largest controlled experiments ever run on nostalgia: it held the game constant and varied only the player, and the results falsified the hypothesis everyone launched with ("it was the game"). What the experiment actually measured was the distance between you and your younger self — and its strangest finding is that people, having met that distance face to face, mostly stayed anyway.
**Format:** Lab report. Hypothesis → methods → results → confounds → revised conclusion; on-screen lab-notebook cards frame each section.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,100 words)
**Voice dial:** High, and the batch's most tender close. Carries Project W's single `[hold]`.

## SCRIPT

In the summer of 2019, several million people volunteered for an experiment.

[VISUAL: a lab notebook cover, hand-labeled: "SUBJECT: HOME. N = MILLIONS." Then period footage — the login queues, the packed starting zones]

No consent forms. No control group. No ethics board — there's no board on Earth that would have approved it. Just a hypothesis, and the hypothesis was never written down because every participant already believed it, the way you believe things that hurt: `[slow]` *it was the game.* The thing we lost was the game. They changed it, patch by patch, year by year, until the place we lived stopped existing — so put the game back, exactly as it was, and the feeling comes back with it.

Blizzard, to its enormous credit and possibly by accident, agreed to run the protocol. The servers opened in August. The queues were hours long — people took the day off work to wait in a line inside a video game — and the results, `[beat]` the results took about six weeks to come in.

» This video is about what the experiment actually found. It's the best data we will ever have on the question "can you go home," and almost nobody read the printout.

---

**METHODS.**

[VISUAL: lab-notebook card: "METHODS." Underneath, in pen: "put everything back."]

Understand how unusual the setup was. When the community spent years demanding classic servers, the demand crystallized into a single fundamentalist slogan: no changes. No rebalancing, no conveniences, no modern affordances quietly slipped in — the artifact, untouched, or nothing. And the museum, for once in the history of museums, agreed. The game shipped from archived data as it stood near the end of the original era. The talent trees, the drop rates, the unforgiving everything: restored to the decimal.

Which means — and this is why I keep calling it an experiment rather than a rerelease — the design achieved something research almost never gets. `[slow]` It held the entire world constant. Every number, every rock, every spawn timer, frozen at 2005. If vanilla-the-experience failed to reappear, the cause could not be the game, because the game was, to the limit of engineering, *identical*.

One variable could not be controlled.

`[beat]` The variable walked in the door wearing 2005's body, carrying fifteen years.

Fifteen years of guides. Wikis that document every spawn to the minute. Spreadsheets that had solved every class by 2007. YouTube, Discord, streamers, speedrun culture, parse culture — an entire civilization of accumulated knowing, none of which existed the first time, all of which logged in on day one wearing a fishing hat.

---

**RESULTS. Part one: the fog never stood a chance.**

[VISUAL: card: "RESULTS (i)." Footage: 2019 Molten Core, a raid in greens and blues folding a boss]

In 2005, the endgame took the population months to crack. Guilds broke on it; the volcano was a frontier, and the frontier was mostly fog — nobody *knew* anything, and the not-knowing was thick enough to live in.

In 2019, the same volcano, the same bosses, byte for byte, fell inside the first week. To players in leveling gear. `[beat]` Fifteen years of homework walked into a 2005 raid, and the fog never stood a fucking chance.

Now be careful with what that result means, because the obvious reading — "players got better" — is the shallow one. The deep reading is that difficulty, the *experienced* thing, the frontier-feeling, had been living somewhere other than where everyone assumed. Everyone thought it lived in the game, in the numbers, in the bosses. The experiment held all of that constant and the difficulty evaporated anyway. `[slow]` Which means it was never stored in the game at all. It was stored in the collective not-knowing — and not-knowing is the one asset no archive can hold.

**Part two: and then the subjects rebuilt their own cage.**

Within weeks, the culture of 2019 fully colonized the body of 2005. Dungeon leveling industrialized into assembly lines — mages boosting strangers through instances for gold, efficiency as a service. Raid preparation turned into logistics: world buffs, the server-wide blessings that older players treated as a nice Tuesday surprise, became mandatory pre-raid procurement, planned in spreadsheets, hoarded by logging out at precisely the right second. And over all of it, the parse: every raider's output graded to two decimal places against the entire planet, every Tuesday a take-home exam with a public transcript.

[VISUAL: split — 2005 raid footage, chaotic, joyful, badly played; 2019 footage of the same fight, immaculate, choreographed, silent]

None of this was cheating. Every one of these behaviors runs on rules that existed, dormant, in 2005 — the world buffs were always there, the mage AoE always worked. The 2019 players simply played the 2005 game *correctly*, for the first time, the way a chess engine plays chess correctly. And anyone who logged in looking for the old feeling watched it recede exactly as fast as their server's spreadsheet culture matured, which took about six weeks.

**THE FINDING**, then, entered in the notebook in block capitals:

» Identical code. Different game. We kept every plank of the ship, `[beat]` and the river changed.

Heraclitus said you can't step in the same river twice — which was him showing off, frankly, a man who never had to sit through a login queue — but the experiment sharpened his point into something more uncomfortable. You can't log into the same server twice. Even when it's bit-for-bit the same server. `[slow]` *Especially* then. Because when the world is held perfectly still, there's only one place left for the change to have happened.

Hold that thought. First, the part of the report where I have to attack my own methodology.

---

**CONFOUNDS. There are two, and they're serious.**

[VISUAL: card: "CONFOUNDS," underlined twice, coffee ring]

First: the sample was contaminated. The 2005 population arrived by accident — kids, roommates, someone's mom — nobody knew what an MMO was, and discovery was ambient because ignorance was universal. The 2019 population *self-selected*: nostalgics chasing a feeling, tourists chasing a headline, streamers chasing the tourists. You cannot re-run a first kiss with volunteers who applied for the position. Any purist can argue the experiment never tested "vanilla," only "vanilla as visited by people who already knew the ending." Fair. Logged.

Second: the control wasn't perfect. The 2019 build ran on modern architecture, with modern population layering at launch and other under-the-hood divergences the community argued about at forensic length. Small deltas, but a purist gets to log those too.

And yet — here's why I don't think the confounds save the hypothesis — every contaminant *pushed the same direction as the finding*. Knowledge, self-selection, modern infrastructure: all of it should have made 2019 feel *less* like 2005, and it did, and the game's constancy is precisely what lets us see it. The experiment is dirty. The signal survives it.

But there's a counter-result in the data, and it's the part that keeps the report from ending bleak, so I want it on the record with the same rigor. `[beat]` New things grew.

Hardcore — one life, delete on death — began as a player-made ruleset enforced by nothing but honor and an addon, a community inventing mortality inside the museum, and it produced the most alive the game has felt in twenty years: strangers cheering level-ups in trade chat, funerals with attendance, fear restored to a world that had been solved to the decimal. (Regular viewers of this channel will recognize this exact shape from an OSRS video about a buried death system. The pattern crosses games. The pattern might be the most important thing this channel has found.) Fresh servers keep launching, and every launch, thousands of adults show up to stand in a starting-zone queue that is somehow both a funeral and a birthday. The old dirt still grows things. `[slow]` What it grows is new.

So the revised conclusion, and notice how much stranger it is than "you can't go home": the place survived. The place is *fine*. It can be resettled, re-cultured, made alive again — by new cultures. What can't be done, by anyone, at any budget, with any fidelity of restoration, is *reoccupancy*: being the person who lived there the first time.

---

**REVISED CONCLUSION. The instrument.**

[VISUAL: card: "CONCLUSION." The pen hesitates.]

Here's what I actually think Classic was, and the report has earned the sentimental sentence it's about to spend. Classic was a mirror with a fixed manufacturing date. The game held perfectly still — the only thing in most players' lives that had held still since 2005 — and so every single difference you felt, logging in, walking into Elwynn with the music swelling and the feeling arriving at only half strength… every missing percentage point of that feeling was a *measurement*. Clean, calibrated, and of you.

Your patience, gone shorter. Your evenings, gone scarcer. Your capacity for aimlessness — the fourteen-year-old's superpower, the thing the boat video in this batch called time held in common — gone entirely, replaced by an adult who checks the clock *inside the game built for wasting time*. The game didn't change and therefore couldn't be the thing that changed. There are very few instruments on Earth that can show a person their own drift with that kind of precision.

`[hold]`

Millions of people logged in, in August 2019, to visit 2005. What they met instead was themselves, at thirty-three, holding the same sword.

And the strangest finding in the whole report — promised at the top, delivered now — is what they did next. `[beat]` They stayed. Enough of them, anyway. Knowing it was half strength. Knowing the fog was gone and the frontier was a spreadsheet. They re-rolled on fresh servers, they died in Hardcore, they stood in the queues again, because it turns out half strength, shoulder to shoulder with strangers in a starting zone at midnight, the distance to 2005 closed to *almost* nothing —

`[slow]` almost is a lot. Almost is more than most things offer.

Home is a time. You can't go back; the experiment is finished on that question, the data's in, n was millions. `[beat]` But you can get close enough to wave. And several million people, given the choice, decided that was worth standing in line for.

[VISUAL: the lab notebook closes; on the back cover, in pen: "EXPERIMENT ONGOING." Cut to the login screen, held, music out]

The experiment is ongoing, by the way. No further funding required. Just a subscription, `[beat]` and everything you remember.
