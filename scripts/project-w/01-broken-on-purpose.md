# Broken on Purpose — Full Script (Project W, #1)

**Thesis:** Every class in vanilla WoW is deliberately incomplete — each one missing something basic that another class has in surplus — and that engineered dependency, the thing modern design pathologizes as friction, is the machine that manufactured the era's community. The patches that made every class self-sufficient were amputations of need, and need was where the people lived.
**Format:** Anatomy lecture. The 40-man raid goes on the dissection table and gets examined as a single organism; the organs are classes.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,050 words)
**Voice dial:** High. Second person throughout, whiplash landings, the batch's warmest close.
**Facts:** No precise numbers appear in the prose. Every mechanic invoked (conjured water, Ritual of Summoning, buff mechanics, warrior sustain) is flagged below and pending `research/wow-facts.md` verification.

## SCRIPT

The most powerful wizard on your server had a second job.

[VISUAL: black; fade in on period footage — a mage in full tier standing by the Ironforge bank, casting the same spell on loop, water stacking up]

You'd see him by the bank every Tuesday around six. Archmage. Personally present at the death of a fire god. Standing in one spot for the better part of half an hour, casting the same spell over and over, filling bag after bag with drinking water — because in a few minutes, forty people are going to work, and somebody has to run the canteen.

No studio would ship that today. A god-slayer moonlighting as a vending machine is a bug report, an obvious quality-of-life fix, a joke. Vanilla World of Warcraft shipped it on purpose and defended it for years.

» Because every class in this game is broken on purpose. Deliberately, surgically incomplete. `[beat]` And the missing pieces are the best design in it.

That's the claim, so you can start arguing now. To defend it I want to do something slightly morbid: put a forty-man raid on the table and open it up. Because from the outside a raid looks like forty separate heroes standing in a volcano. From the inside — from the design's point of view — I'd argue it's one animal. `[slow]` And the classes are its organs.

---

**Start with the warrior, because the warrior is the punchline the whole game is built on.**

The main tank. The class every endgame encounter assumes, the one drawn on the box with the horned helmet. And out in the world, alone, he is the most helpless creature in Azeroth. He has a button for hitting things and no button for having been hit. His health bar goes down and his options are bread. So he fights, and then he sits in the dirt and eats, and fights, and sits, and eats — a leveling warrior is a picnic simulator with rage mechanics. `[beat]` Somewhere right now a druid is levitating past him at double speed, in cat form, unkillable, smug.

[VISUAL: leveling montage — warrior sitting to eat between every pull, timestamp counting up; a druid strolls through frame]

Meanwhile the priest has the opposite disease. She cannot die and she cannot kill. Her damage spell feels like a strongly worded letter. Every fight is a slow, safe, grinding negotiation. Solo, both of these people are having a bad time — in exactly opposite directions.

Put them together and something clicks that neither of them can produce alone. The fights don't stop. The bread stays in the bag. Two half-characters snap together into one whole one, and the seam between them is where the conversation happens — because you talk to the person you're surviving with. You remember who healed you. `[beat]` Hold that thought; I'll collect on it later.

That's the pattern, and once you see it you see it everywhere: every class's power curve has a hole in it shaped exactly like another class. The mage is a glass cannon who makes food for the people who can take a punch. The warlock converts other people's health bars into everyone's convenience. The design keeps doing the same move at every scale, and the move is: no class in vanilla is a whole person.

» The whole person is the raid.

---

**Second organ. The teleport with a minimum occupancy.**

Warlocks can summon a player from anywhere in the world. Enormous, structural power — the kind of spell that in any other game would be a personal convenience button. In vanilla, Ritual of Summoning requires the warlock and two additional party members, all physically present, all clicking the portal together.

Sit with that as a designer for a second. Somewhere in Irvine around 2003, a person wrote a teleportation spell — bending of space, dark magic, the whole business — and then gave it *minimum staffing*. `[beat]` Three consenting adults holding hands around a purple hole in the world so that a fourth doesn't have to take the boat. (There's an entire video in this batch about the boat. The boat deserves it.)

[VISUAL: three players clustered on a summoning portal, the classic green channel beams; frame it like a séance]

It's absurd, and I think it might be the most honest spell in the game — the one teased at the top. Because it prices convenience in the only currency vanilla actually runs on, which is other people's time. Fast travel exists. It costs three humans. Every summon is a tiny negotiation, a favor called in, a "get here and we'll pull you through" — the spell physically cannot be cast by a person who has no one. The game's fastest way to move is also its bluntest test of whether anybody likes you.

And the water is the same design wearing an apron. Conjured food and drink vanish when the mage logs off — you can't stockpile it, can't buy it on the auction house, can't route around the person. If forty people want to drink on Tuesday, a mage has to *be there*, on Tuesday, making it. The chore is the tether.

---

**Third organ, and the one I want to slow down for. The buff round.**

[VISUAL: raid assembling at the Molten Core stone; buff icons blooming across forty unit frames, one by one]

Before every raid, this liturgy. Fortitude, one soul at a time. Intellect for the casters. Mark of the Wild working down the list. Minutes of humans casting small kindnesses on each other while somebody argues about the pull order. Mathematically it's nothing worth filming — some stamina here, a few points of spirit there. Socially it's the whole scene in miniature: forty people saying, in the game's own grammar, *I am spending my mana on you.*

That's a reading, and I'll label it as one — the tooltip just says it increases Stamina. But notice what the design keeps refusing to do. Group-cast versions of these blessings either don't exist yet or demand a reagent, a little tax paid in candles, of all things, so that even the bulk version costs somebody a shopping trip. The game had every opportunity to automate generosity. It kept declining. Whether that was philosophy or accident is a question for the flags section, and honestly, for the turn — because the strongest argument against everything I've said so far is sitting right there.

---

**Here it is. The players killed this system themselves, and they did it with a smile.**

Every single time this playerbase was offered self-sufficiency, it said yes. Dungeon finder arrived, and got used — relentlessly, universally, while the forums mourned community in one tab and queued in the other. Buffs went raid-wide with a single click and nobody wrote the candle industry a eulogy. Mage water became irrelevant and not one person marched on Irvine. If engineered dependency was the beloved soul of the game, it should have survived contact with an alternative. It survived nothing. Anywhere. Ever. What players call "community" in surveys, they call "friction" at the character screen — and they vote at the character screen.

That's the real argument, and I'm not going to strawman my way around it, because it's mostly correct and it's also, I think, aimed at the wrong unit. `[slow]` Revealed preference measures what an individual wants in the moment. A world is a group over years. Those two things can want opposite futures, and the market only ever hears from the first one.

Each dependency removed made every individual session smoother — genuinely, measurably smoother — and made the shared world slightly less necessary. Repeat per patch, per expansion, per decade. No single removal was a mistake. The sum of them is a game where you can go from login to loot without a single sentence passing between you and another living soul, which is either progress or a diagnosis depending on the day.

» Nobody voted for loneliness. They voted for convenience, a hundred separate times, and loneliness was the invoice.

The vending machine was never the point, is what I'm saying. The point was that the machine had a name, and a guild tag, and could leave. Community was never a feature Blizzard could ship. It was a habitat — and the dependencies were the climate it grew in.

---

**Last thing. The dissection's over; you can have the animal back.**

Twenty years is long enough to forget almost anyone. People forget managers they had for years, roommates, whole relationships gone foggy at the edges. But ask someone about vanilla — ask them who tanked, ask them who healed them through their first dungeon at three in the morning — and the names come back like they were carved. Not the boss names. `[beat]` The people.

I told you I'd collect. You thought of a name just now. `[beat]` That's the design working — two decades after the servers it worked on went dark.

[VISUAL: slow pan across an old guild-roster screenshot, names slightly blurred except one]

The classes were broken so the people would hold. I can't prove the designers meant it — flags below, no dev quote in hand — but I can prove the effect outlived the cause, because it's sitting in your head right now with a name attached.

So: somewhere out there is a mage who spent, cumulatively, days of his one finite life standing by a bank, making water for strangers.

If you're watching this: it counted.

`[slow]` It was stupid, and it fucking counted.

[VISUAL: the Ironforge bank mage from the cold open, held a long moment; cut to black]

## Flags for the user
