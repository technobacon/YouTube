# 92 Is Half of 99 — Full Script (Project V, #1)

**Thesis:** The XP table — one exponential function written in 2001 and never touched since — is the best-designed thing in Old School RuneScape: a doubling curve dressed as a linear staircase, and the costume is what makes a mathematically brutal grind feel fair.
**Format:** Museum object study. The whole essay orbits a single number in a glass case; the case returns at the turn and the close.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,100 words)
**Voice dial:** Medium. Grounded through the math, reaches for the register at the open, the turn, and the close.

## SCRIPT

Every museum keeps one case like this. A single object behind glass, lit badly, with a small card underneath, and the card says something unhelpful. *Bronze. Ceremonial. Origin unknown.* You walk past it on your way to the dinosaurs.

[VISUAL: black screen; a museum vitrine fades in; inside it, small and white: 13,034,431]

This is that case. The card reads: *integer, 2001, still in use.* Thirteen million, thirty-four thousand, four hundred and thirty-one. The experience it takes to raise any skill in Old School RuneScape to level 99. It hasn't changed in twenty-five years — through two games, an engine's worth of reworks, a player revolt, and a resurrection.

» And I think it's the best-designed thing in the game. Better than any boss. Better than any quest. `[beat]` It's a number. Let me earn this.

Start with a fact you can check on the wiki before this sentence ends, one that reads like a typo.

» In Old School RuneScape, ninety-two is half of ninety-nine.

[VISUAL: the experience table, rows 92 and 99 highlighted — 6,517,253 and 13,034,431 side by side]

Level 92 sits at 6,517,253 experience. Half the case number, within a rounding error. Which means a level seven steps from the finish line is the *halfway* point, and the game has never once mentioned it. No tooltip. No milestone message. The interface treats 92 like any other Tuesday. `[beat]` There's a machine behind that sentence. By the end of this video you'll see it running under every skill you've ever trained.

---

**First, the function itself.**

Each level costs more experience than the last — every player knows that much. What's less known is the *rate*: the total cost doubles roughly every seven levels. That's the whole design. One rule.

Run the rule forward and the ladder assembles itself. If 92 is half of 99, then 85 is roughly the quarter mark. 78, an eighth. Keep going down and the numbers get silly — the entire climb from 1 to the mid-70s, the part that takes a new player months, the part with the quests and the milestones and the first boss kills, compresses into a sliver of the true total. Now run it the other way. `[slow]` The last seven levels of a skill contain as much game as the first ninety-two.

[VISUAL: the XP table redrawn as area, not rows — 92→99 shaded as one half, everything else crammed into the other]

Exponential growth. The kind of function humans are famously bad at feeling — compound interest and pandemics keep demonstrating this — and somewhere around 2001, a games company being run off a kitchen table pointed that exact blind spot at your sense of progress. I can't show you a design document from 2001; as far as I can find, none survives in public. What I can show you is what the function *does*. And what it does is precise.

---

**Because here's the thing about the staircase: it lies to you, politely, every single level.**

The interface presents progress as linear. Level 1, level 2, level 3 — identical increments, identical fireworks, a tidy green bar filling toward the next ding. Ninety-nine equal-looking steps. The XP behind those steps is doubling on a metronome, but the *display* never changes shape. Same bar. Same chime.

So consider what your first week actually was. Levels fell on you. You couldn't chop a tree without the fireworks going off. Ten levels before lunch, a new tier of armour by dinner — the game handing out achievement at a rate no other part of your life ever has. That week teaches you a rule at a depth no tutorial could reach: *effort here becomes numbers, quickly.* And then the curve begins, very gently, to stretch the interval. Each ding arrives a little later than the last. Never suddenly — the doubling is spread across seven levels, so no single step ever feels like a wall. The price climbs on a schedule tuned so you never see the bill all at once.

[VISUAL: montage — early-level fireworks stacking absurdly fast, then a slow crossfade to a level-91 XP bar crawling]

Want the honest version of that bill? At level 50 — the visual middle of the staircase, the point where the interface's whole layout implies you've done half the work —

`[beat]`

» you have earned less than one percent of the experience in this case.

[VISUAL: the case again; a progress bar under the number filled to a hair's width]

Less. Than one. Percent. The staircase says halfway; the function says you've just walked in the door. And I want to be precise about the design claim here, because it's the heart of the video: the game never shows you this — and shame can't be the reason, since the full table is public, every value exact, anyone can look. The reason is that the *level* is the unit your brain is invited to think in, and levels are the costume the function wears. You count in dings. The machine counts in doublings. The entire felt experience of "progress" in this game is the gap between those two ways of counting.

---

**Now watch what the curve builds with that gap.**

An endgame, out of nothing. Think about what level 99 physically is: the same trees, the same fish, the same click you performed at level 20. No new mechanic arrives. The content doesn't escalate — the *price* does. The curve alone converts repetition into an endgame, and the skillcape at the top is best understood, I think, as a certificate of something specific: you outlasted the function. Everyone knows what it cost, to the exact XP, because the price is public and identical for every account that has ever existed. That's why the cape carries status when a max-level character in most games carries none. Their summit is content. `[quick]` Yours is arithmetic, and arithmetic doesn't do carries.

And then — this is my favourite part — the table just… ends. At 99 the staircase stops. The counter doesn't. Experience keeps accumulating to a cap of 200 million, a ceiling more than fifteen 99s deep, past all levels, past all fireworks, past any reward whatsoever. And people climb it. Multiple players hold 200 million in *Agility*. `[beat]` The curve trains some people so thoroughly that they keep climbing after the staircase ends, on stairs that are no longer there, toward a number that pays nothing.

[VISUAL: hiscores page, 200,000,000 flat in the XP column; hold in silence a moment]

---

**Which brings us to the uncomfortable section, because you may have noticed what I've been describing.**

An escalating cost schedule. Rewards front-loaded to teach the loop, then stretched on a curve tuned to never quite break faith. Celebration effects. Near-miss milestones. If a mobile game shipped this exact architecture in 2026 with a cash shop bolted to the side, we would call it predatory, and we would be correct. The psychology under the XP table and the psychology under a gacha pity counter are cousins at minimum. I'm not going to pretend otherwise, and the honest version of this video has to sit in that discomfort rather than wave at it.

So why does the same function feel *noble* here? Nostalgia is a real answer and an insufficient one. I'll offer two differences, and I'd genuinely encourage you to test whether they're enough.

One: full disclosure. The table is public. Every price, exact, to the experience point, from level 1 to 99, published and stable. Nothing is hidden behind "chance" or seasonal adjustment. The casino posts every odd on the door, in writing, forever.

Two — and this is the one I keep coming back to — » the curve has never been tuned. Not once, in twenty-five years. Modern engagement curves are living things; they're grown in analytics dashboards, adjusted per cohort, personalised to your churn risk. There are careers built on turning those dials. This function was written before that discipline existed, by people who could not have A/B tested you if they'd wanted to, and then it fossilised. It cannot respond to you. It cannot notice you're about to quit and get generous. It is the same for the streamer, the ironman, the bot, and the returning dad with forty minutes a week.

The machinery is manipulative — fine, granted. But it manipulates with its hands flat on the table, in full view, identically, forever. `[slow]` It turns out that's enough to change the moral character of a function. That's an interpretation, mine; sit with whether you buy it.

---

**Last thing. Come back to the case.**

Consider how strange a *constant* is in this medium. Everything in a live game moves. Drop tables get rebalanced. Bosses get nerfed. Metas rot in a season. The one thing in Old School RuneScape that has never moved — through every poll, every rework, every crisis — is the price of a level. Experience you earned in 2004 is worth precisely what it's worth today, converted at an exchange rate of one to one, honoured across two decades and one entire game death. Your account is a savings vehicle in the only currency this industry has never debased. There's a whole other video on this channel about how the game manages its gold; the deeper vault was under the skills panel the entire time.

[VISUAL: the vitrine one last time; the card beneath the number]

So, the card. *Integer, 2001, still in use.* Museums hedge like that when they don't want to commit. I'd add the line the curator was too shy to write:

» *This is the machine the rest of the building was built around.*

Everything else in here is an exhibit. `[beat]` This one's still running.
