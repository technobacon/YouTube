# The Minigame Graveyard — Full Script (Project V, #2)

**Thesis:** OSRS minigames die on a schedule, and the cause of death is always the same — the game taught its players to price their hour in XP and gold, and fun is a currency the ledger doesn't accept. The "minigames" that survive are the ones rebuilt to pay wages, which means OSRS can build brilliant games inside itself and cannot keep them alive *as games*.
**Format:** Walking-tour documentary. The narrator physically travels between locations; each stop is a section. The camera work is the structure.
**Runtime:** ~15 minutes (~2,200 words)
**Voice dial:** Medium-high. Deadpan tour guide, tender at the close.
**Facts:** Deliberately number-light; the tour leans on what the camera can show (empty lobbies). All named mechanics flagged at bottom for verification — none are ledger-verified yet.

## SCRIPT

There's an island in the south-east corner of the map where the lights are still on. Two ships at anchor. A lobby with team banners, blue and red. Boilers, stills, water pumps, a scoreboard — somebody built all of this, textured it, wrote dialogue for it, tuned it, shipped it.

[VISUAL: slow pan across the Trouble Brewing lobby, completely empty; ambient audio only]

Stand here as long as you like. `[beat]` Nobody is coming.

This is Trouble Brewing, and it is one of several places like this — fully constructed, fully functional, permanently empty. The servers hold dozens of games-inside-the-game, and a good number of them have been ghost towns for over a decade. Not disabled. Not removed. Just… unattended, the way a party is unattended after everyone's gone home and the host hasn't turned off the music.

Today I want to walk you through them. Bring a jacket. The itinerary has three stops: a corpse, a pair of zombies, and — at the end — a room that's *full*, which by the time we get there will be the strangest sight on the tour. Somewhere on the walk, I'm going to try to convince you that all of these places died of the same disease, that the disease is us, and that the game gave it to us on purpose.

[VISUAL: stylised tour map with three pins; title card]

---

**Stop one. The corpse.**

Trouble Brewing deserved better, and I want to spend a minute on what it actually is, because almost nobody alive has played it. Two teams race to brew rum. That sentence undersells it — you're growing hops, hauling water, stoking stills, and, this is the good part, you can *sabotage the other team*. Sneak over, break their pumps, torch their stuff. It's a real-time competitive economy with espionage. On paper it's one of the most ambitious things Jagex ever designed — a tiny RTS wearing a pirate hat.

And it pays out in cosmetics. Naval outfits. Hats. `[beat]` Hold that thought; it's the whole autopsy report, and we'll read it properly at the pattern.

Because here's what matters at this stop: the game itself is *good*. People who force a full lobby — clan events, mostly, once every few years, like relatives visiting a grave — consistently report having a great time. The design didn't fail. Something around the design failed, and whatever it was, it killed the most craft-dense minigame on the island without ever filing a bug report.

[VISUAL: archival/staged footage of an actual full Trouble Brewing round, ideally from a clan event — the contrast with the empty lobby is the point]

---

**Stop two. The undead. This is where it gets darker, because worse than a dead minigame is a minigame that can't die.**

The Mage Training Arena is alive in the way a vending machine is alive. Nobody in that building is playing a game. They are executing a sequence, for a wand, while feeling nothing — ask anyone mid-grind how it's going and you'll get the thousand-yard stare of a person alphabetising a warehouse. The minigame *has* players. It has no *play* in it whatsoever. Every person in the room is there to leave.

And then there's Barbarian Assault, which might be the best cooperative game Jagex ever built. Five players, four roles, real interdependence — your attacker literally cannot see what to attack without the collector calling it out. It's a communication game. It's genuinely, mechanically brilliant, and I'll defend that against anyone.

Here's how it's actually played in 2026: `[quick]` you join a service, you pay a fee or you queue with grinders, you speedrun the rounds with people who've optimised every scroll of dialogue out of the experience, you collect your fighter torso, and you never, ever come back.

`[slow]` People pay gold to *skip the fun part*. The fun is so unwanted there's a market for its removal.

[VISUAL: BA role interface + a "torso service" clan advert, anonymised]

Sit with how strange that is for a second. A well-designed game, inside a game people love, and its player base treats it exclusively as an obstacle between them and an untradeable chest piece. If Trouble Brewing is a corpse, this is something out of a folklore warning: still moving, hollowed out, animated entirely by the reward at the end.

---

**Which brings us to the disease.**

Every piece of content in this game, from the day it ships, faces the same interview question. One question, asked by a hundred thousand players simultaneously, with wiki pages and spreadsheets as the interview panel:

» *What do you pay per hour?*

XP is an answer. Gold is an answer. An untradeable upgrade is an answer, once. "You'll laugh with four strangers" has never been an accepted answer, anywhere on the ledger, and content that offers it starves — at any level of quality. That's the pattern, and once you have it, the whole graveyard reads as one epitaph repeated. Trouble Brewing pays in hats: dead on arrival. Barbarian Assault pays once, in torso: undead, farmed, discarded. The disease has a mechanism and everything.

And I want to be careful about where the blame lands, because the obvious take — "players ruined it, efficiency culture ruined it, you optimised the fun out of your own game, look what you did" — is a cop-out. Players didn't invent the hourly wage. The game did. Every skill is a rate. Every drop is a rate. There's a video on this channel about how every reward in OSRS is a posted price for attention, and prices are a *lesson*, repeated ten thousand times, from level 1: your hour has an exchange rate, know it, respect it. `[beat]` You cannot run a twenty-five-year seminar on wage-thinking and then act surprised that the students price things.

[VISUAL: wiki "money making guide" and XP-rate tables scrolling — the syllabus]

---

**Stop three. And now, the strangest sight on the tour: a crowd.**

[VISUAL: hard cut — a packed Wintertodt boss room, chat scrolling, dozens of players]

This is the Wintertodt. Cooperative content. Team-based, communal, dozens of strangers fighting a blizzard together. It has been reliably full — around the clock, on multiple worlds — since the day it launched. By the logic of the graveyard, this should be impossible.

Look closer at what it's paying. Firemaking experience, at excellent rates. Loot crates that scale with your other skills. Same story at Tempoross, its seafaring cousin. Same story at Guardians of the Rift, the runecrafting one — which, notably, the community basically begged Jagex to build after the first two worked. Three cooperative minigames, three living rooms full of players, and all three pass the interview. They pay wages. The play is real, the crowds are real, and the crowds are *purchased*.

There's a name players use for these, and it does a lot of quiet work: skilling *bosses*. The word "minigame" barely gets used for them. As if the community sensed the pattern before anyone wrote it down — over here, things that pay; over there, in the graveyard, "minigames," a word that now functions as a warning label. The genre didn't get fixed. `[beat]` It got renamed, and the renaming was the fix.

---

**Now, the turn, because there are two serious objections to everything I've said, and one of them has teeth.**

The first is the friction theory, and any long-time player will raise it: minigames don't die of bad wages, they die of *logistics*. Trouble Brewing needs a full lobby on one world at one time — a coordination problem, and coordination problems snowball. The lobby dips below critical mass once, so the next person finds it empty and leaves, so it's emptier. Death spiral, no wages involved. And notice the survivors aren't just well-paid — they're *drop-in*. Wintertodt asks nothing of you socially; walk in mid-round, contribute, leave. No team formation, no waiting room. Maybe the graveyard is full of scheduling failures, and I've been performing an autopsy on the wrong organ.

It's a good theory. Here's why I don't think it's the whole story: friction explains the *speed* of death, and wages predict the *direction*, every time. When a reward matters enough — the torso, the wand — players cheerfully bulldoze through worse friction than Trouble Brewing ever imposed. They form five-man teams with strangers, they schedule, they queue, they pay. Coordination costs get paid whenever the wage covers them. The one thing the player base has never done is pay those costs for fun alone. So yes — the two compound, and drop-in design is real craft. But friction decides how fast the patient declines. The wage decides whether anyone calls the doctor.

The second objection is quieter and I have more sympathy for it: *so what?* A twenty-five-year-old world is supposed to have ruins. Jagex barely ever deletes anything — the graveyard is only visible because this game, uniquely, keeps its failures in the world instead of patching them out of existence. Empty places with history are texture. Every proper old city has streets like Trouble Brewing. Maybe a graveyard is just what honesty looks like at this age, and the real design failure would be bulldozing it for something optimal.

`[slow]` I think that's mostly right, actually. Which is why the tour doesn't end with a demand that Jagex fix anything.

---

**Come back to the docks with me. It's nighttime now, in the way it's always some particular time in a game that never closes.**

[VISUAL: Trouble Brewing lobby again, same pan as the open]

I started the tour here on purpose. This is the purest specimen: maximum craft, zero wage, total death. Nothing about this place failed except its economics. And the reason I keep coming back to stand here is that the empty lobby is a document about *us*, far more than about Jagex. The disease metaphor was always a little dishonest — a disease is something that happens to you. This was learned. One rate table at a time, one wiki page at a time, over years, by me as much as anyone.

So here's the experiment, and it's free. Come here yourself. Stand on these docks, where somebody once scripted two ships' worth of piracy for your entertainment, and time how long it takes before some background process in your skull says *this is a waste, nothing here pays, go do your birdhouse run.*

`[beat]`

» That voice has an XP rate. The game installed it. The graveyard is just the one place quiet enough to hear it.

…Anyway. Your birdhouses are probably ready.

[VISUAL: the player character logs out on the empty dock; the lobby music keeps playing over black]
