## SCRIPT

Falador, east bank, sometime in 2005. A kid — eleven, maybe twelve, playing on the family computer with the monitor that hums — has just been told something wonderful by a stranger in a cape: `[slow]` there's a trick where, if you drop your full mithril set and log out fast, the game duplicates it.

You know how this story ends. Everyone who was there knows how this story ends.

[VISUAL: Timeline A — staged low-res footage, 2005 UI, the items hitting the ground, the logout, the empty tile. Hold on the empty tile.]

Cut to now.

[VISUAL: hard cut, Timeline B — modern client, mid-raid]

A player mid-fight needs to know a defensive stat, a drop rate, and which tile is safe on the next phase. Alt-tab. Four seconds. All three answers, exact, sourced, current as of this morning's patch. Back before the tick ends. Same game — same servers, same 600 milliseconds — and those two players might as well be on different planets.

» The distance between them is the subject of this video. Because somewhere between the furnace and the raid, half of Old School RuneScape moved *outside* the game — onto a website Jagex doesn't own — and I want to argue that this was the most consequential interface change in the game's history, that it never appeared in any patch notes, `[beat]` and that at the critical moment, Jagex did something I can't find another studio of its size doing: it looked at the fan project eating its game, and handed over the keys.

That last part comes later. First, the furnace.

---

**Timeline A. The folklore era.**

Here's what information *was*, before documentation. It was a guy. Everybody had a guy — a cousin, a clanmate, a stranger at the anvils who seemed to know things. Knowledge moved person to person, at the speed of typing, and it mutated as it went, because there was no way to check anything against anything.

Some of the folklore was innocent. Rumours about what a quest unlocked, half-right advice about training, treasure-map logic passed between kids like samizdat. Some of it had teeth. The armour-trimming scam ran for *years* — "I can trim your rune for free, just trade it over" — and it worked, at scale, because the claim was unfalsifiable. Nobody could look up whether trimming was a service. There was nowhere to look. An entire criminal economy operated inside the gap where a reference book should have been.

[VISUAL: Timeline A montage — trade window, "trimming armour 4 free", the trade, the teleport]

And the texture of that era deserves better than flat nostalgia, because two things were true at once. The world felt enormous. Ignorance has no edges — when you don't know what's over the hill, the hill is infinite, and every player carried around a personal, wrong, wonderful map of what the game contained. I genuinely miss that. And also: it was mostly noise. The "mystery" we grieve was, in the median case, a twelve-year-old confidently telling another twelve-year-old something false. The romance and the misinformation were the same substance. `[beat]` Hold both. You'll need both at the turn.

---

**Timeline B begins as an accuracy war.**

Fan sites appeared early — databases, forums, calculators — all competing on one axis: being less wrong than the guy at the furnace. The wiki format won because it metabolises corrections faster than anything else humans have invented, and by the OSRS era the community had built something strange: a reference work more rigorous than most published encyclopedias, about a video game, maintained by volunteers, verified by datamining and ten-thousand-kill sample sizes. Players were reverse-engineering drop tables out of sheer statistical patience. The game had hidden numbers; the community was extracting them one kill at a time, like archaeologists with toothbrushes.

Which set up a genuinely interesting standoff. Every big game has a meta-resource like this, and studios historically respond somewhere on a spectrum from ignoring it to fighting it — obfuscating numbers, patching around datamines, treating external knowledge as a leak in the hull.

Here's the payoff of the loop from the top. `[slow]` In 2018, Jagex went the other way. The community wiki walked out of its old ad-farm host, set up independently, and Jagex *backed it* — officially, publicly, with support, and eventually with a button. Look at your minimap area in the modern client: there is a wiki lookup built into the interface. The developer put a door to the fan site inside the game. And in the years since, Jagex has increasingly handed the wiki the real numbers — actual drop rates, from the actual code, published straight into the reference work — ending decades of the community guessing at odds through brute-force sampling.

[VISUAL: Timeline B — the in-client wiki button pressed, item page opening; a drop-rate line with an official-source citation]

Think about what that button *is*, structurally. It's the studio acknowledging, in shipped UI, that the game cannot be played from inside the game. I mean that as the highest compliment I can pay a design decision — every other studio's pride would have fought that sentence to the death. Jagex printed it on the client.

---

**Because by then it was simply true, and the design had started assuming it.**

This is the part I find most interesting as a design question, and it's the claim I'd most like you to argue with in the comments: modern OSRS content is balanced *for the wiki-open player*. The tile-dance bosses, the puzzle rooms, the gear-check raids with their spreadsheet-shaped preparation — the tuning presumes lookup. It has to. When ninety-something percent of your players consult the reference work (I can't source an exact figure and won't invent one — but you know, and I know), designing for the blind player means designing for almost nobody. The wiki stopped being a companion to the game's difficulty and became a *component* of it. It's in the spec now. An encounter is hard or fair or overtuned relative to a player with the page open.

There's a channel-adjacent example that makes this concrete: quests. The old design assumed you'd be lost — being lost was the content — and the modern player runs Quest Helper or a wiki walkthrough as a matter of course. The design and the documentation have fused. You can define an interface as the layer standing between your intent and the game's response, and by that definition, honestly applied, the wiki is the primary display and the client is where you execute what the other screen decided.

[VISUAL: split-screen — left, the wiki strategy page; right, the fight it describes, actions matching the page beat for beat]

---

**Now the turn, and it deserves the strongest version, because "the wiki ruined the game" is one of the most sincerely held positions in this community.**

You'll find it under every nostalgia thread, and it goes like this: perfect information flattened the world. Nothing is far away when everything is indexed. Nothing is a secret when every secret has a page, and the eleven-year-old's infinite map got replaced by an actual map, which is smaller, because real things are smaller than imagined ones. The people who say this aren't wrong, and I have no interest in pretending they are. Something real died. I stood at the empty tile in 2005; I'm allowed to say it.

But two things, honestly held, back.

First: check the time of death. Personal discovery dies the moment you have one friend who knows more than you, which makes the folklore era a period of *worse documentation* rather than blind exploration. By the time the wiki existed there was little left for it to murder — the mystery died in about 2002, at the speed of the first forums, and what filled the next decade and a half was noise with good lighting: scams, myths, confident nonsense. The wiki flattened the world less than it drained the swamp.

Second — and this is the category I promised you — there's a kind of knowledge the wiki cannot hold, and it happens to be the kind this game runs on. » The wiki can tell you the odds. It cannot experience the variance for you. Your first fire cape is not on the page. Going two thousand kills dry is not on the page — the *rate* is, and the rate is precisely the thing that makes the two thousand yours. Everything the wiki indexes is what's true for everyone. Everything that keeps you logging in is what happened to *you*. Total documentation, and the game's actual centre of gravity never even shifted. `[beat]` Turns out they weren't storing the mystery where we thought.

---

**Last scene. Both timelines at once.**

[VISUAL: split-screen — the 2005 kid at the furnace on the left, the modern player with the wiki open on the right]

The kid at the furnace never got the mithril back. What he got instead, eventually — what we all got — was the strangest monument this community ever built: the missing reference book, the one whose absence the scam lived in, written after the fact, by thousands of volunteers, for free, until it became good enough that the game's own developer conceded the point and built it a door.

Most studios treat the space around their game as either marketing or threat. This one, at least once, treated it as *part of the game* — and got the only MMO whose documentation is loved the way content is loved.

So, the biggest interface update in Old School RuneScape's history. No patch notes, no poll, no release date anyone can name. `[slow]` It shipped to the other monitor. It's open right now, isn't it.

…Go on. You can check what mithril trims for. There's a page.

[VISUAL: Timeline B player alt-tabs one last time; cut to black on the sound of a browser tab opening]
