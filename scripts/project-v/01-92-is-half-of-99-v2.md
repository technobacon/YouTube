# 92 Is Half of 99 — Full Script V2 (Project V, #1)

**Thesis:** The XP curve makes RuneScape's grind feel fair by showing the player one believable level while an exponential mountain grows outside the frame. Its fixed, public price turns repetition into a shared measure of persistence.
**Format:** The progress bar as an unreliable map. The script begins with level 50's apparent midpoint, follows the curve into 99 and 200M, then returns through an abandoned account whose XP has preserved a former self.
**Runtime:** ~14–15 minutes (~2,200 words)
**Voice dial:** Medium-high at the open, the 200M turn, and the close; grounded and mechanics-first through the mathematical body.

## SCRIPT

Pick a skill. Any skill. Put it at level 50.

The number sits almost exactly in the middle of the skill tab's little journey from 1 to 99. Fifty looks respectable. Mature. The sort of number that ought to arrive with a mortgage and an opinion about lawn care.

You have earned 101,333 experience.

Level 99 costs 13,034,431.

Your progress is 0.78%.

Congratulations. You have almost started.

[VISUAL: a level-50 skill beside the XP table; the camera pulls back until its progress becomes a hair-thin line]

Seven levels from the end, at level 92, the game finally allows you to call yourself halfway done.

6,517,253 experience behind you.

6,517,178 remaining.

» Ninety-two is half of ninety-nine.

Every RuneScape player knows the phrase. It gets repeated when somebody celebrates level 92, usually by the kind of friend who sees happiness and reaches for a calculator.

But the phrase deserves more than meme status. It exposes the machine beneath the entire game.

RuneScape draws a staircase with 99 equal-looking steps. Underneath, an exponential curve has been gathering mass the whole time. That mismatch shapes your first hour, your first skillcape, and the account you return to after ten years away.

» The XP curve makes RuneScape's grind feel fair by showing you one believable level while the mountain grows outside the frame.

And somehow, for twenty-five years, it has worked.

## Seven levels at a time

The total experience required for a level roughly doubles every seven levels.

That gives the skill table a strange internal rhythm.

Level 78 is roughly one eighth of 99.

Level 85 is roughly one quarter.

Level 92 is half.

Then 99 arrives at 13,034,431.

[VISUAL: four horizontal areas labelled 78, 85, 92 and 99; each successive area doubles]

Every seven levels, the entire journey behind you becomes the price of the next seven.

Everything from level 1 to level 92 fits inside the final stretch from 92 to 99. All the bronze tools, failed cooking attempts, quest requirements and suspiciously long evenings collapse into one side of the scale. The last seven levels occupy the other.

The numbers are brutal. The interface has excellent manners about it.

You never train thirteen million experience at once. You train the next level.

The XP bar empties, gives you a fresh destination, and politely avoids showing the full mountain unless you go looking for it. At level 91, the game asks for level 92. That request feels possible. Once you reach 92, it presents 93 with the same confidence.

The local goal stays close even while the total cost becomes absurd.

This matters because people are dreadful at feeling exponential growth. We understand steps. We understand one more level. Doubling curves arrive wearing fake moustaches and rob us in broad daylight.

RuneScape built an entire progression system inside that blind spot.

I can't tell you whether the original designers understood every psychological consequence of the function. No public design document explains the intention. The behaviour of the curve is plain enough, though.

It keeps the next reward visible while hiding the true distance to the end.

## The generous years

Think back to the beginning of an account.

For the first few hours, level-up fireworks become background noise. Chop two trees. Fireworks. Burn the logs. More fireworks. Bury somebody's femur behind Lumbridge Castle and apparently you are now a better priest.

The game establishes a relationship between effort and progress almost immediately.

You click.

The number goes up.

You receive a new thing to do.

[VISUAL: early-game footage allowed to run; several rapid level-ups, then a slow dissolve into a high-level XP bar]

Then the intervals begin to stretch.

No individual level introduces a shocking increase. The curve raises the price gradually, and better training methods soften it. Your XP per hour improves as your account grows. You gain faster tools, better routes and twenty years of community knowledge produced by people who regard spreadsheets as a competitive sport.

Those gains chase a price that keeps doubling.

The fireworks arrive every few minutes, then every hour, then after several evenings. By that point, the game has already taught you how to wait for them.

That early generosity is doing serious design work. A new player gets variety and progress while they are still learning the world. A committed player receives longer projects once they have reasons to stay. The same function handles both without asking the game to invent ninety-nine separate tiers of content.

The curve creates an endgame out of repetition.

Woodcutting at level 90 still involves clicking a tree. Fishing remains an argument between a person and some water. The action has barely changed. Its accumulated price gives it weight.

That sounds almost insulting when written down.

It feels completely different when the cape appears.

## What a skillcape actually records

A fire cape records a successful performance. You entered the caves, survived the waves and beat Jad.

A skillcape records persistence with unusual precision.

Every player who reaches 99 crosses the same numerical threshold. Their routes can differ wildly. One player chose efficient methods and watched every tick. Another player chopped redwoods during meetings and developed a professional ability to say "absolutely" while having heard nothing.

Both arrive at 13,034,431.

The number gives the achievement a common language. You may know nothing about the person wearing the cape, yet you understand the scale of what happened. Thousands of actions went into it. The exact actions matter less than the fact that they kept returning.

» A skillcape is a receipt for a promise somebody kept making to themselves.

[VISUAL: a skillcape on an otherwise ordinary account; hold on the examine animation and worn cape]

That receipt stays legible because the threshold stayed fixed.

Bosses get rebalanced. Drop tables move. Methods improve. The XP required for level 99 remains where it was placed in 2001.

The hours behind the number have changed. Modern training methods can make old grinds much faster. Plugins remove friction. Guides solve routes before most of us have found the bank.

So an old 99 and a new 99 can represent very different amounts of time.

The shared threshold still matters. It gives every generation of players the same unit, even as the routes around it evolve. A player from the early game and a player training today can argue about methods while agreeing on the destination.

Thirteen million means thirteen million.

The table preserves the denomination while the economy of time changes around it.

## The staircase ends

At level 99, the staircase stops.

Experience continues.

The counter will accept XP until it reaches 200 million in a skill. That is more than fifteen journeys to 99, stacked on top of each other. There are no further levels waiting up there. No second cape. No meaningful unlock at the summit.

People climb anyway.

[VISUAL: a Hiscores page filled with 200,000,000 XP entries; slow push toward the Agility column]

At 99, the number still points toward a reward.

Past 99, it becomes something stranger. A record. A routine. An answer to a question the game has stopped asking.

Humanity discovered calculus and then used it to optimise rooftop laps until a counter reached 200 million. This is, from several angles, fucking stupid.

It is also revealing.

The curve spends ninety-nine levels teaching you to value progress as a number. Eventually the rewards can disappear and the number survives them. A player at 200 million Agility has long since run out of useful explanations. The account becomes explanation enough.

Maybe they enjoy the routine. Maybe they want a place on the Hiscores. Maybe the repetition gives an anxious evening somewhere harmless to go.

The game keeps counting. That is all it promised.

## The ugly reading

There is a much less flattering interpretation of everything I have described.

The XP table front-loads rewards, stretches the cost and celebrates each completed interval with lights and sound. Public rankings add status. Repetition becomes identity.

That is excellent compulsion technology.

Calling the curve brilliant can turn thousands of consumed hours into a romantic story about discipline. Plenty of players have trained after the enjoyment disappeared. Some have damaged wrists, sleep schedules and relationships while chasing a number designed to recede.

The grind can give structure to a life.

It can also eat one.

[VISUAL: the XP bar continues filling over a dark room; only the monitor changes]

A modern live-service game could take the same curve and attach XP boosts, limited-time multipliers and a shop that sells relief from the problem it created. OSRS has bonds, and bond-funded gold can buy faster supplies for a main account, so claims of moral purity would be ridiculous.

The level thresholds themselves remain fixed.

The permanent game asks every account for 13,034,431. The table is public. The price stays after an update and survives a bad quarter. It cannot examine your play history, predict your departure and offer a personalised discount before you escape.

That permanence changes the bargain without cleansing it.

The curve can still manipulate you. It simply does so with a rule you can inspect before beginning. The mountain has a marked height. Jagex may build faster paths up it, but the summit stays where it was.

A fair price can still be too high.

OSRS leaves you to decide whether the thing at the top deserves that much of your life, which is an alarming amount of responsibility to hand someone wearing a dragon med helm.

## The account you left behind

Imagine logging into an account you abandoned in 2014.

Twelve years have passed. You have lived in different rooms. People you once played with have vanished from your friends list. Somewhere along the way, staying awake until 3 a.m. stopped feeling like freedom and started producing back pain.

The account opens.

Level 73 Woodcutting.

Exactly where you left it.

[VISUAL: an old account logging in at Lumbridge; the skills tab opens and remains on screen]

An XP table is a dreadful historian. The room, the music and the voice chat disappear. What survives is the total those evenings produced.

A number.

Crude, incomplete, strangely durable.

Every skill on an old account becomes a timestamp written in effort. That unfinished level holds a version of you who expected to come back tomorrow.

Sometimes you did.

Sometimes twelve years happened.

The curve gives those hours a fixed conversion rate, then leaves the total alone. Your younger self earned the XP. The game kept the receipt.

## Halfway

The joke behind "92 is half of 99" comes from watching two systems disagree.

The skill tab shows ninety-nine orderly steps.

The XP table shows a mountain doubling every seven.

One gives you milestones small enough to pursue. The other makes the final milestone expensive enough to mean something.

[VISUAL: the level staircase laid over the exponential XP curve; the staircase fades, leaving the curve]

At level 1, the page is empty.

At level 50, you have completed 0.78%.

At level 92, everything you have ever earned in that skill must be earned again.

At level 99, the arithmetic becomes a cape.

» The curve takes a repeated action and waits to see how long you are willing to remain the person doing it.

There may be no healthy answer to that question. There may only be answers you can afford.

Still, level 92 arrives. The fireworks go off. The game looks at everything you have done and says:

Halfway.

The cruelty is obvious.

So is the invitation.

[VISUAL: the level-92 fireworks fade; the XP bar begins moving again]

You click again.
