# A Funeral for Death — Full Script (Project V, #5)

**Thesis:** When OSRS replaced items-on-the-floor with gravestones and Death's Office, it buried its oldest teacher — fear — and almost nobody grieved, because the old death had already been dying for years. The real evidence of what was lost is hardcore ironman mode: the player base paying to have the fear installed back, voluntarily, which is the design lesson — punishment works as a covenant and fails as ambient tax.
**Format:** Eulogy. The video is a funeral service — the life, the decline, the death, the widow's rebuttal, the committal. Organ-adjacent music cues, order-of-service on screen.
**Runtime:** ~14 minutes (~2,050 words)
**Voice dial:** High. This is the format-risk video of the batch; the funeral conceit holds the tonal whiplash (solemn → deadpan → sincere) natively.
**Facts:** Death's Coffer mechanics ledger-verified (`research/osrs-facts.md`). Rework year, items-kept rules, HCIM date, reclaim-fee history all flagged — no fee numbers appear in the prose.

## SCRIPT

We are gathered here today to bury a game mechanic.

[VISUAL: black screen; an order-of-service card fades in — "In Memoriam: DEATH (2001–2020), beloved teacher, feared by all"]

The deceased: death — the real one. Born 2001, in a browser. Passed away around 2020, survived by a gravestone system, a themed reaper with an office, and an itemised fee schedule. The service will include a reading of the life, an account of the long illness, one eulogy from the widow — who has asked to speak, and whose remarks, I'm warning you now, are not kind — and a committal.

`[beat]` Attendance, you'll notice, is sparse. When this mechanic finally died, the community that had lived under it for two decades barely looked up from its bank standing. That absence of grief is the strangest fact in the room, and by the end of the service I'd like you to understand it.

But first, as is proper: the life.

---

**He was born simple.** You died, and you kept three things. `[slow]` Everything else hit the floor.

Not a percentage. Not a durability tax. Your actual items, on the actual ground, on a despawn timer, in a world where other people exist. If the floor was far away — and the floor was always far away — you ran back naked through everything that had just killed you, doing arithmetic about timers the whole way, heart rate fully decoupled from the fact that this is a point-and-click game about a guy.

Everyone of a certain age has their corpse-run story, and every one of those stories has the same shape: the sprint, the bargaining, the corner you rounded to find the tile `[beat]` bare.

[VISUAL: staged period footage — the death, the respawn in Lumbridge, the naked run; shoot it like a war memory]

And here is what the service is actually about, so let me say it while the room is quiet. » Fear was that system's *product* — the thing it manufactured on purpose, shipped daily — and fear, in this game's first two decades, did the work of an entire design department. It priced every trip: bring the good gear and risk it, or bring the safe gear and earn less — a wager, renewed daily, that made loadout decisions *mean* something. It made geography real: danger was measured in distance from a bank, and whole regions had a felt temperature because of it. It taught preparation the way games have forgotten how to teach anything — knowledge arriving through loss, once, permanently. Ask a veteran what an antipoison is *for*, and somewhere behind their answer is a specific swamp and a specific year.

An invisible designer, salaried in adrenaline, on duty every hour of every trip. `[beat]` That's who died. The gravestone update was merely when the paperwork went through.

---

**Because — and this is the part the obituaries got wrong — the update didn't kill him. The long illness did, and the cause is standing in this room.**

Old death was designed for a world where your gear was cheap. Losing a rune set stung for an afternoon; the lesson was worth more than the metal. Then the game got old and the numbers got tall. Gear stopped costing afternoons and started costing months — items worth more than entire accounts, entire *years* — and somewhere along that curve, full-loss death changed species without anyone marking the day. It stopped being a lesson. `[slow]` It became an amputation.

And the game knew. Look at the medical record: untradeable items started getting special rules, because losing a fire cape to a lag spike teaches nothing to anyone. High-value bosses shipped with reclaim systems — die at the snake, pay a fee, collect your things from an NPC like a coat check — years before the general rework. Patch by patch, exception by exception, the old mechanic was put on morphine. By the end he governed almost nothing but horror stories: the disconnect at the wrong tick, the misclick that cost a bank. The floor-loot era's last living function was generating the worst day of some stranger's year.

So the cause of death, for the record: `[beat]` your own bank value. Fear scales with what's at stake, and the stakes outgrew what any teacher could ethically hold. Call it natural causes. He was outlived.

[VISUAL: a graph-free montage instead — gear from 2005 vs. gear from 2020, the visual inflation doing the work a chart would]

---

**The death itself, then, was quiet. Administrative.** Around 2020, after consultation and a beta and the full modern process, the successor arrived: gravestones. Your items wait for you now, held safely, for a fee. Behind it all, an office — an actual office, with Death in it, wearing the robe, behind what is structurally a service counter. There's a coffer where you can pre-pay your own retrieval costs by sacrificing items, which means this game now contains, and I need you to appreciate this sentence fully, `[quick]` funeral insurance.

Consequence still exists, technically. It has a receipt now. Dying at a boss is a parking ticket — annoying, budgeted, forgotten by dinner. The system is fair, humane, sensibly priced, and communicates all the menace of a municipal renewals office. The player base looked at it, nodded, and went back to farming. `[beat]` Sparse attendance, as I said.

[VISUAL: Death's Office, the coffer, the fee interface — shot deadpan, fluorescent, DMV energy]

---

**And now the widow will say a few words.** The widow being — well, you. All of you. Twenty years married to that fear, and entitled to the honest speech, which goes like this:

*Good riddance.*

Because let's tell the truth at the funeral, since apparently that's legal now. Old death was a bad teacher with tenure. His lessons landed hardest on the people with the least — the new player who lost her whole unremarkable bank learned mostly that this game is cruel, while the veteran with real wealth had long ago learned the actual meta: `[slow]` don't bring it. The fear that supposedly made gear choices meaningful mostly made one choice, universally: risk nothing. Whole guides were built on the doctrine of the three-item trip. That famous wager renewed daily had a dominant strategy, and the dominant strategy was cowardice — a design producing the exact opposite of the boldness it took credit for.

And his sentencing guidelines were a scandal. The same maximum penalty for hubris as for a router reboot. No appeal, no intent considered — your ISP hiccups at the wrong second and the mechanic hands down bank-deletion, then goes to lunch. You can't call a system *meaningful* when half its executions are clerical errors. The widow yields the floor, `[beat]` and she isn't crying.

---

**Which should end the service. Verdict read, coffin closed, bad teacher buried, progress wins.**

Except one mourner showed up whom nobody can explain.

[VISUAL: a hardcore ironman helmet icon, placed on the coffin like a wreath]

Four years before the funeral, Jagex shipped hardcore ironman mode: one life. Die once — to a boss, a misclick, a disconnect, anything — and the status is gone forever. It is the old teacher's harshness, uncapped, made *total*, with fewer appeals than the system we just buried. By everything the widow said, it should have found no takers.

It is one of the most beloved ways to play Old School RuneScape. Tens of thousands of players — the count is for the researchers, the direction isn't — have queued up, voluntarily, for a strictly worse deal than the one this funeral celebrates escaping. The most-watched deaths in this game's history are hardcore deaths. Streamers have made careers out of one heart attack per fortnight. `[slow]` The fear survived its own funeral. It moved into better housing, and the tenants pay the rent gladly.

» And that's the finding that reorganises the whole service: this player base will accept consequence at any severity, in any quantity, on a single condition — it has to be chosen. Punishment as ambient tax — falling on newbies and lag spikes, unchosen, unappealable — got buried without tears. Punishment as covenant — walked into with open eyes, its terms famous, its stakes the entire point — is thriving one menu option away. Same fear. Same teacher, even. The only variable that changed is the signature at the bottom of the contract.

---

**The committal, then.**

[VISUAL: the order-of-service card again; beneath the dates, a line being added by hand]

Games are the one place where death was ever negotiable — where a community could spend twenty years arguing terms with mortality and actually win concessions. This game ran that negotiation in public, patch by patch: full loss, then exceptions, then fees, then gravestones, then — the final settlement — an opt-in wing where the old terror lives on for anyone who misses him. It's the strangest labour dispute in the history of the medium, and it ended the only way it could: consequence kept its job and lost its jurisdiction.

Here's the detail I can't put down, though. When a main dies, whatever it costs, nobody says anything. When a hardcore dies — someone's eight-hundred-hour one-life account, gone to a lag spike at a bird — the clan chat stops. And then, one by one, letter by letter, the Fs. `[beat]` It's a joke, obviously. It's mostly a joke. But watch how the pause before the Fs gets longer the more hours the account had, and tell me this community didn't figure out mourning.

Nobody ever typed F for a gravestone fee.

`[hold]`

The difference was the choosing. It was always the choosing.

Service concluded. There's tea and biscuits at the Falador party room, `[beat]` and the deceased requests that you bring your three most valuable items.

[VISUAL: the coffin lowers; the level-up fireworks play over it once, soft and wrong; cut to black]
