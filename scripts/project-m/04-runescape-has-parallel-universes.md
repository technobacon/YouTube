# RuneScape Has Hundreds of Parallel Universes — Full Script (Project M, #4)

**Thesis:** World hopping turned server infrastructure into a game mechanic. A RuneScape account crosses between nominally identical universes to gather resources, find empty bosses, buy local stock, escape crowds, and manipulate scarcity. The system keeps the game playable while weakening the idea that any one world is a place.
**Format:** Interdimensional immigration office. Every hop earns a passport stamp; the officer gradually realizes the traveller is using reality as an inventory tool.
**Runtime:** ~8 minutes (~1,200 spoken words)
**Voice dial:** Deadpan bureaucracy for the mechanics, increasingly strange during the identity section, sincere at the final return to a home world.
**Facts:** Current hop restrictions, world categories, resource/shop behavior, and any cooldown details require verification. No world count appears in narration.

## SCRIPT

Anything to declare?

[VISUAL: RuneScape world-switcher beside a severe passport desk; a player stands holding one iron ore]

Weapons.

Food.

Several thousand coins.

One iron ore extracted from World A.

The immigration officer turns the ore over.

“And where are you going?”

World B.

“Purpose of travel?”

There is another iron rock there.

The officer looks past you toward the portal. Through it lies the same mine. Same walls. Same ladder. Same rock, except this version has not been mined yet.

He stamps the passport.

You step through reality, walk three tiles, and strike the second ore.

The officer opens a new form.

“Length of stay?”

Until the rock disappears.

» RuneScape turned the multiverse into a gathering method.

The remarkable part is how normal this feels.

---

**Local reality.**

An MMO world is sold as one continuous place.

The tree you cut exists beside the road. The monster you kill occupied that cave. The stranger at the bank shares your afternoon.

Underneath the fiction, servers divide the population into separate copies. RuneScape exposes the division and gives the copies numbers.

The account persists.

Your inventory comes with you. Skills, quests, equipment, friends, and the humiliating hairstyle chosen seventeen years ago cross the boundary intact.

Many parts of the environment are local.

Players differ. Resource states differ. Shop activity can differ. A training spot occupied in one world may sit empty in another. The same boss room, market square, or patch of sand can hold a different social weather behind each number.

[VISUAL: identical mining location shown across several worlds; only players and depleted rocks change]

This produces a strange ontology.

The iron ore in your inventory is permanent enough to cross worlds.

The rock it came from belongs to one world and one moment.

The passport officer does not ask which reality forged your sword because the sword has escaped locality. Items become citizens of the account. Places remain citizens of the server.

World hopping is the border between them.

---

**Scarcity becomes a dropdown.**

Suppose every rock is occupied.

In a traditional persistent world, the crowd is the problem. You wait, compete, negotiate, move elsewhere, or develop a lifelong enemy whose username contains several numbers.

RuneScape offers another answer.

Change universe.

[VISUAL: world list opens over a crowded resource spot; each selected world briefly reveals a different population]

The same move applies everywhere.

A shop has been emptied. Hop.

A boss entrance is busy. Hop.

A training tile contains another player performing the ancient territorial ritual of standing there first. Hop.

A dangerous area looks active. Hop.

A clan event needs a quieter copy of the map. Hop.

A market needs a busier one.

The server list becomes an atlas of conditions rather than locations. Low population, high population, activity labels, regions, latency, rulesets. The player reads worlds as weather.

Reality turns into a consumable search result.

This is powerful because it converts waiting into agency. It also converts geography into an interface layer. The mine no longer contains six rocks. It contains six rocks multiplied by every accessible world, separated by loading screens and a short administrative delay.

Scarcity still exists.

It has been distributed across universes.

---

**The home-world problem.**

Players still say “my world.”

That phrase carries more emotion than the system deserves.

A world may be chosen for ping, clan presence, activity, habit, or the faint belief that the people at this Grand Exchange are somehow less deranged than the people at the adjacent Grand Exchange.

You recognize names.

The same skiller occupies the same bank.

The same argument appears to have continued since Tuesday.

A stranger's outfit becomes familiar enough that their absence registers before their username does.

[VISUAL: one bank filmed over several days on the same world; recurring players are marked only after they reappear]

This is how a numbered server becomes a neighborhood.

Then a resource is unavailable and you leave in four seconds.

The contradiction is sharp. Persistence asks you to treat the world as place. Hopping asks you to treat it as service instance. One produces attachment. The other produces efficiency.

A city cannot become home if every inconvenience sends you to an identical city where nobody knows you.

Yet RuneScape players perform both attitudes constantly. We speak about home worlds and hop through twenty strangers' worlds before lunch. We want belonging with a low-friction escape hatch.

That may be the most honest architecture the internet has built.

---

**The case for closing the borders sounds romantic for approximately six minutes.**

One world.

One economy of space.

If the mine is crowded, the crowd matters. If a boss is occupied, another person has altered your plan. Resources regain local scarcity. Reputation strengthens because the same population repeatedly meets. The world develops history rather than parallel versions of Tuesday.

Then the population arrives.

Every useful spot becomes a queue or a fight. Dominant groups control scarce areas. Griefing gains structural power. Launch days turn into performance art about human density. Friends separated by server choice discover that “persistent community” often means filling out a transfer form or starting over.

[VISUAL: the same mine imagined as one global world; players accumulate until the rock is invisible]

World hopping absorbs pressure.

It lets thousands of people share one game without occupying one physical copy. It keeps public spaces public while offering relief from the worst consequences of publicity. It allows the developer to add capacity through servers rather than redesigning every resource around peak population.

Instancing could solve parts of this. Dynamic layers could hide the machinery. Personal resource nodes could remove competition.

Each solution sacrifices something else.

RuneScape's visible worlds are crude, comprehensible, and player-controlled. The exit door sits in the interface. You decide when the crowd becomes intolerable.

The multiverse is inelegant.

It is also why the mine works.

---

**The officer reviews your passport.**

Thirty-eight stamps.

Most visits lasted under a minute.

World 302: purchased one item.

World 365: checked one spawn.

World 420: entered, observed the Grand Exchange, reconsidered several assumptions about civilization, departed.

“Do you belong anywhere?” he asks.

You begin to answer with a world number.

Then you remember what belonging actually contains.

[VISUAL: passport stamps dissolve into recurring names at one familiar bank]

The clan meets there.

The latency is good.

The bank tile is familiar.

A person you have never formally befriended always trains nearby. You know their level changes. They know yours. Neither of you has acknowledged this because some relationships survive through disciplined silence.

The officer turns to the final page.

One stamp remains.

You could use it on the nearest available universe, the emptiest mine, the shortest queue.

Instead, you choose home.

» A world becomes real through everything that would be easier somewhere else.

The portal opens.

Same castle. Same bank. Same argument beside the fountain.

A familiar player is standing on your preferred tile.

You consider hopping.

You sigh and stand beside them.

[VISUAL: the player closes the world switcher and remains in the crowded bank; long hold as ordinary chat continues]

Passport closed.

For now.

**Description:** Old School RuneScape exposes its servers as numbered “worlds” and lets players move between them. That makes infrastructure into gameplay: world hopping changes resource availability, shop activity, crowds, bosses, risk, and social space. This essay asks how a persistent world survives when reality is selectable—and why the crude multiverse may be essential to making the MMO playable.
