# Firemaking Is a Fossil From a Different Game — Full Script (Project M, #6)

**Thesis:** Firemaking survives because long-running MMOs are accumulated history rather than clean products. The skill preserves an earlier RuneScape's assumptions about survival, cooking, darkness, and scarcity. Its awkwardness is evidence of a vanished ecosystem—though historical texture can easily become an excuse for neglect.
**Format:** Natural-history museum. A single campfire is reconstructed behind glass while curators argue over whether the specimen belongs in a living game.
**Runtime:** ~8½ minutes (~1,250 spoken words)
**Voice dial:** Museum-documentary calm, dry comedy around the exhibit, then sincere about preservation and decay.
**Facts:** Firemaking's original design intent, historical uses, current training activities, and dates require primary/wiki verification. The script labels ecosystem claims as interpretation.

## SCRIPT

Please do not tap the glass.

[VISUAL: one ordinary OSRS fire burning in a dark museum display; a plaque reads “FIRE, PLAYER-MADE”]

The specimen is sensitive.

It was produced by placing logs on the ground and applying a tinderbox. The subject received experience. The fire persisted briefly, accepted food for cooking, then disappeared without descendants.

Beside the exhibit is a reconstruction of the creature responsible.

A player walks east across a bank, lighting identical fires in a straight line.

One.

Two.

Three.

The museum guide lowers her voice.

“By level ninety-nine, the animal may have done this tens of thousands of times.”

A child raises a hand.

“Why?”

The guide looks toward the curator.

The curator looks toward the funding application.

» Firemaking is an entire skill built around a world that no longer exists.

The fire remains.

That makes it evidence.

---

**Reconstructing the ecosystem.**

Paleontologists do not begin with a complete animal. They begin with a tooth, a footprint, a suspicious depression in stone.

Game historians often receive a mechanic.

Firemaking suggests an earlier RuneScape where fire mattered as infrastructure.

Food was caught raw.

Cooking ranges were not always nearby.

Travel happened on foot.

Inventory space was severe.

A fire created a temporary kitchen wherever the player could obtain logs.

[VISUAL: early-game route reconstructed as an ecological diagram: tree → logs → fire → raw food → cooked food → continued travel]

That chain has coherence.

Woodcutting gathers fuel. Firemaking converts fuel into a temporary station. Fishing or combat supplies food. Cooking converts the food. The player carries survival forward through the world.

The skill icon belongs to that grammar.

So do lanterns, dark places, quest fires, cremation rituals, minigames, and later activities that found more elaborate things for heat to mean. Exact examples belong in the verification pass. The broad shape is visible without them: the game once imagined fire as a portable answer to distance.

Then distance became easier.

Banks multiplied.

Ranges sat near teleports.

Food arrived through markets, boss drops, shops, noted items, and supply systems designed by people who had observed how rarely adventurers wished to role-play municipal catering.

The ecosystem changed.

The skill remained on the character sheet, staring forward with a small orange flame.

---

**A skill without a job.**

RuneScape skills usually promise growing authority.

Higher Mining reaches stronger ore.

Higher Magic produces stronger spells, movement, combat, and utility.

Higher Firemaking allows the player to burn increasingly prestigious logs with the advanced technique known as using the same tinderbox.

[VISUAL: log tiers arranged like an evolutionary line; the interaction animation remains almost unchanged]

The number rises.

The practical transformation often feels modest.

This creates a mismatch between progression scale and functional scale. Firemaking occupies the same visual territory as combat skills and world-shaping crafts. It asks comparable hours from the player. Its central verb remains lighting a temporary object that most accounts can route around.

Later content has worked to give the skill jobs: dangerous cold, specialized fires, ritual systems, group activities, and rewards attached to controlled burning. Some additions are substantial. They also reveal the problem by behaving like prosthetics around the original bone.

The core action survived.

Designers kept building ecosystems where it could breathe.

That is either devotion or administrative panic.

Possibly both.

---

**Living fossils.**

An old MMO cannot be redesigned from first principles every year.

Its systems are treaties with the past.

Players have levels, capes, records, memories, market positions, and identities attached to mechanics that a new design document might reject before lunch. Remove the mechanic and you remove more than code. Change it too far and the number on the character sheet may continue while the activity beneath it becomes somebody else's skill wearing the original name.

[VISUAL: museum hall of old MMO systems; each exhibit is connected to player records, items, quests, and memories by fine threads]

This is why old games accumulate strange organs.

Skills with narrow purpose.

Currencies left from retired events.

Interfaces that survived because every replacement forgot one behavior veterans use daily.

Quest items whose original context has dissolved but whose bank slot has become legally protected by sentiment.

The mess is informational.

A clean game shows the designer's present intention.

An old game shows negotiations between every intention it has ever had.

Firemaking is unusually legible because the historical layer sits exposed. The player can perform an ancient verb at modern scale and feel the gap between them. Tens of thousands of fires produce a cape whose prestige comes largely from having accepted the gap.

The fossil still moves.

It even has a skill guide.

---

**The museum has received a petition from the Department of Useful Things.**

Their case is strong.

Historical evidence belongs in archives. Players inhabit a live service.

A skill with thin utility wastes reward space, update attention, and new-player trust. It teaches that progression may consist of repeating a weak action until the number becomes respectable. Every attempt to preserve the original shape limits what designers can build in its place.

Calling a mechanic a fossil can flatter neglect.

[VISUAL: museum placard removed; the fire is shown inside the actual game, surrounded by unused space]

Age is evidence. Wisdom requires a separate argument.

Awkwardness can contain depth, negligence, or both.

A system may survive because replacement is politically expensive, technically dangerous, or less urgent than the next marketable update. Nostalgia can become a conservation law protecting every bad decision from examination.

And Firemaking has received new purposes precisely because the original one was insufficient. A successful rework or activity can turn heat into pressure, cooperation, route planning, survival, economy, or ritual. The living skill deserves more than preservation behind glass.

The question is how to add life without falsifying the specimen.

---

**A restoration has two possible failures.**

The first leaves the fossil untouched and calls the dust authenticity.

The second rebuilds it so aggressively that the museum displays a dragon assembled from three bones and an executive's enthusiasm.

[VISUAL: split exhibit — neglected campfire on one side, absurd overengineered fire machine on the other]

A good extension reads the old mechanic closely.

Fire consumes fuel.

Fire creates temporary space.

Fire manages cold, visibility, danger, transformation, gathering, and congregation.

Those properties contain more design potential than faster experience attached to more expensive logs. They can support systems that feel descended from the original verb rather than stapled onto its level requirement.

The past becomes material.

It does not become a veto.

---

**The final tour.**

The museum is closing.

The guide extinguishes most of the lights, leaving Exhibit A visible in the dark.

One small fire.

A temporary kitchen from a world where the nearest range felt farther away.

A skill icon from an era that believed survival needed its own chain of verbs.

A ridiculous method for making a number reach ninety-nine.

All of these readings fit in the flame.

[VISUAL: slow push toward the fire; faint reflections show old and modern RuneScape footage in the glass]

» A live MMO needs fossils because memory requires a body. It also needs evolution because bodies must still work.

Keep Firemaking.

Interrogate it.

Build descendants worthy of the bone.

And preserve enough of the awkward original that a future player can look at the skill list, ask why an entire profession exists for this, and discover that the game once imagined a different world around the answer.

The guide hands the child a tinderbox.

Museum policy immediately regrets this.

One match is struck.

History catches.
