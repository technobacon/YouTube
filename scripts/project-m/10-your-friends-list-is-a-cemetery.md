# Your Friends List Is a Cemetery — Full Script (Project M, #10)

**Thesis:** Persistent online worlds preserve relationships after the people disappear. Old usernames remain in the interface without an ending, explanation, or final conversation. An MMO friends list becomes an accidental memorial: evidence that someone once shared this world with you, and that most departures happen too gradually to receive a goodbye.
**Format:** Memorial roll call. Each grey name opens a different form of disappearance, while the narrator resists inventing endings the interface cannot know.
**Runtime:** ~9 minutes (~1,300 spoken words)
**Voice dial:** High and intimate. Humor appears early and sparingly; the middle remains disciplined, and the close offers no false consolation.
**Facts:** No real player's story should be used without consent. Any claims about account inactivity, name changes, privacy indicators, or list behavior require current verification.

## SCRIPT

Roll call.

[VISUAL: an old RuneScape friends list opened on a quiet account; usernames are obscured and recreated]

AbyssalToast.

Offline.

MithrilDad.

Offline.

xXDeathElfXx, whose name was selected during a period when subtlety had not yet reached the internet.

Offline.

The cursor moves down.

Some names produce a face immediately.

Some produce only a place.

A fishing dock.

A clan hall.

The upstairs room of a bank where you both stood because the main floor was crowded and, for reasons lost to history, this felt private.

One name produces nothing.

You know it mattered because you added it.

You cannot remember why.

The list continues.

Nobody answers.

» An MMO friends list records the beginning of a relationship and almost never records the end.

The end usually happened on an ordinary night.

Neither person knew to stay for it.

---

**How people disappear.**

Very few players announce the final login.

They announce breaks.

Exams.

A new job.

A different game everyone is trying.

A broken computer.

A membership lapse.

A promise to return after the next update.

Then absence develops slowly enough to avoid becoming an event.

[VISUAL: calendar pages turn beside a username whose last visible appearance is never marked]

At first you notice.

A week later, you assume timing.

A month later, the name remains on the list because removal would feel premature.

A year later, deleting it feels rude in a way that makes no practical sense. The slot is not urgently needed. The person may return. The account may still exist. The relationship has entered administrative limbo.

So the name stays.

The friends list becomes a room where nobody has been formally declared gone.

This is different from losing contact in physical life. Addresses change. Workplaces disappear. Phone numbers become other people's numbers. Evidence erodes.

The MMO preserves a stable label.

The person leaves.

The username remains exactly where you placed it.

---

**A username is a very small container.**

It may hold hundreds of hours.

The first boss attempted together.

The item loaned before lending systems, trust interfaces, or adult judgment.

The night one person stayed online because the other had nowhere else to speak.

The argument whose subject is forgotten while the embarrassment remains in excellent condition.

[VISUAL: each recreated username reflected faintly over one location or object; no faces invented]

Outside the game, memory attaches to photographs and messages.

Inside an MMO, memory attaches to geography and equipment.

That cape was earned during the summer they were online every day.

That town was where the clan met.

That useless object remains in the bank because somebody gave it to you when both accounts were poor enough for the gift to matter.

The friends list compresses all of this into colored text.

Online.

Offline.

The interface has no category for “was once part of my ordinary life.”

So it uses grey.

---

**Persistent worlds create an unusual asymmetry.**

The place continues.

The road remains.

The bank booth receives new players.

The boss respawns with admirable emotional independence.

You return older to a world that has preserved enough geometry for memory to locate itself.

[VISUAL: present-day account revisits old meeting locations; old silhouettes appear only as empty standing positions]

This is one reason old MMOs become powerful memory machines.

They outlive phases of the people who used them.

A school friendship can vanish while the fishing spot survives. A family computer can be replaced while the login screen keeps the same music. Entire versions of yourself disappear—student, night-shift worker, new parent, lonely child—and the account carries objects gathered by each of them.

The world becomes a shared external memory.

Then the sharing ends, and the memory remains attached to one person.

You stand at the old meeting place alone, holding both halves of a history because the server preserved the coordinates and forgot the attendance.

That is a special kind of loneliness.

It requires excellent uptime.

---

**The memorial interpretation is dangerous.**

A grey name does not imply tragedy.

The person may be healthy, busy, happy, and completely uninterested in returning to a game where progress involved clicking a tree until the tree negotiated surrender.

They may have changed names.

Changed privacy settings.

Changed accounts.

Changed friends.

You may be the person they were relieved to stop hearing from.

[VISUAL: imagined stories about one offline name appear, then are crossed out before becoming specific]

The interface does not grant permission to compose a death around an absence.

Nostalgia is greedy. It converts missing information into emotional certainty because certainty makes the memory easier to hold. “We drifted apart” feels less dramatic than “the old world died,” though drift is what most human relationships do.

The game also benefits from this sentiment. Persistent accounts, anniversary events, old music, and returning-player campaigns can turn memory into retention. A company may preserve your past because preserved pasts are commercially excellent at renewing subscriptions.

None of that makes the feeling false.

It makes interpretation a responsibility.

The list contains evidence of contact.

Everything beyond that belongs to humility.

---

**There is another possibility the memorial metaphor hides.**

To somebody else's friends list, you are the grey name.

[VISUAL: camera pulls back from the viewer's list to several other obscured lists containing the viewer's old account name]

Somebody may remember you through a badly chosen username.

They may still own the item you gave them.

They may wonder why you stopped logging in after saying “tomorrow.”

Your departure may have been completely ordinary. Life changed shape. The game lost priority. No final scene announced itself.

From the other side, ordinary absence becomes mystery.

This symmetry matters because it removes the comfort of imagining yourself as the sole keeper of vanished people. You have vanished too. Repeatedly. From clans, servers, message histories, routines, and versions of the world whose inhabitants continued without you.

Persistent worlds contain overlapping cemeteries where many of the dead are alive elsewhere.

That sentence is melodramatic.

It is also mechanically accurate enough to hurt.

---

**One name turns green.**

[VISUAL: after a long static friends-list shot, one recreated username changes from offline to online]

No music yet.

No miracle framing.

The person logs in.

Perhaps after ten years.

Perhaps after changing a password and discovering the account still owns thirty-seven burnt lobsters for reasons neither of you can reconstruct.

You type hello.

The cursor blinks.

The old friendship does not reassemble automatically. Both people have acquired years, obligations, new language, and the mild terror of speaking to somebody who remembers a version of you unavailable for comment.

They answer.

“Hey.”

That is all.

[VISUAL: the two avatars meet at an old location; keep physical distance ordinary, no cinematic embrace]

For several minutes, you compare accounts.

Then lives.

Then the exact point at which each stopped playing, and neither date matches the other's memory.

The conversation is good.

It is awkward.

It ends because one person has to put a child to bed.

The name turns grey again.

This time the absence has a shape.

---

**Final roll call.**

The list remains.

Some names will never turn green.

Some should probably have been removed years ago.

Some belong to people you would no longer recognize.

Keeping them cannot restore anything. Deleting them cannot erase what occurred.

[VISUAL: friends list beside old map locations, both held still]

» The cemetery is the version of life that placed those names there. That version has ended.

An MMO preserves artifacts from that life with absurd fidelity.

Numbers.

Items.

Coordinates.

Names.

It cannot preserve the ordinary availability of another person.

Nothing can.

You close the interface.

The world continues around the empty panel.

A stranger walks past wearing an outfit you recognize from twenty years ago. For a second, the silhouette resembles somebody.

It is not them.

You let the stranger pass.

Roll call complete.

Nobody marked absent.

## FACT FLAGS / SOURCE NOTES

- Use invented or fully anonymized usernames. Never infer death, illness, or personal circumstances from inactivity.
- Verify current friends-list visibility, privacy behavior, name-change handling, and offline display before showing interface claims.
- Obtain consent before using any real friendship story, screenshot, chat log, or gifted item with identifiable context.
- Claims about returning-player marketing should be documented with specific campaigns if retained in the final cut.
- The essay's emotional thesis is interpretation; keep the narration explicit about uncertainty.
