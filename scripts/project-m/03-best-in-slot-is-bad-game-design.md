# Best-in-Slot Is Bad Game Design — Full Script (Project M, #3)

**Thesis:** A single universal best-in-slot ladder turns an MMO's past into demolition waste. Old School RuneScape survives through conditional superiority: the best weapon depends on the target, defence profile, attack style, inventory, risk, and encounter. Sidegrades keep old items and old places economically alive, though the price is opacity, bank clutter, and a game increasingly played through calculators.
**Format:** Tool workshop. Famous weapons arrive expecting coronation and are assigned jobs instead.
**Runtime:** ~10 minutes (~1,500 spoken words)
**Voice dial:** Analytical and sharp, with workshop comedy throughout. The close opens onto mortality and usefulness without drifting away from the mechanics.
**Facts:** Every named weapon interaction, stat comparison, and special-effect description requires a current wiki/in-game verification pass. The script deliberately avoids numerical DPS claims.

## SCRIPT

The sword arrives in a velvet case.

[VISUAL: an ornate fantasy weapon laid on a workshop bench under a single inspection lamp]

Its name is written in gold.

Its acquisition involved a raid, a prophecy, six friends with compatible calendars, and a drop rate nobody discusses around polite company. It has killed a god. The owner has composed several forum posts about deserving it.

The sword expects a coronation.

At the workshop desk, an old woman puts on her glasses.

“What do you do?”

The sword glows.

“Yes,” she says. “But what are you *for*?”

Silence.

It has the largest number.

The woman checks the work orders.

Dragons need piercing. Armoured creatures need accuracy. Something enormous has three body tiles and poor personal boundaries. A demon requires a particular material, a particular blessing, or the kind of paperwork usually reserved for customs enforcement.

The sword continues glowing, now with less confidence.

The woman attaches a label to its case.

**GENERAL USE.**

Then she reaches for an ugly old spear.

» In a healthy MMO, the best weapon should lose arguments.

---

**The ladder.**

Most equipment progression is architecture drawn vertically.

A sword has ten power. The next sword has twelve. The expansion sword has twenty-seven and emits weather. Once the player acquires it, the ten-power sword becomes a memory, a cosmetic, or vendor trash with an unusually detailed origin story.

The logic is clean.

Harder content gives stronger gear. Stronger gear permits harder content. The player climbs. Numbers certify movement.

The ladder also has a wrecking ball attached.

Every new rung places distance between the player and the old world. Earlier dungeons lose their rewards. Earlier crafting materials lose demand. Earlier bosses become tourism. An entire continent may remain physically present while its economic purpose collapses.

[VISUAL: a tall gear ladder rising through successive MMO expansions; lower dungeons fade as each new rung appears]

Developers can revive the ruins with scaling, cosmetics, achievements, or seasonal resets. Each solution admits the underlying problem: universal superiority makes replacement inevitable.

A single answer to “what is best?” produces a single destination.

Once everyone arrives, most of the game lies behind them.

---

**The workshop in Gielinor operates differently.**

Old School RuneScape certainly has upgrades. Some items are outrageously strong, expensive, rare, and capable of making an older weapon look like cutlery.

Then the target changes.

The workshop wall contains tools for categories of problem.

A weapon that punishes dragons.

A weapon whose value rises against magically powerful targets.

A blade that loves large bodies.

An ancient implement with suspicious enthusiasm for insects.

Fast weapons for low-defence enemies. Accurate weapons for hard shells. Crush for one skeleton, slash for another, stab for the thing whose anatomy suggests somebody at Jagex has unresolved feelings about puncture wounds.

[VISUAL: workshop pegboard; each weapon is hung beneath a simple job label rather than a power ranking]

The equipment screen calls these attack styles and bonuses. The lived effect is a web of conditional authority.

One item is superior here.

Another wins there.

A third is best only if the player can afford its ammunition, charges, upkeep, inventory cost, risk, or the concentration required to switch into it without eating the next attack.

“Best” becomes a sentence with a dependent clause.

That clause is where much of the game survives.

---

**Consider what a sidegrade preserves.**

A new boss drops a weapon with a narrow talent. The weapon gains value wherever that talent matters. Earlier bosses may become more attractive because the tool changes their speed or accessibility. An older weapon retains demand because its own talent still owns another corner of the map. Crafting inputs, consumables, and supporting gear remain attached to both.

The update adds a route through the economy rather than paving over the previous one.

[VISUAL: one new weapon placed at the centre of a map; lines spread toward several old bosses, materials, and supporting items]

This is why an old RuneScape bank can resemble a museum whose exhibits keep receiving work calls.

The dusty helmet from a quest.

The shield tied to one damage type.

The boots that lose every fashion contest and win one encounter.

The amulet kept because a particular enemy category made the jeweller briefly omniscient.

These objects remain mechanically legible years after their release. Their age does not automatically condemn them. A tool waits for the job that remembers its shape.

That preservation reaches beyond gear.

If an old boss supplies a still-useful component, the boss keeps economic gravity. If an old quest grants access to the tool, the quest remains part of progression. If the tool requires an old resource, somebody still gathers it. Conditional equipment distributes relevance backward through the world.

» Sidegrades let an MMO update without declaring its own history unemployed.

---

**This is also why RuneScape players talk about gear like accountants trapped in an armoury.**

The workshop has too many tools.

A newcomer asks which melee weapon to buy and receives a legal deposition.

What are you fighting?

What is its defence?

Which style is weak?

Do you own the supporting necklace?

Are you using prayer?

How many inventory spaces can the switch occupy?

Are you learning, farming, speedrunning, risking the item, or merely hoping to avoid public embarrassment?

The answer ends with a spreadsheet link.

[VISUAL: simple weapon question written on a chalkboard; qualifying clauses accumulate around it until the board is full]

Conditional superiority creates meaningful choice only when the conditions are readable.

Often they are buried in stat pages, wiki tables, hidden target attributes, special-effect wording, and community testing. The game client may show two weapons whose real comparison requires outside software and several assumptions about attack cycles.

The system also feeds switchscape: carrying a wardrobe into battle and changing clothes between attacks because each second contains a different mathematical job. What began as equipment diversity can turn the player into a panicked stagehand, dressing for scenes nobody else can see.

Banks fill.

Prices become difficult to understand.

A valuable drop may feel disappointing because its niche is narrow, while a broad generalist compresses the value of half the tool wall.

The clean ladder solves these problems. It tells the player where progress points. It reduces regret. It allows designers to balance encounters around a narrower expectation. A sword with twenty-seven power may be boring, but nobody needs a doctoral supervisor to equip it.

That clarity deserves respect.

---

**The harder question is where the line belongs.**

Every item cannot remain relevant forever. A world where nothing replaces anything becomes sediment: twenty years of special cases, each demanding memory, testing, interface space, and balance attention.

Power growth provides excitement.

Universal tools provide relief.

Sometimes the legendary raid sword should walk into the workshop and be obviously excellent. A reward can carry breadth as part of its prestige.

The danger appears when breadth becomes sovereignty.

When one item dominates every target, acquisition, and budget where it can physically be equipped, the workshop closes. The pegboard becomes decoration. Content variety survives on the map while equipment variety dies in the bank.

[VISUAL: the workshop wall gradually empties until one glowing weapon remains; the room becomes brighter and less interesting]

OSRS has crossed that line at times. The community knows the shape: one weapon spreads through encounter after encounter, prices organize around it, and balance changes arrive carrying the unpleasant task of making alternatives exist again.

Sidegrades carry their own failures.

Dominance carries an ecological cost.

---

**The tool wall.**

The old woman finishes the inspection.

The legendary sword receives several excellent assignments. It will be used often. It may be the finest general tool in the building.

The workshop has no throne.

[VISUAL: the sword joins the pegboard among cheap, strange and ancient weapons; each has a worn label]

On the wall, value is relational.

The spear needs the dragon.

The crush weapon needs the shell.

The enormous blade needs the enormous body.

Remove the conditions and each becomes an object with a number. Preserve them and the weapon participates in a world: creatures, materials, quests, prices, tactics, and the specific memory of why the player bought it.

Human beings understand this outside games. A chef owns more than one knife. A mechanic does not melt the wrench when a better hammer arrives. Expertise often looks like a room full of objects that appear redundant until the exact problem enters.

MMOs prefer crowns because crowns photograph well.

Tools age better.

» The strongest equipment system asks which item is best, then forces you to finish the sentence.

The workshop closes for the evening.

A new weapon waits outside in a velvet case, shining with the absolute confidence of a thing that has not met its first resistant enemy.

The old woman turns the sign to OPEN.

There is always another job.
