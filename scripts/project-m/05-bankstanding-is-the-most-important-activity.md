# Bankstanding Is the Most Important Activity in RuneScape — Full Script (Project M, #5)

**Thesis:** Bankstanding looks like the absence of play, yet shared idleness creates the weak social ties that make an MMO feel inhabited: familiar names, overheard arguments, fashionscape, advice, scams, jokes, and spontaneous friendship. A multiplayer world needs reasons for people to be unproductive together.
**Format:** Urban-planning hearing. A commission evaluates whether to remove an apparently useless public square from the city.
**Runtime:** ~8½ minutes (~1,250 spoken words)
**Voice dial:** Civic-documentary deadpan with observational comedy; the final section turns warm without idealizing the crowd.
**Facts:** Any use of “third place” scholarship, historical bank layouts, or player-population claims requires verification. The essay makes no numerical social-science claim.

## SCRIPT

The planning commission has received a complaint.

[VISUAL: Grand Exchange photographed like a municipal plaza; a red boundary outlines the central standing area]

At the centre of the city is a large paved square.

It contains no boss.

No resource node.

No quest objective of lasting importance.

Citizens arrive, stand still, open a storage interface, close it, rotate slightly, and continue standing still.

Some have remained for several hours.

The square occupies valuable land. It produces no experience. Its benches cannot be sat on in any meaningful mechanical sense. Several residents are dressed as vegetables.

The proposal is simple:

Remove the square.

Move banking, trading, grouping, and travel into efficient menus. Give every citizen private access from wherever they happen to be. Replace the plaza with content.

A man wearing a party hat approaches the microphone.

“Before you do,” he says, “where will everyone go?”

» Bankstanding is what happens when an MMO leaves people together after the useful part is over.

That may be the useful part.

---

**The commission requests evidence of activity.**

At 8:03, a player asks which weapon to buy.

Three people answer. Two answers conflict. The fourth person recommends an item that does not exist.

At 8:11, somebody completes a level and receives several “gz” messages from strangers who did not witness the work and will never investigate whether congratulations were warranted.

At 8:26, an argument begins about a balance update.

At 8:42, the argument has expanded to taxation, adulthood, and the moral character of people who use a particular prayer book.

At 9:05, a player changes outfit six times while pretending the crowd is not the audience.

[VISUAL: one uninterrupted hour at a busy bank or GE, accelerated only between notable interactions; captions mark small social events]

No single event justifies the square.

Together they form weather.

The plaza allows speech to occur without an appointment. You need no party, clan, queue, or shared objective. You arrive for a functional reason—to access the bank, trade, prepare, teleport—and remain because another person is saying something incorrect within typing distance.

This is low-grade social life.

MMOs are built from more of it than their raid trailers admit.

---

**A friendship is too large a unit for understanding a world.**

Most people in a city are not friends.

They are the barista who recognizes the order, the neighbor whose dog has an unnecessarily formal name, the person seen on the same train, the cashier who notices when you disappear for a month.

These relationships are thin.

They still matter.

A place feels inhabited when other lives become predictable at the edge of yours.

RuneScape produces this through repetition. The same bank. The same world. The same time after work. A player with a memorable outfit occupies the eastern booth. A skiller's level rises across weeks. A merchant continues advertising a price nobody accepts, displaying the admirable persistence of a lighthouse with a gambling problem.

[VISUAL: recurring players at one bank across several days; no names highlighted until the second appearance]

You may never add them.

Recognition arrives first.

Then absence.

The square teaches you that the population continues while you are away. It offers evidence of lives adjacent to the account: people preparing for different bosses, celebrating different milestones, returning from different disasters.

A friends list can tell you who you know.

A public square tells you there are people you do not.

That distinction is the difference between a multiplayer game and a populated world.

---

**Efficiency removes benches.**

Modern online games have become extraordinarily good at connecting people to activities.

Click a queue.

Choose a role.

Teleport into the dungeon.

Complete the objective.

Receive rewards.

Return to the private interface where the next activity waits.

This removes travel, assembly, shouting, waiting, and the many opportunities for a stranger to waste your evening.

It also removes the evening in which the stranger becomes familiar.

[VISUAL: a sequence of clean matchmaking menus; between each, the avatar never appears in a shared public space]

Designers often try to rebuild community through explicit systems: guild finders, recommendation panels, social quests, commendations, scheduled events. These can help. They approach social life as an outcome to be delivered.

A plaza works indirectly.

It creates collision.

People arrive for separate reasons and become visible to one another. The space tolerates waiting, performance, commerce, confusion, and conversation that serves no progression system. Its social value comes from leaving interaction partially undesigned.

The bank is especially effective because it sits between activities. Players return carrying the residue of wherever they have been. Gear, loot, questions, excitement, frustration. The square becomes a customs hall for private adventures.

Every trip ends there.

Another may begin after somebody asks what happened.

---

**The commission now hears from opponents of nostalgia.**

Most bankstanders are not participating in a civic miracle.

They are AFK.

The client is behind another window. The visible body is a coat left on a chair while the person cooks dinner, answers email, or watches somebody else play a more demanding game.

Crowds also contain spam, manipulation, harassment, begging, gambling history, scams, and people training conversational models using material that would get the models expelled from a respectable laboratory.

[VISUAL: crowded GE audio falls away; many avatars are marked as inactive while spam fills the chatbox]

A busy square can feel lonely.

Proximity does not create kindness. Repetition can normalize hostility as easily as familiarity. The loudest residents may dominate public chat until sensible people retreat into clan channels or silence.

And forced idleness is a terrible social policy. Long travel, slow banking, or artificial queues do not become good design because two prisoners eventually exchange recipes.

The commission should reject the lazy version of the argument: inconvenience creates community.

Often inconvenience creates logout.

The actual design requirement is narrower.

People need optional reasons to share space, plus enough unclaimed time for the space to become more than a loading screen.

---

**The square remains under review.**

A city planner asks whether its social function could be measured.

How many friendships began here?

How many questions received useful answers?

How many players logged in partly because familiar strangers made the world feel continuous?

The room has no reliable number.

That is inconvenient for a profession that likes traffic models.

[VISUAL: planning diagram covered in measurable flows; one blank area labelled “RECOGNITION”]

Social infrastructure often looks inefficient because its product cannot be assigned cleanly to the moment of use.

A bench produces sitting.

A park produces routes nobody needed.

A pub produces beverages and, occasionally, a person who will help move a sofa three years later.

The plaza produces possibility at low concentration.

Most visits yield nothing.

That is why the encounters that do occur feel unpurchased.

---

**The vote.**

The commission may improve the square.

Mute tools.

Better grouping.

Cleaner trade interfaces.

Spaces for performance, recruiting, teaching, or simply watching the world pass without blocking the bank chest.

It should resist perfect efficiency.

» An MMO becomes social through the minutes nobody successfully optimized.

The party-hat man leaves the microphone.

He returns to the plaza, stands near the central counter, and says nothing for a while.

A new player arrives wearing mismatched armour.

They open the bank.

They close it.

They hesitate.

“Does anyone know what I should do next?”

[VISUAL: the question appears in public chat; hold long enough for several ordinary players to turn toward the speaker]

One person answers.

Another disagrees.

A third begins explaining something unrelated.

The city resumes.

Proposal denied.
