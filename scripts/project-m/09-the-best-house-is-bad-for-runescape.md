# The Best House in RuneScape Is Bad for RuneScape — Full Script (Project M, #9)

**Thesis:** The player-owned house is a utility masterpiece and a public-world extraction machine. By concentrating restoration, travel, storage, worship, spellbook services, and training inside private instances, the perfect house removes reasons to rely on towns and other people. Construction succeeds as personal progression while exposing a central MMO tension: the better the home becomes, the less the world is needed.
**Format:** Estate-agent tour. Every room is magnificent; the street outside grows quieter after each door opens.
**Runtime:** ~8 minutes (~1,200 spoken words)
**Voice dial:** Luxurious property-show comedy through the tour, colder during the public-world section, warm at the hosting turn.
**Facts:** Every named house feature, requirement, interaction, and hosting behavior requires current verification. No construction cost or level appears in narration.

## SCRIPT

Welcome home.

[VISUAL: estate agent opens the portal to an immaculate high-level player-owned house; camera enters in one slow movement]

Please remove your boots.

The floor cost more than several kingdoms.

To the left, a room restores your body, your prayer, your run energy, your dignity, and certain other conditions the brochure describes as “subject to current game mechanics.”

Ahead, transportation.

Jewellery destinations.

Portal destinations.

A tree indoors.

A fairy ring indoors.

Several magical systems that once required geography have agreed to rent a room together.

The estate agent smiles.

“Where would you like to go?”

You ask where the nearest town is.

She looks confused.

“You're already home.”

» The perfect RuneScape house contains so much of the world that leaving begins to look inefficient.

This is brilliant design.

That is the problem.

---

**The utility mansion.**

Construction begins as an expensive relationship with chairs.

The player builds one, removes it, builds another, and gradually discovers that carpentry in Gielinor resembles feeding furniture into a furnace until architecture respects you.

Then the house becomes infrastructure.

[VISUAL: early sparse house evolves room by room into a mature utility layout; camera position remains fixed]

A portal room compresses travel.

A restoration feature compresses recovery.

Storage compresses banks and costume systems.

Altars and magical fixtures compress services previously attached to NPCs, locations, or journeys.

Workshops, lecterns, display rooms, menageries, gardens, and specialized spaces turn the private instance into a personalized operating system.

The mature house is less a home than a city whose population consists entirely of one account and several servants with astonishing labor protections.

Every improvement feels excellent.

Routes shorten.

Preparation becomes elegant.

The player returns from danger, restores, changes destination, and leaves through another portal in seconds. The house becomes punctuation between activities: a private comma inserted between boss trips.

That is what utility progression should feel like. Years of work condense friction into mastery.

The estate agent calls it “open-plan living.”

The plan includes most of known transportation.

---

**Now step outside.**

There was once a reason to visit a particular altar.

A reason to use a public fountain, bank route, teleport NPC, transport hub, or restoration point.

A reason to see the people gathered there because they shared the same need.

Move the service into every advanced player's house and the public route loses traffic.

[VISUAL: each house feature is paired with a public location; as the private version appears, the public footage becomes emptier]

This does not always kill the location. Quests, clues, shops, aesthetics, and other activities may keep it alive. The effect is cumulative.

One convenience removes one trip.

Ten conveniences remove a routine.

A hundred thousand private routines removed from the map become silence nobody can attribute to a single update.

The house extracts function from geography.

Towns remain beautiful containers for quests and memories while the practical life of the advanced account passes through an instance beyond the world map.

An MMO can preserve every building and still lose the traffic that made the buildings feel occupied.

---

**Housing intensifies an old contradiction.**

Players want a place that belongs to them.

MMOs want places players share.

Personal housing offers control, expression, storage, status, creativity, and relief from the visual democracy of public spaces, where another person's outfit may constitute an attack on color theory.

Instancing protects performance and ownership.

It also places the most expressive spaces where strangers cannot encounter them accidentally.

[VISUAL: spectacular private rooms behind closed portals; outside, ordinary players pass without knowing they exist]

A city street displays its residents' choices.

An instanced house displays them only after invitation.

The same applies to utility. Public services create collision because multiple lives route through one point. Private services create sovereignty. The player waits for nobody, competes with nobody, and sees nobody unless social contact has already been arranged.

The house is safe from the MMO.

That may be its most desirable feature.

---

**The estate agent opens the entertainment room.**

“This is ideal for hosting,” she says.

Here the indictment weakens.

RuneScape houses have supported parties, gilded-altars gatherings, combat rooms, games, costume displays, pet collections, clan events, and the intimate ritual of visiting somebody else's architectural decisions.

A house can create social life unavailable in public space.

[VISUAL: house party footage; visitors arrive through the portal and spread into distinct rooms]

Ownership gives the host a role.

The layout becomes authored hospitality.

A player can build services for others before those visitors can afford their own. Public house worlds and advertised hosts turn private investment into shared infrastructure. The house may be instanced, yet the door can function like a public institution.

Construction also creates one of RuneScape's clearest long-term transformations. An empty plot becomes a legible record of wealth, priorities, taste, and technical understanding. The player does not merely equip progress. They walk through it.

And public inconvenience is an unreliable community engine. Forcing players to restore at a shared location may produce a crowd without producing connection. People can stand together while resenting every second that brought them there.

The house earns its utility.

The question concerns direction, not guilt.

---

**A healthy home should have windows.**

Mechanically, the best housing systems remain permeable to the world.

They send residents outward for materials, relationships, maintenance, discoveries, and services that retain local meaning. They let private investment create public expression. They make visiting easy enough that hospitality can occur before a calendar invitation and a risk assessment.

[VISUAL: house floor plan drawn with arrows flowing both inward and outward toward towns, resources, guests and public services]

RuneScape's house already does parts of this.

Materials come from the economy.

Guests can benefit.

Some functions remain outside.

The design tension appears whenever a new convenience is proposed: should this service become another room? The immediate player answer is usually yes. The ecological answer depends on which journeys, locations, and encounters disappear when the room opens.

Convenience has a destination.

Often it is solitude.

That does not make solitude wrong. It makes the destination worth naming.

---

**The final room is unfinished.**

The estate agent apologizes.

No portal.

No pool.

No display.

One chair faces an open door.

[VISUAL: plain room at the edge of the mansion; through the doorway, visitors and the exterior portal remain visible]

“What goes here?” you ask.

She checks the plan.

“Traffic.”

The best house should make a player more capable of participating in the world. It should collect stories, people, and objects from outside, then return them altered. A home earns meaning through the door.

» The MMO survives when private mastery still produces public reasons to leave.

You sign the papers.

The house is magnificent.

Before the agent departs, she asks whether the portal should be locked.

You look back through the rooms: the compressed transport network, the restoration machinery, the trophies, the expensive floor, the entire tiny city built around one account.

Leave it open.

[VISUAL: visitors begin arriving through the portal; camera exits into the public world while the house remains active behind]

The street outside is still quiet.

For the first time during the tour, somebody is walking toward the door.
