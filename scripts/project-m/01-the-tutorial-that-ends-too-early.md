# The Tutorial That Ends Too Early — Full Script (Project M, #1)

**Thesis:** Tutorial Island succeeds because it teaches RuneScape's verbs and refuses to assign the player a life. You leave knowing how to fish, cook, mine, fight, pray, bank, and cast a spell, then the game abandons you in Lumbridge without a main objective. The confusion is the first lesson in self-direction.
**Format:** Graduation ceremony. Each instructor signs the certificate; the final speaker discovers there is no course waiting on the mainland.
**Runtime:** ~11 minutes (~1,600 spoken words)
**Voice dial:** Warm, wry, and increasingly sincere. The broader MMO comparison stays grounded; the close carries the philosophical register.
**Facts:** Tutorial Island's instructor sequence and taught actions require a current in-game/wiki verification pass before recording. The argument avoids claims about completion or retention rates.

## SCRIPT

Your name is called.

You walk across a small stone room while nine exhausted adults applaud with the careful enthusiasm of people who have watched several million identical children graduate.

[VISUAL: Tutorial Island instructors arranged like a graduation stage; slow pan across their faces, ending on the exit door]

The survival instructor signs first. You have caught a shrimp, burned a shrimp, caught another shrimp, and cooked that one adequately. The chef confirms you can put dough in an oven without involving the emergency services. The mining tutor has seen you strike a rock. The combat instructor watched you defeat something living and has chosen not to dwell on how quickly you accepted the assignment.

A banker signs. A priest signs. A wizard signs after you cast one spell at one chicken.

The certificate is passed to the head instructor.

He reads it twice.

Fishing. Cooking. Woodcutting. Firemaking. Mining. Smithing. Melee. Ranged. Prayer. Magic. Banking. Quests, in the technical sense that somebody explained the icon.

Everything seems in order.

Then he reaches the final box.

**PURPOSE AFTER GRADUATION:**

Blank.

You are teleported to Lumbridge.

» RuneScape's tutorial teaches you nearly every basic verb in the game, then refuses to write a sentence for you.

That refusal looks primitive. It may be the most sophisticated thing on the island.

---

**First, the education.**

Tutorial Island has the subtlety of a workplace safety video.

A person stands beside a tree and tells you to cut the tree. Another stands beside a furnace and develops a sudden professional interest in whether you can use the furnace. Doors remain locked until the relevant adult is satisfied that you have clicked the relevant object. The island is a corridor decorated as a country.

Yet look at what the corridor teaches.

It teaches actions rather than ambitions.

You learn that a tree can become logs, logs can become fire, raw food can become cooked food, ore can become metal, metal can become equipment. You learn that an NPC may want something, that your inventory has limits, that death is at least theoretically possible, and that several tabs along the edge of the screen conceal different ways of interfering with reality.

[VISUAL: one continuous route through Tutorial Island; each station adds a simple verb on screen: CUT, LIGHT, COOK, MINE, FIGHT, BANK, PRAY, CAST]

The island never tells you to become a miner.

It never selects combat as your build.

It never announces that the kingdom requires a chosen hero to gather twelve wolf spleens before the music swells.

You receive verbs. The nouns wait on the mainland.

That distinction matters because a verb survives a change of plan. The person who learns to fish may feed themselves, complete a quest, chase profit, train toward a cape, or spend an evening beside a river talking to strangers about a divorce. The tutorial cannot know which life the verb will enter.

So it does the modest thing.

It teaches the hand.

Then it releases the person attached to it.

---

**Lumbridge.**

You appear beside a castle with a bronze dagger, a handful of mismatched supplies, and the expression of a small animal placed on a kitchen floor.

Nobody has outlined Act One.

There is no large arrow pointing toward the plot. The Duke has problems, certainly, but he is coping. A cook downstairs has misplaced several groceries. A man outside may be collecting bones. Across the river, goblins are conducting the same unresolved territorial disagreement they have maintained since childhood.

You can help.

You can also walk away.

[VISUAL: fresh account in Lumbridge; camera holds while possible destinations sit in every direction and no marker is selected]

This is the moment players remember. The tutorial had walls. Lumbridge has roads.

One leads north toward a city whose guards can kill you. One bends east through cows and chickens. One crosses the river toward a swamp, a mine, a cemetery, and eventually a gate you cannot pass without membership. Somewhere beyond all of it are dragons, gods, account builds, raids, market scams, and a man who will pay you to shear sheep.

The game presents these possibilities with equal ceremony.

Almost none.

A modern designer might call this abandonment. The player has acquired controls without motivation, capability without a plan. The screen contains several dozen clickable things and no evidence that any one deserves the next hour.

That discomfort is real.

It is also the first time the game asks a question the tutorial could never answer for you:

What do you feel like doing?

---

**Now place another MMO beside it.**

The contemporary onboarding chain is a minor miracle of logistics. It introduces movement, combat, equipment, crafting, social systems, travel, monetization, and narrative while keeping a new player pointed toward the next useful encounter. Designers know where confusion becomes churn. They instrument the first hour until every hesitation has a name.

A marker appears above the quest giver.

The quest giver sends you to the field.

The field contains the exact enemies you need.

Their corpses sparkle.

A tracker counts upward.

A trail returns you to the quest giver, who promotes you toward another marker at a suitable level.

This works. Millions of players reach the real game because the road was impossible to lose.

The road also answers a question the player never got to ask.

[VISUAL: clean objective chain from a modern MMO; arrows and highlighted targets slowly fill the frame until the landscape is barely visible]

The world becomes a sequence before it becomes a place. Forests are crossed because the objective is behind them. Enemies are killed because the tracker requires them. A town is the interval between two exclamation marks.

Eventually the player receives freedom, usually at a carefully selected point after the game has demonstrated its best systems.

By then freedom feels like the absence of instructions.

RuneScape makes the opposite wager. Freedom arrives first, badly dressed, standing in Lumbridge with ten coins and no pitch deck.

The player has to manufacture intention before the game manufactures momentum.

That is a severe entrance exam.

---

**The argument against this is obvious, and strong.**

A lost player cannot appreciate being trusted. They are simply lost.

Children in 2004 had school friends, library computers, fan sites, and afternoons long enough to spend forty minutes discovering that flour requires a pot. A new player today arrives in a market where another game can be installed before the Lumbridge music finishes its first loop.

Some players want a story. Some need a clear first goal before self-direction becomes pleasurable. Accessibility includes cognitive orientation: knowing what the game considers possible, useful, and appropriate at your current stage. A blank purpose box may feel like freedom to one person and clerical negligence to another.

The old tutorial also benefits from memory. Veterans praise the purity of being released into Lumbridge while carrying twenty years of invisible guidance. We know where Varrock is. We know which strangers are scams. We know that the ominous gate near Taverley is commerce rather than lore.

A genuine beginner knows none of this.

[VISUAL: two Lumbridge shots side by side — veteran view covered in remembered routes; beginner view left bare]

So the stronger position cannot be that tutorials should explain less.

The question is what their explanations should preserve.

Guidance can teach the next action while leaving the next *desire* available. A game can show the road to Varrock without declaring Varrock the reason you logged in. It can offer several beginnings with different textures. It can let a player abandon the suggested path without treating departure as failure.

The danger arrives when onboarding becomes a replacement will.

When every minute is justified by a marker, the player gets very good at obedience. Remove the marker fifty hours later and the world feels empty, even when it contains more content than Tutorial Island could fit in the sea around it.

The game taught motion.

It forgot to teach wanting.

---

**Commencement.**

Back on the island, the instructors are waiting.

The head instructor still has your certificate. He considers filling in the final box himself.

Become rich.

Complete every quest.

Reach the highest level.

Kill the hardest boss.

Collect everything.

Each answer would be defensible. Each would also shrink the mainland before you saw it.

[VISUAL: the blank certificate; possible goals appear faintly, then are erased one by one]

So he leaves the box empty.

This is the strange generosity at the center of Tutorial Island. It prepares you for interaction and withholds interpretation. It gives you enough grammar to begin making bad decisions in your own voice.

You may leave Lumbridge and mine copper for three days because the orange rock pleases you. You may follow a road until the enemies become frightening, then remember the shape of the danger years later. You may decide that money is the game, or quests are the game, or fashion is the game, or standing beside a bank while another person explains cryptocurrency badly is the game.

Most of those choices are inefficient.

That is how you can tell they belong to you.

» A tutorial should make the world legible. It should leave your life unwritten.

The ceremony ends. The instructors file away the paperwork. Another new account appears beside the first tree, ready to learn that an axe cuts wood.

You arrive in Lumbridge.

The music begins.

Nothing is waiting for you except everything, which is an appalling amount of responsibility to hand someone carrying a bronze dagger.

[VISUAL: the player stands at Lumbridge crossroads; after a long hold, chooses an unremarkable direction and walks out of frame]

Graduation complete.

Purpose pending.
