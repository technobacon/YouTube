# The Exurb1a Style Guide

A working reference for writing and producing video essays in the style of **exurb1a** (Alexander McKechnie) — British YouTuber, novelist, and podcaster known for fusing hard science, existential philosophy, dark comedy, and dick jokes into strangely moving 15–20 minute video essays.

This document is meant to be *used*, not just read. When you're scripting a video and want it to feel like exurb1a, come back here.

---

## 1. Who he is (and why it matters for the style)

- Real name **Alexander McKechnie**, English, based in Bulgaria. Physics-adjacent background who pivoted to philosophy and fiction.
- He is a **novelist first, YouTuber second**. His videos read like literary prose read aloud, not like scripted explainer content. This is the single most important thing to internalise: **you are writing an essay/short story, then narrating it — you are not "making a YouTube video."**
- He also writes fiction (*The Fifth Science*, *The Prince of Milk*, *Sixteen Small Deaths*, etc.). The video voice and the prose voice are the same voice. When in doubt, write like you're writing a chapter, not a script.
- His own framing of the difference between video and prose (from interview): **video means you "covet the viewer's attention at all times, slimming down every sentence,"** whereas books let you "really try and romance the reader, draw them into something more complicated, make it dark or nuanced." → For video, **cut every sentence to the bone**, but keep the darkness and nuance.

---

## 2. The core tonal formula

The one-line description that recurs everywhere: **"philosophy, theoretical physics, and dick jokes."** His own Patreon tagline: **"Philosophy, Awkwardness, and Dick jokes."**

The magic is the *collision* of three registers that shouldn't sit together:

1. **The Cosmic / Intellectual** — real physics, real philosophy, real history, delivered with genuine authority and wonder (Camus, entropy, the Fermi paradox, consciousness, heat death).
2. **The Tender / Sincere** — sudden, disarming emotional honesty about loneliness, death, love, and meaning. He earns the right to be sincere by never staying sincere for long.
3. **The Profane / Absurd** — an untimely swear, a stupid joke, a self-deprecating aside, a fart of a metaphor dropped into a sacred moment.

**The technique is tonal whiplash.** You build something beautiful and cosmic, then puncture it with a dumb joke — and then, crucially, you use the laugh as cover to land a gut-punch of sincerity. Beauty → joke → devastation. That's the rhythm.

> Feel it in his aphorisms:
> - "Hell isn't a fire pit but a museum of regrets."
> - "Happy people are just people you aren't acquainted enough with yet to know how miserable they really are."
> - "Society grows great when old men plant trees whose shade they know they'll never sit in."
> - "Humans are the only animals that build zoos for themselves."
> - "No one is guaranteed happiness. It's not a human right. It's a house you have to build yourself."
> - "There's no grand plan and no one's in charge. It's all just fumbling about in the dark."

Notice: each one is short, has a **turn** in the middle (a "but" / a reversal), and lands somewhere sadder or truer than it started.

## 3. Voice & prose mechanics (the actual how)

### Point of view
- Heavy use of the **second person "you."** He implicates the viewer directly: *you* were born without being asked, *you* are going to die, *you* don't really exist. It's intimate and slightly accusatory.
- Frequent **first-person "we"** for the cosmic-species scale — "we humans," "our little planet." Zooms between *you* (personal) and *we* (species) constantly.
- Sometimes an **omniscient narrator-of-the-universe** voice, wry and god-like, treating cosmic events as a slightly incompetent story ("the Sun's impending rage quit," the universe "fumbling about in the dark").

### Sentence craft
- **Short declaratives** for impact. Then one long, winding, comma-spliced sentence that builds and builds. Vary the length deliberately — the rhythm is the music.
- **Understatement and deadpan.** Describe something enormous or horrifying in flat, casual, slightly bureaucratic language. ("The Sun will eventually rage quit." A black hole is just... there, doing its thing.)
- **Anthropomorphise the cosmos.** Stars, atoms, galaxies, entropy — all get personalities, moods, petty motives. This is a signature move.
- **The untimely swear.** Profanity is a *tool*, not decoration. It arrives exactly when the register is at its most elevated, to deflate it — or at the emotional climax, because sometimes "and it's all so fucking strange" is the only honest thing to say.
- **Non-sequiturs and juxtaposition** as the engine of the humour. Put two things next to each other that don't belong.
- **Self-deprecation and awkwardness.** The narrator is not a guru. He's a confused, slightly stoned bloke who happens to have read a lot. He admits he doesn't know. That humility is what makes the profundity land instead of feeling preachy.

### Register
- Casual, conversational, "stoner-philosopher-who-actually-did-the-reading." Feels facetious and offhand even while it's rigorous.
- **British.** Dry, ironic, self-mocking. Never earnest-American-TED-talk. Never hype.
- He'll cite Camus and then make a joke about doing the dishes. High and low, always both.

## 4. Structure of a typical video

Average length ~**15–20 minutes** (roughly 17). Loose, but a recurring shape:

1. **The cold, strange open.** Drop the viewer into something — a mundane scene that curdles, a cosmic fact, a short-story fragment, or a blunt existential statement. He begins *mid-thought* or *mid-story*, not with "Hey guys." (e.g. "In the beginning there was Nothing, but Nothing is unstable, so Something came about.")
2. **The hook question.** An unanswerable big one: Why is there something rather than nothing? Are we alone? Do you actually exist? Does any of this mean anything?
3. **The genuine content.** He *actually teaches you something* — real physics, real philosophy, real history, explained clearly and correctly. This is not fake-deep; the substance is real (Camus's three responses to the absurd; the Fermi paradox; the second law of thermodynamics; theory of consciousness). **Earn the profundity with real information.**
4. **The story / thought experiment.** Frequently he'll drop into an extended narrative — a sci-fi vignette, a historical anecdote (e.g. Shackleton's crew staging plays in Antarctica), a parable — to *dramatise* the idea rather than argue it. Show, don't lecture.
5. **The turn.** The intellectual material gets personal. The cosmic becomes about *you*, your death, your loneliness, your one shot at this.
6. **The landing.** A quiet, sincere, often beautiful close that offers no cheap comfort but some hard-won reason to keep going anyway — usually undercut *just* enough by humour to avoid sentimentality. ("Do the dishes anyway." Maintain "uncomfortable eye contact" with the absurd.) He resolves *emotionally*, rarely *intellectually* — he leaves the big question open.

He does **not** do: clean thesis statements, bullet-pointed takeaways, "smash that subscribe," or tidy conclusions that resolve the mystery. **The mystery stays open. That's the point.**

## 5. Recurring themes & subject matter

His obsessions — reach for these wells:

- **Existentialism & the absurd** — nihilism (both "liberating and terrifying"), Camus, absurdism, finding *subjective* meaning in an indifferent universe.
- **Death & mortality** — the certainty of it, and the worse horror that we'll "never truly discover what it was all for."
- **Consciousness & the self** — free will, whether "you" exist, whether you control your own thoughts, the illusion of the ego.
- **Cosmology & physics** — the Big Bang, entropy/heat death, black holes, the scale of the universe, the Planck length, time.
- **The Fermi paradox & aliens** — the Great Filter, why the sky is silent, humanity's future and self-destruction.
- **AI & the future of humanity** — often genocidal or godlike AI, transhumanism, what comes after us.
- **Time travel & civilisation** — frequently paired; the long arc of human history and its smallness.
- **Loneliness & human connection** — the counterweight to all the cosmic bleakness. His actual thesis, underneath everything: *the universe is meaningless and cold, so the warmth we make between each other is the only thing that matters.* "Nothing is more gorgeous than meeting another creature who shares your deepest values." Most people aren't malicious, "only drastically misguided and desperate in their loneliness."

**The emotional throughline of the whole channel:** cosmic pessimism as the setup, human tenderness as the payoff. Bleak facts, warm heart. If your script is only bleak, or only warm, it isn't exurb1a.

## 6. Production & aesthetic

- **Narration-driven.** His voice over visuals is the whole thing. The writing carries it; visuals serve the words.
- **Visuals:** a loose, lo-fi, collage-y mix — stock footage, space/nature imagery, simple animation, sketches, occasional webcam, text on screen, found footage. Deliberately *not* the slick, uniform, high-budget vector animation of Kurzgesagt. The roughness is part of the intimacy; it feels handmade and personal, like a friend showing you something.
- **Music** is a major emotional lever — melancholic piano, ambient, swelling strings under the sincere moments. He even builds whole videos *as* songs ("A Song About Rockets": a cheery piano tune about the Blitz, JFK's assassination, and nuclear war — the tonal-clash principle applied to form itself).
- **Pacing:** unhurried. He lets silences and slow passages breathe. Not everything is maximally "retention-optimised" — the meditative pace *is* the experience.
- **The look and sound should feel intimate and slightly melancholy**, like late-night thoughts, not like a lecture or a corporate explainer.

## 7. Influences to absorb (write like these had a baby)

- **Kurt Vonnegut** — dark comedy deployed in service of deep seriousness; cosmic-scale absurdity; plain, punchy prose; humane pessimism.
- **Douglas Adams** — the universe as absurd cosmic joke; deadpan British wit; making physics funny.
- **Albert Camus** — the philosophical spine: the absurd, and living meaningfully *in spite of* meaninglessness.
- **Carl Sagan** — the sincere cosmic awe and the "pale blue dot" sense of scale (which exurb1a then undercuts with a joke).
- General **absurdist fiction** (Kafka, Beckett) and **existentialist philosophy** (Kierkegaard, Sartre, Nietzsche).

## 8. Quick checklist — "Is this exurb1a?"

Run a script against these:

- [ ] Does it open mid-thought / strangely, with **no "hey guys" intro**?
- [ ] Is there **real, correct intellectual content** (a genuine idea from science/philosophy/history), not just vibes?
- [ ] Does it swing between **cosmic bleakness and human tenderness** — and end on the tenderness?
- [ ] Is there **tonal whiplash**: beauty → dumb joke → gut-punch?
- [ ] At least one **untimely swear** placed for maximum register-collision?
- [ ] Does it address **"you"** directly and make it personal?
- [ ] Is there a **story, parable, or thought experiment** dramatising the idea instead of arguing it?
- [ ] Are the sentences **cut to the bone**, with rhythm (short–short–long)?
- [ ] Does it **refuse to resolve the mystery** — no tidy answer, just a reason to keep going?
- [ ] Is the narrator **humble and awkward**, not a guru?
- [ ] Would it work as **prose read aloud**, i.e. does it sound like literature, not a script?

## 9. Traps to avoid (common ways imitations fail)

- **Fake-deep with no substance.** He always does the reading. Pseudo-profundity without real ideas is the opposite of the style. Learn the actual physics/philosophy before you riff on it.
- **Being only bleak (edgelord nihilism).** The warmth is the whole point. Bleakness without tenderness is just doom.
- **Being only warm (greeting-card sincerity).** Sincerity without the puncturing joke becomes saccharine. He *never* lets a beautiful moment stand un-undercut for too long.
- **Over-explaining / resolving the mystery.** Don't hand the viewer a neat thesis. Leave the door open.
- **Guru voice / TED-talk confidence.** He's confused *with* you, not lecturing *at* you.
- **Slick corporate polish.** The lo-fi intimacy matters. Too clean = too cold.
- **Swearing as constant texture.** Profanity works because it's *rare and precisely timed*. If everything is profane, nothing lands.
- **Optimising every second for retention.** Some of the best moments are slow. Trust the meditative pace.

## 10. Notable videos to study directly

Watch these and note the moves in each (this list is representative, not exhaustive):

- **"The Fermi Paradox"** series / **"The Great Filter"** — cosmic dread + humanity's future.
- **"You (Probably) Don't Exist"** — consciousness, free will, the illusion of self, second-person implication.
- **"Absurdism | How to Party at the End of Meaning"** — the cleanest statement of his Camus thesis; ends on "do the dishes anyway."
- **"Big Oxygen"** — his short-story/sci-fi mode; deadpan everyman narrator, reality vs. comfortable fiction.
- **"A Song About Rockets"** — the tonal-clash principle applied as *form* (jaunty music, horrifying content).
- **"27"** — much-referenced, structurally distinctive.
- Anything on **entropy / heat death**, **the universe explained fast**, **AI**, or **time** — the core wells.

> Method: transcribe a favourite passage by hand and mark where he switches register (cosmic ↔ tender ↔ profane). The switching *is* the style.
